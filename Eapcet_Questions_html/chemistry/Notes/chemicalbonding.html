Chemical Bonding

Chemical Bond

A chemical bond is the force of attraction that holds atoms together in molecules and compounds.

Lewis Symbols with Examples

Lewis symbols represent valence electrons around an atom. For example:

  • Oxygen (O): :O: (six valence electrons)
  • Chlorine (Cl): :Cl: (seven valence electrons)
  • Magnesium (Mg): Mg (two valence electrons)

Octet Rule with Exceptions

The octet rule states that atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to attain eight valence electrons. However, there are exceptions:

  • Incomplete Octet: Some elements are stable with fewer than 8 electrons. Example: Boron in BF₃ has only 6 valence electrons.
  • Expanded Octet: Elements in period 3 or beyond can have more than 8 valence electrons. Example: SF₆ (Sulfur has 12 valence electrons).
  • Odd-Electron Species: Molecules with an unpaired electron, also called radicals. Example: NO (Nitric Oxide) has 11 valence electrons.
  • Ionic Compounds: Some ions do not follow the octet rule strictly. Example: \( \text{NH}_4^+ \) and \( \text{SO}_4^{2-} \).

Ionic Bond

An ionic bond is formed due to the transfer of electrons from one atom to another, resulting in the formation of positively and negatively charged ions held together by electrostatic forces.

Favorable Factors for Ionic Bond Formation

  • Low Ionization Energy: The metal should lose electrons easily. Example: Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
  • High Electron Affinity: The non-metal should readily accept electrons. Example: Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻
  • High Lattice Energy: The strong attraction between ions stabilizes the ionic compound.
  • Large Difference in Electronegativity: A greater difference favors ionic bond formation. Example: Na (0.9) & Cl (3.0).

Ions and General Characteristics of Ionic Bonds

Ions

  • Cations: Positively charged ions formed by losing electrons. Example: Na⁺, Mg²⁺.
  • Anions: Negatively charged ions formed by gaining electrons. Example: Cl⁻, O²⁻.

Characteristics of Ionic Bonds

  • High Melting & Boiling Points: Due to strong electrostatic forces.
  • Solubility in Water: Most ionic compounds dissolve in polar solvents.
  • Electrical Conductivity: Conduct electricity in molten state or aqueous solution.
  • Crystalline Structure: Arranged in a rigid lattice formation.

Method of Writing Formulas of Ionic Compounds

  • Step 1: Identify the cation and anion along with their charges. Example: Na⁺ (Sodium) and Cl⁻ (Chloride).
  • Step 2: Criss-cross the charges to balance them. Example: Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻ → MgCl₂.
  • Step 3: Write the formula without charges. Example: \( \text{Al}^{3+} \) and \( \text{O}^{2-} \) → \( \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 \).

Covalent Bond

A covalent bond is formed by the sharing of electron pairs between two atoms to attain a stable electronic configuration.

Example: H₂, O₂, CH₄, H₂O

Types of Covalent Bonds

  • Single Covalent Bond: One pair of electrons is shared between two atoms. Example: H₂ (H—H)
  • Double Covalent Bond: Two pairs of electrons are shared. Example: O₂ (O=O)
  • Triple Covalent Bond: Three pairs of electrons are shared. Example: N₂ (N≡N)
  • Coordinate Covalent Bond (Dative Bond): Both electrons are donated by one atom. Example: NH₄⁺

Properties of Covalent Compounds

  • Low Melting and Boiling Points: Due to weak intermolecular forces.
  • Poor Electrical Conductivity: No free ions or electrons.
  • Solubility: Insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents.
  • Directional Nature: Covalent bonds have fixed orientations.

Formal Charge of an Atom in a Molecule

Formal charge is the hypothetical charge on an atom in a molecule, assuming equal sharing of electrons.

Formula:

\( \text{Formal Charge} = \text{Valence Electrons} - \text{Non-bonding Electrons} - \frac{\text{Bonding Electrons}}{2} \)

Example: Formal charge on oxygen in O₃:

  • For central O: \( 6 - 2 - \frac{6}{2} = +1 \)
  • For terminal O: \( 6 - 4 - \frac{4}{2} = 0 \)
  • For another terminal O: \( 6 - 6 - \frac{2}{2} = -1 \)

Bond Characteristics

Bond Length

The equilibrium distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms.

Trend: Single Bond > Double Bond > Triple Bond

Bond Angle

The angle between two adjacent bonds in a molecule.

Example: H₂O (104.5°), CH₄ (109.5°), CO₂ (180°)

Bond Order

Bond order represents the number of chemical bonds between two atoms.

Formula: \( \text{Bond Order} = \frac{1}{2} (\text{Bonding Electrons} - \text{Anti-bonding Electrons}) \)

Example: O₂ has a bond order of 2.

Bond Enthalpy

The amount of energy required to break a bond in one mole of a gaseous substance.

Trend: Triple Bond > Double Bond > Single Bond

Example: Bond enthalpy of H-H is 436 kJ/mol.

Fajan’s Rule

Fajan’s rule determines the covalent character in ionic compounds based on the charge and size of ions.

Factors Affecting Covalent Character:

  • Smaller Cation: High polarizing power increases covalent character.
  • Larger Anion: High polarizability enhances covalent character.
  • Higher Charge: Increases attraction, making the bond more covalent.

Example: AlCl₃ is more covalent than NaCl due to Al³⁺ having a higher charge and polarizing power.

Resonance

Resonance occurs when more than one valid Lewis structure can be drawn for a molecule.

Example: Ozone (O₃)

O=O—O ↔ O—O=O

Properties of Resonance:

  • The actual structure is a hybrid of all resonance forms.
  • Resonance stabilizes the molecule.
  • Bonds in the molecule have fractional character.

Other Examples: SO₂, NO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻, Benzene (C₆H₆)

Dipole Moment

Dipole moment (μ) is the measure of charge separation in a molecule.

Formula: \( \mu = q \times d \)

Where:

  • q = Magnitude of charge
  • d = Distance between charges

Examples:

  • H₂O: 1.85 D (Has a net dipole due to bent shape).
  • CO₂: 0 D (Linear shape, dipoles cancel).
  • NH₃: 1.47 D (Trigonal pyramidal, dipole exists).
  • BF₃: 0 D (Symmetric trigonal planar, dipoles cancel).

Applications of Dipole Moment:

  • Determination of Molecular Polarity: Higher μ → More polar.
  • Distinguishing Cis and Trans Isomers: Cis isomers have higher dipole moment than trans.
  • Determination of Shape: Nonzero μ suggests asymmetry.
  • Calculation of % Ionic Character: Used to determine bond nature.

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) Theory

Key Principles:

  • The shape of a molecule depends on the number of bond pairs and lone pairs around the central atom.
  • Electron pairs repel each other and arrange themselves to minimize repulsion.
  • Lone pairs exert more repulsion than bonding pairs.
  • The molecular shape is determined by both bond pairs and lone pairs.

General Order of Repulsions:

  • Lone Pair - Lone Pair (LP - LP) > Lone Pair - Bond Pair (LP - BP) > Bond Pair - Bond Pair (BP - BP)

Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

Key Postulates
  • Atoms form bonds by overlapping atomic orbitals.
  • The greater the overlap, the stronger the bond.
  • Bonding electrons are localized between atoms.
  • Only half-filled orbitals participate in bonding.
Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds
Property Sigma (σ) Bond Pi (π) Bond
Formation End-to-end (axial) overlap Sidewise (lateral) overlap
Orbital Overlap s-s, s-p, p-p p-p, d-p
Strength Stronger (greater overlap) Weaker (less overlap)
Rotation Free rotation possible Restricted rotation
Example Single bonds in H₂, CH₄ Double bond in O₂, triple bond in N₂
Limitations of VBT
  • Does not explain delocalized bonding (e.g., benzene).
  • Fails to explain magnetic properties of O₂.
  • Cannot predict molecular shapes accurately.
  • Does not account for resonance and hybridization properly.

Dative (Coordinate) Bond

Definition

A dative bond is a covalent bond where both electrons come from the same atom (donor) and are accepted by another atom (acceptor).

Examples
  • Ammonium Ion (NH₄⁺): NH₃ donates a lone pair to H⁺.
  • Hydronium Ion (H₃O⁺): H₂O donates a lone pair to H⁺.
  • AlCl₃ & NH₃ (Adduct): NH₃ donates a lone pair to AlCl₃.
  • SO₂ & BF₃: SO₂ donates electrons to BF₃.

Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT)

Key Postulates
  • Atomic orbitals combine to form molecular orbitals.
  • Molecular orbitals are of two types: Bonding (σ, π) and Anti-bonding (σ*, π*).
  • Electrons fill molecular orbitals following Aufbau, Hund’s Rule, and Pauli’s Principle.
  • If unpaired electrons are present → Paramagnetic.
  • If all electrons are paired → Diamagnetic.
Formation of Molecular Orbitals

Molecular orbitals are formed by the linear combination of atomic orbitals (LCAO). The combination can be:

  • Constructive Interference: Forms bonding orbitals (lower energy).
  • Destructive Interference: Forms anti-bonding orbitals (higher energy).

Bond Order, Stability & Magnetism

Bond Order Formula

Bond Order (BO) = (Number of Bonding Electrons - Number of Anti-bonding Electrons) / 2

Relation with Properties
  • Higher Bond Order → More Stability.
  • Lower Bond Order → Less Stability, Possible Instability (BO = 0 → No Bond).
  • Bond Order ∝ Bond Strength (Higher BO = Stronger Bond).
  • Bond Order ∝ 1 / Bond Length (Higher BO = Shorter Bond).
  • Unpaired electrons → Paramagnetic.
  • All paired electrons → Diamagnetic.

Hydrogen Bonding

Characteristics of Hydrogen Bonding
  • Occurs when H is bonded to highly electronegative atoms (F, O, N).
  • Strong dipole-dipole attraction between H and lone pairs on electronegative atoms.
  • Weaker than covalent/ionic bonds but stronger than van der Waals forces.
  • Affects boiling point, solubility, and physical properties of compounds.
Types of Hydrogen Bonding
  • Intermolecular H-Bonding: Between different molecules.
  • Intramolecular H-Bonding: Within the same molecule.
Examples
  • Intermolecular: H₂O, NH₃, HF, Alcohols, Carboxylic Acids
  • Intramolecular: o-Nitrophenol, Salicylic Acid
Applications of Hydrogen Bonding
  • Stabilizes DNA double helix (A-T and G-C base pairing).
  • Influences water’s high boiling point & surface tension.
  • Essential in protein structure (α-helix, β-sheets).
  • Determines properties of drugs & biomolecules.

Metallic Bonding

Characteristics of Metallic Bonding
  • Formed by a "sea of electrons" around metal atoms.
  • Electrons are free to move → High electrical & thermal conductivity.
  • Responsible for malleability, ductility, and metallic luster.
  • Strength depends on number of delocalized electrons and nuclear charge.
Examples of Metallic Bonding
  • Na, Cu, Fe, Ag, Au, Al, Mg
Factors Affecting Metallic Bond Strength
  • More valence electrons → Stronger bond (e.g., Mg > Na).
  • Smaller atomic size → Stronger bond (e.g., Al > K).
  • Higher nuclear charge → Stronger bond (e.g., Fe > Na).

Bond Strength Order

Strength Order of Bonds (Strongest → Weakest)
  • 1️⃣ **Ionic Bond** > 2️⃣ **Covalent Bond** (Triple > Double > Single)
  • 3️⃣ **Metallic Bond** > 4️⃣ **Hydrogen Bond**
  • 5️⃣ **Dipole-Dipole Interaction** > 6️⃣ **London Dispersion Forces (van der Waals)**
Explanation of Bond Strength
  • Ionic Bond: Strongest due to strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions (e.g., NaCl, MgO).
  • Covalent Bond: Strong, but generally weaker than ionic bonds. Strength depends on bond type (Triple > Double > Single).
  • Metallic Bond: Strength depends on the number of delocalized electrons (e.g., Cu, Fe).
  • Hydrogen Bond: Weaker than covalent/ionic bonds, but significant in molecules like water and DNA.
  • Dipole-Dipole Interaction: Moderate strength in polar molecules (e.g., HCl, CH₃Cl).
  • London Dispersion Forces: Weakest, present in all molecules, strongest in large non-polar molecules.
