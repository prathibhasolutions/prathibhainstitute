Environmental Chemistry

Definition of Environmental Chemistry

Environmental Chemistry is the scientific study of the chemical and biochemical phenomena that occur in natural environments. It involves understanding the sources, reactions, transport, effects, and fate of chemical species in the air, water, and soil.

Classification of Environment

  • Atmosphere: The layer of gases surrounding the Earth, essential for weather and climate regulation.
  • Hydrosphere: Includes all water bodies such as oceans, rivers, lakes, and groundwater.
  • Lithosphere: The outermost layer of the Earth, consisting of rocks, minerals, and soil.
  • Biosphere: The global sum of all ecosystems, encompassing all living organisms and their interactions.

Layers of the Atmosphere

Troposphere

The lowest layer of the atmosphere, where weather occurs. It extends up to about 8-15 km from Earth's surface.

Stratosphere

Contains the ozone layer, which absorbs harmful UV radiation. It extends from about 15 km to 50 km above Earth.

Mesosphere

The coldest atmospheric layer, where meteors burn up. It extends from 50 km to about 85 km above Earth.

Thermosphere

The layer with extremely high temperatures due to solar radiation absorption. It extends from 85 km to 600 km.

Exosphere

The outermost layer, gradually fading into space. It starts at about 600 km and extends beyond 10,000 km.

Environmental Pollution

Environmental pollution refers to the contamination of the natural environment due to harmful substances and human activities, leading to adverse effects on ecosystems and human health.

Types of Pollution

Natural Pollution

Pollution caused by natural events, occurring without human intervention.

Examples:
  • Volcanic eruptions (release of ash and gases)
  • Forest fires (smoke and CO2 emissions)
  • Dust storms (airborne particles)
  • Algal blooms (water pollution due to natural nutrient increase)
Man-Made Pollution

Pollution resulting from human activities, often causing long-term environmental damage.

Examples:
  • Industrial emissions (factories releasing harmful gases)
  • Plastic waste (ocean and land pollution)
  • Vehicle emissions (air pollution from CO and NOx gases)
  • Oil spills (water pollution affecting marine life)
  • Deforestation (land degradation and habitat loss)

Pollutants and Their Classification

Pollutants are substances that cause harm to the environment and living organisms. They can be classified based on various criteria.

1. Classification Based on Degradation

Biodegradable Pollutants

These pollutants can be broken down naturally by microorganisms over time.

Examples:
  • Food waste
  • Paper and wood
  • Sewage
Non-Biodegradable Pollutants

These pollutants do not decompose easily and persist in the environment for a long time.

Examples:
  • DDT (Dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane)
  • Pesticides
  • Plastics

2. Classification Based on Occurrence in Nature

Primary Pollutants

These pollutants are emitted directly from a source and remain unchanged in the environment.

Examples:
  • DDT
  • Pesticides
  • Fertilizers
Secondary Pollutants

These pollutants form when primary pollutants react in the atmosphere.

Examples:
  • Nitric acid (HNO₃) formed from NO₂
  • Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) formed from SO₂
  • Ozone (O₃) formed from NOx and VOCs

3. Classification Based on Existence in Nature

Quantitative Pollutants

These pollutants are naturally present in the environment but become harmful when their concentration increases.

Examples:
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
  • Nitrogen oxides (NOx)
  • Sulfur dioxide (SO₂)
Qualitative Pollutants

These pollutants are not naturally found in the environment and are introduced by human activities.

Examples:
  • DDT (pesticides)
  • Plastics
  • Heavy metals (Lead, Mercury)

Tropospheric Pollution

Tropospheric pollution refers to the contamination of the lowest layer of the atmosphere (0-15 km above Earth's surface) due to human activities and natural sources.

Types of Tropospheric Pollution

Gaseous Air Pollutants

These pollutants exist in the form of harmful gases released into the atmosphere.

Examples:
  • Oxides of Sulfur (SO₂, SO₃)
  • Oxides of Nitrogen (NO, NO₂)
  • Oxides of Carbon (CO, CO₂)
  • Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S)
  • Ozone (O₃)
  • Hydrocarbons (CH₄, VOCs)
Particulate Pollutants

These are solid or liquid particles suspended in the air, which can be harmful when inhaled.

Examples:
  • Dust
  • Fumes
  • Mist
  • Smoke
  • Smog

Air Pollution and Air Pollutants

Air pollution occurs when harmful substances contaminate the atmosphere, affecting human health, ecosystems, and climate. These pollutants come from natural and human-made sources.

Major Air Pollutants

Oxides of Sulfur (SO₂, SO₃)

These gases are released from burning fossil fuels and industrial processes. They contribute to acid rain and respiratory diseases.

Examples: Sulfur dioxide (SO₂), Sulfur trioxide (SO₃)
Oxides of Nitrogen (NO, NO₂)

Produced from vehicle emissions and industrial combustion, these gases cause photochemical smog and acid rain.

Examples: Nitric oxide (NO), Nitrogen dioxide (NO₂)
Smoke and Dust

Particulate matter (PM) consisting of tiny particles affects visibility and can cause lung diseases.

Examples: Industrial smoke, Road dust, Construction dust
Ammonia (NH₃)

Released from agricultural activities and fertilizers, ammonia contributes to water pollution and respiratory issues.

Examples: Agricultural fertilizers, Livestock waste
Mercaptans

Organic sulfur compounds with a strong odor, found in natural gas leaks and industrial emissions.

Examples: Methyl mercaptan (CH₄S), Ethyl mercaptan (C₂H₆S)
Heavy Metals (Zn, Cd)

These toxic metals are released from industrial waste and battery disposal, leading to soil and water contamination.

Examples: Zinc (Zn), Cadmium (Cd)
Freons (CFCs)

Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are used in refrigerants and aerosols, depleting the ozone layer.

Examples: CFC-11, CFC-12
Hydrocarbons

These organic compounds contribute to smog formation and are emitted from vehicle exhausts and industrial processes.

Examples: Methane (CH₄), Benzene (C₆H₆)
Oxides of Carbon (CO, CO₂)

Carbon monoxide is a toxic gas that reduces oxygen supply in the blood, while carbon dioxide is a major greenhouse gas.

Examples: Carbon monoxide (CO), Carbon dioxide (CO₂)

Greenhouse Effect and Global Warming

The greenhouse effect is the trapping of heat by certain gases in the Earth's atmosphere, keeping the planet warm. Excessive greenhouse gases due to human activities have led to global warming.

Major Greenhouse Gases

Greenhouse gases absorb infrared radiation, preventing heat from escaping into space.

Examples: Carbon dioxide (CO₂), Methane (CH₄), Nitrous oxide (N₂O), Water vapor (H₂O), Ozone (O₃), Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs).

Top 3 Consequences of Global Warming

Rising Temperatures

Global temperatures are increasing, leading to more heatwaves, droughts, and extreme weather events.

Melting Glaciers & Rising Sea Levels

Glaciers are melting, causing sea levels to rise, which threatens coastal areas and increases the risk of flooding.

Extreme Weather Events

Hurricanes, storms, wildfires, and unpredictable climate patterns are becoming more frequent and intense.

Acid Rain

Acid rain is caused by the reaction of sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) with water vapor, forming sulfuric (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃) in the atmosphere.

pH Value of Acid Rain

Normal rainwater has a pH of about 5.6 due to dissolved carbon dioxide. Acid rain has a pH below 5.6, sometimes reaching as low as 4 or even 3 in highly polluted areas.

Major Causes

  • Industrial emissions from burning fossil fuels
  • Vehicle exhaust releasing NOₓ gases
  • Natural sources like volcanic eruptions

Major Consequences

  • Damages soil and affects plant growth
  • Harms aquatic life by lowering water pH
  • Corrodes buildings and monuments

Smog and Its Types

Smog is a type of air pollution caused by the accumulation of smoke, fog, and pollutants in the atmosphere. It reduces visibility and affects human health.

Classical Smog (London Smog)

Occurs in cool, humid conditions and is caused by the presence of smoke, fog, and sulfur dioxide (SO₂).

Characteristics:
  • Mainly contains SO₂ and carbon particles
  • Occurs in winter and is reducing in nature
  • Harmful to the respiratory system

Photochemical Smog (Los Angeles Smog)

Occurs in dry, sunny conditions and is caused by the reaction of pollutants like nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) and hydrocarbons in the presence of sunlight.

Characteristics:
  • Mainly contains ozone (O₃), NO₂, and PAN (Peroxyacyl nitrates)
  • Occurs in summer and is oxidizing in nature
  • Causes eye irritation and respiratory issues

Stratospheric Pollution

Stratospheric pollution mainly involves the depletion of the ozone layer due to harmful pollutants, leading to increased ultraviolet (UV) radiation reaching Earth's surface.

Ozone Layer Depletion

The ozone layer in the stratosphere absorbs harmful UV radiation. Its depletion is mainly caused by human-made chemicals like chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs).

The Ozone Hole

The ozone hole is a region of significant ozone thinning, primarily over Antarctica, observed annually during spring (September to November). It is caused by CFCs and other ozone-depleting substances.

Key Facts:
  • First observed in the 1980s over Antarctica
  • Leads to increased UV radiation exposure in affected areas

Major Causes

  • Release of CFCs from refrigerants, air conditioners, and aerosol sprays
  • Halons from fire extinguishers
  • Nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) from supersonic jets

Major Consequences

  • Increased UV radiation leading to skin cancer and cataracts
  • Reduced plant growth and crop damage
  • Harmful effects on marine ecosystems

Water Pollution

Water pollution occurs due to the introduction of harmful substances into water bodies, affecting aquatic life and human health.

Aerobic Oxidation

Occurs in the presence of oxygen, breaking down organic matter into CO₂, water, and other simple molecules.

Example: Oxidation of organic pollutants in wastewater treatment plants.
Anaerobic Oxidation

Occurs in oxygen-deficient conditions, leading to the formation of methane, ammonia, and hydrogen sulfide.

Example: Decomposition of organic matter in deep water bodies and sewage systems.
Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD)

BOD measures the amount of oxygen required by microorganisms to decompose organic matter in water.

Higher BOD: Indicates more organic pollution and lower water quality.

  • Clean Water: BOD < 5 mg/L
  • Polluted Water: BOD > 17 mg/L
  • Sewage Water: BOD = 100 mg/L - 250 mg/L
Chemical Oxygen Demand (COD)

COD measures the total amount of oxygen required to oxidize both organic and inorganic substances in water.

Higher COD: Suggests industrial or chemical pollution in water sources.

  • Clean Water: COD < 10 mg/L
  • Polluted Water: COD > 250 mg/L

Sources of Water Pollution

  • Industrial waste containing heavy metals and toxic chemicals
  • Agricultural runoff with pesticides and fertilizers
  • Domestic sewage and untreated wastewater
  • Oil spills from marine transport

Effects of Water Pollution

  • Fluorides: Causes fluorosis, affecting bones and teeth.
  • Sulfates: Leads to laxative effects and gastrointestinal issues.
  • Nitrates: Causes blue baby syndrome in infants.
  • Heavy Metals (Cd, Pb, Hg): Bioaccumulate in the body, leading to neurological and kidney damage.

Control of Water Pollution

  • Industrial Waste Treatment: Proper disposal and treatment of industrial effluents.
  • Sewage Treatment Plants (STPs): Use of biological and chemical treatment methods before discharge.
  • Minimizing Agricultural Runoff: Using organic farming techniques and controlled use of fertilizers/pesticides.
  • Oil Spill Control: Using bioremediation and dispersants to clean up oil spills in water bodies.
  • Public Awareness: Promoting responsible water usage and pollution control measures.

Soil/Land Pollution

Soil pollution occurs due to the accumulation of harmful chemicals, waste materials, and pollutants, reducing soil fertility and affecting ecosystems.

Sources of Soil Pollution

  • Industrial Waste: Heavy metals, chemicals, and toxic residues contaminate soil.
  • Agricultural Runoff: Excessive use of pesticides, herbicides, and fertilizers.
  • Deforestation: Loss of vegetation leads to soil erosion and degradation.
  • Urbanization & Construction: Dumping of non-biodegradable waste and improper landfills.
  • Mining Activities: Disrupts land, leading to contamination with minerals and chemicals.
  • Plastic & E-Waste Dumping: Non-degradable materials cause long-term pollution.

Control of Soil Pollution

  • Proper Waste Management: Recycling and disposal of hazardous waste safely.
  • Reducing Chemical Fertilizers & Pesticides: Promoting organic farming.
  • Afforestation & Reforestation: Prevents soil erosion and enhances fertility.
  • Land Reclamation: Treating polluted land with bio-remediation techniques.
  • Strict Regulations: Implementing laws to control industrial and municipal waste dumping.
  • Encouraging Composting: Converting organic waste into natural fertilizers.
