Surface Chemistry

Surface Chemistry

Definition: Surface chemistry deals with the study of chemical phenomena that occur at the interface of two phases, such as solid-liquid or solid-gas.

Examples:

  • Corrosion of metals
  • Heterogeneous catalysis
  • Colloidal systems
Adsorption and Causes

Adsorption: The accumulation of molecules of a substance on the surface of a solid or liquid, rather than in the bulk phase.

Adsorbent and Adsorbate:

  • Adsorbent: The solid or liquid substance on which adsorption occurs.
    Example: Activated charcoal, silica gel, metals.
  • Adsorbate: The substance that gets adsorbed onto the surface of the adsorbent.
    Example: Gases like O₂, N₂, or moisture in air.

Causes of Adsorption:

  • Surface Energy: Atoms on the surface have unbalanced forces, attracting molecules.
  • Physical Adsorption: Weak Van der Waals forces hold molecules on the surface.
  • Chemisorption: Strong chemical bonding between adsorbate and adsorbent.

Important Characteristics of Adsorption:

  • Adsorption is a surface phenomenon and occurs at the boundary of two phases.
  • Adsorption increases with increase in surface area of the adsorbent.
  • It is an exothermic process (heat is released during adsorption).
  • The extent of adsorption depends on temperature and pressure.
  • Adsorption is reversible in nature (can be reversed by heating or reducing pressure).

Examples:

  • Adsorption of gases on activated charcoal
  • Adsorption of moisture on silica gel
  • Adsorption of dye on animal charcoal
Desorption and Sorption

Desorption: The process in which an adsorbed substance is released from the surface of an adsorbent. It is the reverse of adsorption.

Example: Removal of adsorbed gas molecules from activated charcoal by heating.

Sorption: A general term that includes both adsorption and absorption, where a substance is either accumulated on the surface (adsorption) or inside the bulk phase (absorption).

Example: When a sponge soaks up water, absorption occurs, while if a gas accumulates on the surface of a solid, adsorption occurs.

Difference Between Adsorption and Absorption
Adsorption Absorption
Occurs only at the surface of a material. Occurs throughout the bulk of the material.
It is a surface phenomenon. It is a bulk phenomenon.
Rate is high initially and then decreases. Rate remains uniform.
Exothermic process (releases heat). Endothermic process (absorbs heat).
Examples: Adsorption of gases on activated charcoal. Examples: Water absorbed by a sponge.
Positive and Negative Adsorption

Positive Adsorption: When the concentration of the adsorbed substance is higher on the surface of the adsorbent than in the bulk phase.

Example: Adsorption of gases like oxygen or nitrogen on activated charcoal.

Negative Adsorption: When the concentration of the adsorbed substance is lower on the surface than in the bulk solution.

Example: In some colloidal solutions, water molecules move away from the surface, leading to lower concentration.

Difference Between Physisorption and Chemisorption
Physisorption Chemisorption
Occurs due to weak van der Waals forces. Occurs due to strong chemical bond formation.
Low enthalpy change (~20-40 kJ/mol). High enthalpy change (~80-240 kJ/mol).
Reversible process. Irreversible process.
More significant at low temperatures. More significant at high temperatures.
Forms multiple layers on the adsorbent surface. Forms a single layer on the adsorbent surface.
Example: Adsorption of gases like CO₂ on charcoal. Example: Adsorption of hydrogen on iron in Haber’s process.
Factors Affecting Adsorption
  • Nature of Adsorbate: Easily liquefiable gases (e.g., NH₃, CO₂) are adsorbed more than permanent gases (e.g., H₂, O₂).
  • Nature of Adsorbent: Surface area and porosity of adsorbents (e.g., activated charcoal, silica gel) influence adsorption.
  • Surface Area: Greater surface area increases adsorption. Finely divided or porous adsorbents show higher adsorption.
  • Temperature:
    • Physisorption: Decreases with an increase in temperature (exothermic process).
    • Chemisorption: Initially increases with temperature, then decreases at very high temperatures.
  • Pressure:
    • Higher pressure favors adsorption, following Freundlich's Adsorption Isotherm.
    • In chemisorption, pressure has lesser impact compared to physisorption.
  • Activation of Adsorbent: Heating or chemical treatment can increase adsorption capacity.
  • Concentration of Adsorbate: Higher concentration of adsorbate in solution leads to higher adsorption, following Langmuir & Freundlich isotherms.
  • Nature of Adsorption Forces:
    • Van der Waals forces:In physisorption.
    • Chemical bonds: In chemisorption.
Adsorption Isotherms

Definition: Adsorption isotherms describe the variation of the amount of gas adsorbed by a solid with pressure at constant temperature.

1. Freundlich Adsorption Isotherm

Empirical equation:

$$ \frac{x}{m} = k P^{\frac{1}{n}} $$

Where:

  • \( x/m \) = Amount of gas adsorbed per unit mass of adsorbent
  • \( P \) = Pressure of gas
  • \( k, n \) = Constants (depend on nature of adsorbent and adsorbate)

Logarithmic form:

$$ \log \frac{x}{m} = \log k + \frac{1}{n} \log P $$

Graph: A straight line when plotting \( \log \frac{x}{m} \) vs. \( \log P \).

Applications of Adsorption

Adsorption plays a crucial role in various industrial and scientific processes.

1. Gas Masks

Activated charcoal is used in gas masks to adsorb poisonous gases and purify the air.

2. Catalysis

Heterogeneous catalysis relies on adsorption. For example:

  • Finely divided nickel (Ni) is used in the hydrogenation of oils.
  • Vanadium pentoxide (V₂O₅) is used in the contact process for sulfuric acid.
3. Chromatographic Separation

Adsorption is the principle behind chromatography, where components of a mixture get adsorbed at different rates on a stationary phase.

4. Water Purification

Activated carbon is used in water filters to remove impurities and toxic substances through adsorption.

5. Softening of Hard Water

Ion-exchange resins adsorb calcium and magnesium ions, helping in water softening.

6. Medicine and Drug Delivery

Adsorption is used in drug formulations where medicines adhere to surfaces for controlled release.

Example: Activated charcoal is used in treating poison ingestion.

7. Dehumidification

Silica gel is used as a desiccant to adsorb moisture from the air.

8. Paints and Inks

Pigments in paints and inks are adsorbed onto surfaces for better adhesion.

9. Froth Flotation Process

Adsorption is used in the purification of ores, where unwanted impurities are removed by froth flotation.

10. Removal of Colour and Odour

Animal charcoal is used to remove colored impurities in sugar solutions and odorous substances in air purification.

Catalysis

Catalysis is the process of increasing the rate of a chemical reaction by adding a substance known as a catalyst, which remains unchanged at the end of the reaction.

Characteristics of Catalysts
  • Does Not Initiate a Reaction: A catalyst only speeds up an existing reaction but cannot start one.
  • Remains Chemically Unchanged: A catalyst does not undergo permanent chemical change.
  • Effective in Small Quantities: A small amount of catalyst is enough to accelerate a reaction.
  • Increases Reaction Rate: It lowers the activation energy, making the reaction faster.
  • Selective Action: A catalyst works only for specific reactions.
  • Can Be Poisoned: Impurities like lead (\(\ce{Pb}\)) and arsenic (\(\ce{As}\)) can deactivate catalysts.
  • Can Be Promoted or Inhibited: Some substances enhance catalyst activity (promoters), while others decrease it (inhibitors).
Types of Catalysis
  • Homogeneous Catalysis: The reactants and the catalyst are in the same phase (solid, liquid, or gas).
  • Heterogeneous Catalysis: The reactants and the catalyst are in different phases (e.g., solid catalyst and gaseous reactants).
  • Autocatalysis: One of the products of the reaction acts as a catalyst.
  • Enzyme Catalysis: Biological catalysts (enzymes) speed up biochemical reactions.

Important Features of Solid Catalysis
  • Surface Phenomenon: Catalysis occurs on the surface of solid catalysts.
  • Activity Depends on Surface Area: Finely divided solids show greater catalytic activity.
  • Presence of Active Sites: The number of active sites determines catalytic efficiency.
  • Influence of Promoters and Inhibitors: Some substances enhance catalytic activity, while others reduce it.
  • Specificity: Different catalysts catalyze different reactions.
Enzyme Catalysis

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up biochemical reactions.

  • Inversion of Sucrose: \(\ce{C12H22O11 + H2O -> 2C6H12O6}\) (Catalyst: Invertase)
  • Decomposition of Urea: \(\ce{(NH2)2CO + H2O -> CO2 + 2NH3}\) (Catalyst: Urease)
  • Conversion of Starch to Maltose: \(\ce{(C6H10O5)n + H2O -> Maltose}\) (Catalyst: Amylase)
  • Decomposition of Hydrogen Peroxide in Cells: \(\ce{2H2O2 -> 2H2O + O2}\) (Catalyst: Catalase)
Characteristics of Enzyme Catalysis
  • Highly Specific: Each enzyme catalyzes only one specific reaction.
  • Extremely Efficient: Enzymes increase reaction rates by millions of times.
  • Work Under Mild Conditions: Enzymes function best at body temperature and normal pressure.
  • Can Be Inhibited: Certain chemicals can block enzyme activity (enzyme inhibitors).
  • Biocompatibility: Enzymes work efficiently in living organisms without causing harm.
  • Regulated by pH and Temperature: Enzymes function optimally at specific pH and temperatures.
Colloids

A colloid is a heterogeneous mixture in which the dispersed phase is distributed uniformly throughout the dispersion medium. The particle size of colloidal particles ranges from 1 nm to 1000 nm.

Visibility Invisible, even under a microscope Visible under an ultramicroscope Visible to the naked eye
Stability Stable, does not settle Stable, does not settle easily Unstable, settles on standing

Classification of Colloids Based on Physical State

Colloids can be classified based on the physical state of the dispersed phase and the dispersion medium. The dispersed phase can be a solid, liquid, or gas, and the dispersion medium can also be a solid, liquid, or gas.

Classification of Colloids Based on Interaction Between Dispersed Phase & Dispersion Medium

Colloids can be classified based on the interaction between the dispersed phase and the dispersion medium. This classification divides colloids into two major types:

1. Lyophilic Colloids (Solvent-Loving)

Definition: Lyophilic colloids have a strong affinity for the dispersion medium. They are easily formed by mixing the dispersed phase with the dispersion medium.

Characteristics:

  • Stable and do not precipitate easily.
  • Can be reconstituted by adding the dispersion medium again (Reversible sols).
  • Highly hydrated due to strong interaction with the solvent.

Examples: Gum, Gelatin, Starch, Proteins.

2. Lyophobic Colloids (Solvent-Hating)

Definition: Lyophobic colloids have little to no affinity for the dispersion medium. They require special methods for preparation.

Characteristics:

  • Unstable and tend to precipitate easily.
  • Cannot be easily reconstituted once separated (Irreversible sols).
  • Require stabilizers to prevent coagulation.

Examples: Gold sol, Ferric hydroxide sol, Sulfur sol.

Classification of Colloids Based on the Type of Particles in the Dispersed Phase

Colloids can be classified based on the type of particles in the dispersed phase. The three major types are:

1. Multimolecular Colloids

Definition: These colloids consist of aggregates of atoms or small molecules (each less than 1 nm in size) that form larger colloidal particles.

Characteristics:

  • Held together by van der Waals forces.
  • Each particle contains a collection of smaller molecules.
  • Examples include gold sol, sulfur sol.
2. Macromolecular Colloids

Definition: These colloids contain individual macromolecules dispersed in the medium. The size of the macromolecules is within the colloidal range.

Characteristics:

  • Stable and behave like true solutions.
  • Form a continuous phase in the dispersion medium.
  • Examples include starch, proteins, cellulose, rubber.
3. Associated Colloids (Micelles)

Definition: These colloids behave like normal electrolytes at low concentrations but form aggregates or micelles at higher concentrations.

Characteristics:

  • Micelle formation occurs above a specific concentration called the Critical Micelle Concentration (CMC).
  • Examples include soaps and detergents.
  • Micelles have hydrophobic tails and hydrophilic heads, making them useful in cleaning actions.
Preparation of Colloids

Colloidal solutions can be prepared by two main methods: Condensation methods and Dispersion methods.

1. Condensation Methods

Definition: In this method, small molecules or ions are chemically combined to form larger colloidal particles.

Techniques:

  • Oxidation: Sulfur sol can be prepared by oxidizing hydrogen sulfide gas with nitric acid.
  • Reduction: Metal sols (like gold or silver) can be prepared by reducing their salts using reducing agents (e.g., formaldehyde).
  • Hydrolysis: Ferric hydroxide sol is prepared by boiling ferric chloride solution.
  • Double Decomposition: Arsenic sulfide sol is prepared by reacting hydrogen sulfide with arsenic trioxide.
2. Dispersion Methods

Definition: In this method, larger particles are broken down into colloidal size.

Techniques:

  • Mechanical Dispersion: Colloidal solutions of metals like platinum or silver are prepared by grinding the material in a colloidal mill.
  • Electro-Dispersion (Bredig’s Arc Method): In this method, an electric arc is struck between metal electrodes immersed in water to form metal sols.
  • Peptization: A precipitate is converted into a colloidal solution by adding a small amount of electrolyte (peptizing agent). Example: Ferric hydroxide sol prepared by adding FeCl₃ to Fe(OH)₃ precipitate.
  • Ultrasonic Dispersion: High-frequency ultrasonic waves break large particles into colloidal size.
Purification of Colloids

Colloidal solutions often contain impurities like electrolytes and unwanted particles. To obtain a pure colloidal solution, different purification methods are used.

1. Dialysis

Definition: The process of removing dissolved impurities (electrolytes) from a colloidal solution using a semi-permeable membrane.

Process: The colloidal solution is placed in a bag made of parchment or cellophane and immersed in pure water. The impurities diffuse out, leaving the purified colloid inside.

Example: Purification of ferric hydroxide sol.

2. Electrodialysis

Definition: A faster version of dialysis where an electric field is applied to speed up the removal of ions.

Process: Electrodes are placed near the dialysis membrane, attracting the charged electrolyte impurities and pulling them out of the colloidal solution.

Application: Used for the purification of colloidal solutions in biochemical and pharmaceutical industries.

3. Ultrafiltration

Definition: A process where a colloidal solution is forced through a special membrane with very fine pores, which allow impurities to pass but retain colloidal particles.

Process: Colloidal solutions are filtered using ultrafilters (made of materials like collodion or gelatin) under pressure.

Example: Used in water purification and protein separation.

4. Ultracentrifugation

Definition: A high-speed centrifuge is used to separate colloidal particles from impurities based on their size and density.

Process: When spun at high speeds, heavier colloidal particles settle at the bottom while lighter impurities remain in solution.

Application: Used for separating proteins, viruses, and macromolecules in biological research.

Properties of Colloidal Solutions

Colloidal solutions exhibit unique physical, optical, electrical, and mechanical properties due to their dispersed phase and medium interaction.

1. Colligative Properties

Definition: Colloidal solutions show lower colligative properties (e.g., lower osmotic pressure, lower freezing point depression) than true solutions due to larger particle size.

Example: Colloidal gold solution exhibits lower osmotic pressure compared to a true solution of gold chloride.

2. Tyndall Effect

Definition: The scattering of light by colloidal particles when a strong beam passes through the solution.

Reason: Colloidal particles are large enough to scatter light, making the path visible.

Example: Light scattering in fog, dust particles in sunlight.

3. Brownian Movement

Definition: Random, zigzag motion of colloidal particles due to continuous collisions with solvent molecules.

Effect: Prevents colloidal particles from settling due to gravity.

Example: Random motion of smoke particles under a microscope.

4. Charge on Colloidal Particles

Definition: Colloidal particles acquire a positive or negative charge, leading to stability.

Cause: Selective adsorption of ions from the dispersion medium.

Example: Fe(OH)₃ sol has a positive charge, while AgCl sol has a negative charge.

5. Electrophoresis

Definition: Movement of charged colloidal particles under an electric field towards the oppositely charged electrode.

Example: Positively charged Fe(OH)₃ sol moves towards the cathode when an electric field is applied.

6. Coagulation

Definition: The process of settling down colloidal particles by adding electrolytes.

Cause: Neutralization of charge leading to precipitation.

Example: Blood coagulation using alum.

Protective Colloids and Gold Number
1. Protective Colloids

Definition: Protective colloids are lyophilic (solvent-attracting) colloids that prevent the coagulation or precipitation of lyophobic colloids by forming a protective layer around their particles.

Mechanism: When a small amount of a protective colloid is added to a lyophobic sol, it adsorbs on the particle surface, preventing aggregation and stabilizing the sol.

Example: Gelatin, starch, gum arabic, and casein are commonly used as protective colloids.

2. Gold Number

Definition: The gold number is the minimum amount of a protective colloid (in mg) required to prevent the coagulation of 10 mL of a standard gold sol when 1 mL of a 10% NaCl solution is added.

Interpretation: A lower gold number indicates a more effective protective colloid.

Examples of Protective Colloids and Their Gold Numbers:

  • Gelatin → 0.005-0.01
  • Albumin → 0.1
  • Gum Arabic → 0.15
  • Starch → 25

Application: Used to determine the effectiveness of a protective colloid in stabilizing colloidal solutions.

Emulsions
1. Types of Emulsions

Definition: An emulsion is a colloidal system in which liquid droplets (dispersed phase) are dispersed in another immiscible liquid (dispersion medium).

Types:

  • Oil-in-Water (O/W) Emulsion: Oil is the dispersed phase, and water is the dispersion medium. Example: Milk, Vanishing cream.
  • Water-in-Oil (W/O) Emulsion: Water is the dispersed phase, and oil is the dispersion medium. Example: Butter, Cold cream.
2. Characteristics of Emulsions

✔️ Emulsions are usually opaque and can appear white or slightly translucent.

✔️ They exhibit the Tyndall effect (light scattering).

✔️ They can be broken into separate phases by heating, freezing, or adding chemicals.

✔️ Stability depends on the type and concentration of emulsifier present.

3. Emulsifiers

Definition: Emulsifiers are substances that stabilize an emulsion by reducing the interfacial tension between the two immiscible liquids.

Examples:

  • Soaps and detergents
  • Proteins (casein in milk)
  • Natural gums (gum arabic)
  • Lecithin (found in egg yolk)

Function: Emulsifiers form a protective film around dispersed droplets, preventing coalescence.

4. Demulsification

Definition: Demulsification is the process of breaking an emulsion into its separate components (oil and water phases).

Methods:

  • Heating – Increases kinetic energy, leading to coalescence.
  • Chemical addition – Adding demulsifiers like alcohol or strong acids.
  • Electric fields – Used in industries to break crude oil emulsions.
  • Centrifugation – High-speed rotation separates the phases.
Gels, Colloids Around Us, and Applications of Colloids
1. Gels

Definition: Gels are colloidal systems where the dispersed phase is a liquid, and the dispersion medium is a solid.

Types of Gels:

  • Elastic Gels: These gels can regain their shape after deformation. Example: Gelatin, Agar.
  • Non-Elastic Gels: These do not regain their shape after deformation. Example: Silica gel.

Properties:

  • Show rigidity and resistance to flow.
  • Exhibit the Tyndall effect (scattering of light).
  • Some gels undergo syneresis (loss of liquid upon standing).
  • Can be used as drug carriers in pharmaceuticals.
2. Colloids Around Us

Colloids are present in various natural and artificial forms:

  • Biological Colloids: Blood, Cell cytoplasm.
  • Food Products: Milk, Butter, Jellies, Ice cream.
  • Industrial Colloids: Paints, Inks, Soaps, Rubber.
  • Atmospheric Colloids: Clouds, Fog, Smoke.
3. Applications of Colloids

1. Medical Applications:

  • Colloidal medicines (silver sol as an antiseptic).
  • Blood clotting (hydrophilic colloids assist in clot formation).

2. Industrial Applications:

  • Used in making paints, inks, and rubber.
  • Soaps and detergents act as emulsifiers in cleaning.

3. Water Purification:

  • Alum coagulates impurities in water purification.

4. Agriculture:

  • Fertilizers in colloidal form improve absorption by plants.

5. Photography:

  • Colloidal silver bromide used in photographic films.
