Chemistry in Everyday Life

Medicines or Drugs

Medicines or drugs are chemicals that interact with biological systems to treat, prevent, or diagnose diseases. They can be classified based on their chemical properties, action, and usage.

Antacids

Antacids are substances that neutralize excess stomach acid, providing relief from acidity and heartburn. They work by increasing the pH of the stomach contents.

Examples of Antacids

  • Sodium Bicarbonate (NaHCO₃)
  • Magnesium Hydroxide (Mg(OH)₂)
  • Aluminium Hydroxide (Al(OH)₃)
  • Milk (Contains natural buffering agents)

Antihistamines

Histamine is a chemical involved in allergic reactions and inflammation. It causes symptoms like swelling, itching, and mucus production. Antihistamines are drugs that block histamine receptors to provide relief from allergies, colds, and stomach acid disorders.

Examples of Antihistamines

  • Cimetidine
  • Ranitidine (Zantac)
  • Omeprazole
  • Lansoprazole

Tranquilisers

Tranquilisers are drugs used to reduce anxiety, stress, and mental tension. They work by affecting neurotransmitters in the brain to produce a calming effect.

Examples of Tranquilisers

  • Noradrenaline
  • Iproniazid
  • Phenelzine
  • Barbituric acid and its derivatives:
    • Veronal
    • Amytal
    • Seconal
    • Luminal
  • Equinol
  • Chlordiazepoxide
  • Meprobamate

Analgesics

Analgesics are drugs that relieve pain without causing significant impairment of consciousness, mental confusion, or other major disturbances of the nervous system.

Types of Analgesics

Narcotic Analgesics

These are strong pain relievers that act on the central nervous system. They are often used for severe pain but can be addictive.

  • Morphine
  • Codeine
  • Heroin

Non-Narcotic Analgesics

These are mild pain relievers that work by reducing inflammation and are commonly used for headaches, fever, and minor injuries.

  • Aspirin
  • Paracetamol
  • Ibuprofen
  • Naproxen

Antipyretics

Antipyretics are drugs used to reduce fever by acting on the hypothalamus to lower body temperature. They help in managing fever caused by infections or other illnesses.

Examples of Antipyretics

  • Paracetamol
  • Aspirin
  • Phenacetin
  • Analgin
  • Novalgin

Antimicrobials

Antimicrobials are substances that inhibit the growth of or destroy harmful microorganisms. They are widely used to treat infections and prevent contamination.

Types of Antimicrobials

1. Antibiotics

Antibiotics are drugs used to treat bacterial infections by either killing bacteria or inhibiting their growth.

  • Penicillin
  • Streptomycin
  • Tetracycline
  • Erythromycin
  • Ofloxacin
  • Chloramphenicol

2. Antiseptics

Antiseptics are substances applied to the skin or tissues to prevent infection by killing or slowing down microorganisms.

  • Dettol (Chloroxylenol)
  • Tincture of Iodine
  • Hydrogen Peroxide
  • Boric Acid
  • Bithional
  • Iodoform

3. Disinfectants

Disinfectants are chemicals used on non-living surfaces to eliminate harmful microorganisms.

  • Phenol
  • Chlorine
  • Formaldehyde
  • Bleach (Sodium Hypochlorite)

Antifertility Drugs

Antifertility drugs are used to control birth rates and prevent pregnancy. These drugs work by inhibiting ovulation or altering hormonal balance to prevent conception.

Examples of Antifertility Drugs

  • Norethindrone
  • Levonorgestrel

Synthetic Estrogen and Progesterone Derivatives

These are hormonal drugs that mimic natural estrogen and progesterone to regulate ovulation and prevent pregnancy.

  • Ethynylestradiol (Synthetic Estrogen)
  • Diethylstilbestrol (DES) (Synthetic Estrogen)
  • Norethindrone (Synthetic Progesterone)
  • Medroxyprogesterone acetate (Synthetic Progesterone)

Chemicals in Food

Artificial Sweetening Agents

Artificial sweetening agents are synthetic compounds used as sugar substitutes. They provide sweetness without adding significant calories, making them useful for diabetics and weight management.

Examples of Artificial Sweeteners

  • Aspartame (≈300 times sweeter than sugar)
  • Saccharin (≈550 times sweeter than sugar)
  • Sucralose (≈600 times sweeter than sugar)
  • Cyclamate (≈30 times sweeter than sugar)
  • Alitame (≈2000 times sweeter than sugar)

Food Preservatives

Food preservatives are substances added to food to prevent spoilage, microbial growth, and oxidation, thereby extending shelf life.

Commonly Used Natural Preservatives

  • Salt (Used in pickles, meats, and fish to inhibit bacterial growth)
  • Sugar (Preserves jams, jellies, and fruit syrups by reducing water activity)
  • Vegetable Oil (Prevents oxidation and spoilage in dried foods and pickles)
  • Vinegar (Acetic acid in vinegar prevents microbial growth in pickles and sauces)
  • Citric Acid (Naturally found in citrus fruits, used in juices and canned foods as a preservative)
  • Spices (Like turmeric, cloves, and cinnamon, which have antimicrobial properties)

Synthetic Food Preservatives

  • Sodium Benzoate (Used in acidic foods like jams and soft drinks)
  • Sorbic Acid (Used in cheese, baked goods, and beverages)
  • Sodium Metabisulfite (Used in dried fruits, wine, and pickles)
  • Propionic Acid (Prevents mold in baked goods)
  • Calcium Propionate (Used in bread to prevent mold and bacteria growth)
  • BHT - Butylated Hydroxytoluene (Used in oils, snacks, and cereals to prevent oxidation)
  • BHA - Butylated Hydroxyanisole (Used in butter, meats, and baked goods to prevent spoilage)

Cleansing Agents

Cleansing agents are substances used to remove dirt, grease, and impurities from surfaces, skin, clothes, and utensils. They function by breaking down oils and suspending dirt in water for easy removal.

Cleansing agents can be classified into soaps and detergents based on their composition and effectiveness in different types of water. While soaps are derived from natural fats and oils, detergents are synthetic and can work efficiently in both soft and hard water.

Soaps

Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain fatty acids. They are commonly used as cleansing agents for removing dirt, grease, and oil by emulsification.

Examples of Soaps

  • Sodium Stearate (C17H35COO⁻Na⁺) – Common soap ingredient
  • Potassium Palmitate (C15H31COO⁻K⁺) – Used in liquid soaps
  • Sodium Oleate (C17H33COO⁻Na⁺) – Found in shaving creams

Preparation of Soaps

Soaps are prepared by the process of saponification. In this process, fats or oils (triglycerides) are hydrolyzed using an alkali (NaOH or KOH) to form soap and glycerol.

Reaction:
Fat/Oil + NaOH → Soap + Glycerol

Types of Soaps

  • Hard Soaps – Made using NaOH; used for bathing and washing.
  • Soft Soaps – Made using KOH; used in liquid soaps and shaving creams.
  • Transparent Soaps – Contain glycerol; provide better moisturizing.
  • Medicated Soaps – Contain antiseptics like triclosan.
  • Floating Soaps – Contain air bubbles, making them float on water.

Disadvantages of Soaps

  • Less Effective in Hard Water – Forms insoluble scum with calcium and magnesium ions.
  • Not Suitable for Acidic Solutions – Soaps decompose in acidic water.
  • Can Cause Dryness – Frequent use may strip natural oils from the skin.

Soapless Soaps (Synthetic Detergents)

Synthetic detergents, also known as soapless soaps, are cleansing agents that can work effectively in both soft and hard water. Unlike soaps, they do not form scum in hard water because they do not react with calcium and magnesium ions.

Classification of Synthetic Detergents

1. Anionic Detergents

Anionic detergents are sodium salts of sulphonated long-chain alcohols or hydrocarbons. They have negatively charged hydrophilic groups and are widely used in household cleaning products.

  • Sodium Lauryl Sulfate (SLS) – Used in shampoos and toothpaste.
  • Sodium Dodecylbenzene Sulfonate – Used in laundry detergents.

2. Cationic Detergents

Cationic detergents are quaternary ammonium salts of amines, which have positively charged hydrophilic groups. They are mainly used as fabric softeners, antiseptics, and disinfectants.

  • Cetyltrimethylammonium Bromide – Used in hair conditioners and antiseptic solutions.
  • Benzalkonium Chloride – Used in disinfectants and hand sanitizers.

3. Non-Ionic Detergents

Non-ionic detergents do not carry any charge and are effective in removing oily and greasy stains. They are used in household and industrial cleaning products.

  • Polyethylene Glycol Stearate – Used in dishwashing liquids and cosmetics.
  • Sorbitan Esters – Used in creams and lotions.

Advantages of Synthetic Detergents Over Soaps

  • Works in Hard Water – Does not form scum with calcium and magnesium ions.
  • Effective in Acidic Solutions – Can be used in acidic environments without decomposition.
  • More Soluble – Dissolves better in water, even at low temperatures.
  • Better Cleaning Power – Removes grease and dirt more effectively than soaps.
