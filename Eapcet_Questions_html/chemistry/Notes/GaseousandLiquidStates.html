Processes of Interconversion

Matter changes from one state to another based on temperature and pressure.

Intermolecular Forces

Types of Intermolecular Forces

Intermolecular forces (IMF) are the attractive forces between molecules. They determine physical properties like boiling and melting points.

  • Ion-Dipole Forces: Strongest IMF, occurs between ions and polar molecules (e.g., NaCl dissolved in water).
  • Hydrogen Bonding: Strong force between H and highly electronegative atoms (N, O, F). (e.g., H2O, NH3, HF)
  • Dipole-Dipole Interactions: Between two polar molecules (e.g., HCl, SO2).
  • London Dispersion Forces (Van der Waals): Weakest, present in all molecules, dominant in non-polar substances (e.g., CH4, Ar, I2).
Order of Strength

Ion-Dipole > Hydrogen Bonding > Dipole-Dipole > London Dispersion Forces

Examples of Intermolecular Forces
Type Example
Ion-Dipole Na+ in H2O
Hydrogen Bonding H2O, NH3, HF
Dipole-Dipole HCl, SO2
London Dispersion CH4, I2, CO2

Thermal Energy

Definition

Thermal energy is the energy possessed by a substance due to the motion of its particles. It is directly related to temperature and plays a crucial role in determining the physical state of matter.

Effects of Thermal Energy
  • Increases molecular motion.
  • Overcomes intermolecular forces, leading to phase transitions.
  • Higher thermal energy → gaseous state, Lower thermal energy → solid state.
Examples
  • Ice melts into water when thermal energy increases.
  • Water evaporates to steam at boiling temperature.

Factors Affecting the Physical State of Matter

Key Factors

The physical state of a substance depends on the balance between intermolecular forces and thermal energy.

  • Intermolecular Forces: Stronger forces → solid state (e.g., NaCl), weaker forces → gaseous state (e.g., O2).
  • Thermal Energy: Higher thermal energy → increased kinetic energy, overcoming attraction between molecules (e.g., water to steam).
  • Pressure: Increased pressure favors condensed phases (e.g., CO2 solidifies under high pressure).
  • Nature of Substance: Ionic compounds have strong bonds and exist as solids, while covalent molecules can be gases, liquids, or solids.
Example Scenarios
Condition State of Matter Example
Strong Intermolecular Forces Solid NaCl, Ice
Moderate Intermolecular Forces Liquid Water, Mercury
Weak Intermolecular Forces Gas Oxygen (O2), Nitrogen (N2)
High Pressure on a Gas Liquefied Gas Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG)
Low Temperature Solid Frozen Carbon Dioxide (Dry Ice)

Gaseous State & Characteristics

Definition

The gaseous state is a physical state of matter where particles are widely spaced and move freely, resulting in indefinite shape and volume.

Characteristics of Gases
  • Indefinite Shape & Volume: Gases take the shape and volume of their container.
  • High Compressibility: Gases can be compressed due to large intermolecular spaces.
  • Low Density: Gases have much lower density compared to solids and liquids.
  • Diffusion: Gases mix completely and uniformly without mechanical aid.
  • Exert Pressure: Gas particles collide with container walls, exerting pressure.
  • Expansion: Gases expand infinitely to fill any space.
Examples
  • Oxygen (O2), Nitrogen (N2), Carbon Dioxide (CO2), Hydrogen (H2)

Measurable Properties of Gases

Important Properties

The behavior of gases is described using the following measurable properties:

  • Pressure (P): Force exerted by gas particles per unit area of the container walls. Measured in atm, Pascal, mmHg.
  • Volume (V): Space occupied by the gas. Measured in liters (L) or cubic meters (m³).
  • Temperature (T): Determines the kinetic energy of gas particles. Measured in Kelvin (K).
  • Amount of Gas (n): Number of moles of gas, measured in moles.
Relationship Between Properties

These properties are interrelated and governed by the gas laws.

Gas Laws

1. Boyle's Law (Pressure-Volume Relationship)

Statement: At constant temperature, the volume of a gas is inversely proportional to pressure.

Formula: $$ P_1 V_1 = P_2 V_2 $$

Example: Compressing a gas-filled balloon reduces its volume.

2. Charles' Law (Temperature-Volume Relationship)

Statement: At constant pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

Formula: $$ \frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2} $$

Example: A balloon expands when heated.

3. Gay-Lussac's Law (Temperature-Pressure Relationship)

Statement: At constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

Formula: $$ \frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2} $$

Example: Pressure in a sealed container increases when heated.

4. Avogadro's Law (Volume-Mole Relationship)

Statement: Equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of molecules.

Formula: $$ V \propto n $$

Example: Doubling the number of gas molecules doubles the volume.

5. Combined Gas Law

Formula: $$ \frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2} $$

Combines Boyle's, Charles', and Gay-Lussac's laws into a single equation.

Ideal Gas Equation

Equation

PV = nRT

Where:

  • P = Pressure (atm or Pa)
  • V = Volume (L or m³)
  • n = Number of moles
  • R = Universal gas constant (0.0821 L atm/mol K or 8.314 J/mol K)
  • T = Temperature in Kelvin
Applications
  • Determining molar mass of gases.
  • Calculating unknown pressure, volume, or temperature.
  • Explains behavior of gases under various conditions.
Limitations

The ideal gas equation assumes no intermolecular forces and negligible molecular volume, which is not valid for real gases.

Graham's Law of Diffusion

Statement: The rate of diffusion (or effusion) of a gas is **inversely proportional** to the square root of its **molar mass** or **density**.

Formula (Based on Molar Mass):

$$ \frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_2}{M_1}} $$

Formula (Based on Density):

$$ \frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{d_2}{d_1}} $$

Time Taken for Diffusion:

$$ \frac{t_1}{t_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_1}{M_2}} $$

Applications of Graham's Law:

  • Lighter gases diffuse faster than heavier gases.
  • Explains why H₂ diffuses faster than O₂.
  • Used in gas separation techniques (e.g., Uranium enrichment in nuclear power plants).

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures

Statement: The total pressure exerted by a mixture of non-reacting gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of individual gases present in the mixture.

Mathematical Expression:

$$ P_{total} = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + ... + P_n $$

Partial Pressure in Terms of Mole Fraction:

$$ P_A = \chi_A \cdot P_{total} $$

Where:

  • \( P_A \) = Partial pressure of gas A
  • \( \chi_A \) = Mole fraction of gas A
  • \( P_{total} \) = Total pressure of the gas mixture

Application of Dalton’s Law:

  • Used to calculate pressure in gas collection over water.
  • Helps in determining oxygen levels in scuba diving tanks.
  • Used in industrial gas mixtures (e.g., air composition).

Correction for Aqueous Vapors (Gas Collected Over Water):

$$ P_{dry gas} = P_{total} - P_{H_2O} $$

Where \( P_{H_2O} \) is the vapor pressure of water at the given temperature.

Kinetic Theory of Gases

Statement: The kinetic theory explains the behavior of gases based on the idea that gas particles are in constant, random motion and obey Newton’s laws.

Postulates of Kinetic Theory:

  1. Gases consist of a large number of tiny, discrete particles (atoms or molecules).
  2. Gas particles are in continuous, random motion.
  3. The volume occupied by the gas molecules themselves is negligible compared to the total volume of the gas.
  4. There are no intermolecular forces between gas particles (ideal gas assumption).
  5. Collisions between gas particles and with the walls of the container are perfectly elastic (no loss of energy).
  6. The pressure exerted by a gas is due to collisions of gas molecules with the container walls.
  7. The average kinetic energy of gas molecules is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
  8. Different gases at the same temperature have the same average kinetic energy.
  9. The distribution of molecular speeds follows Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.
  10. The kinetic energy of a gas molecule is given by:

Equation for Average Kinetic Energy:

$$ KE_{avg} = \frac{3}{2} k_B T $$

Where:

  • \( k_B \) = Boltzmann constant (\( 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \) J/K)
  • \( T \) = Absolute temperature (Kelvin)

Root Mean Square (RMS) Speed:

$$ v_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} $$

Most Probable Speed:

$$ v_{mp} = \sqrt{\frac{2RT}{M}} $$

Average Speed:

$$ v_{avg} = \sqrt{\frac{8RT}{\pi M}} $$

Where:

  • \( R \) = Universal gas constant (\( 8.314 \) J/mol·K)
  • \( M \) = Molar mass of gas (kg/mol)

Relationship Between Different Speeds:

$$ v_{mp} : v_{avg} : v_{rms} = \sqrt{2RT/M} : \sqrt{8RT/\pi M} : \sqrt{3RT/M} $$

or approximately

$$ v_{mp} : v_{avg} : v_{rms} = 1 : 1.128 : 1.225 $$

Deviation from Ideal Gas Behavior

Statement: Real gases deviate from ideal gas behavior due to the presence of intermolecular forces and the finite volume of gas molecules.

Reasons for Deviation:

  • Ideal gas law assumes no intermolecular forces, but real gases have attractive and repulsive forces.
  • Real gases have finite molecular volume, unlike ideal gases where molecules are point masses.
  • At high pressure, gas molecules are forced close together, increasing intermolecular interactions.
  • At low temperature, intermolecular forces become significant, leading to condensation.

Compressibility Factor (Z):

The deviation from ideal behavior is measured using the compressibility factor \(Z\):

$$ Z = \frac{PV}{nRT} $$

For an ideal gas, \( Z = 1 \), but for real gases:

  • \( Z > 1 \): Gas shows positive deviation (dominant repulsive forces).
  • \( Z < 1 \): Gas shows negative deviation (dominant attractive forces).

Van der Waals Equation for Real Gases:

To correct the ideal gas equation, Van der Waals equation is given by:

$$ \left( P + \frac{a}{V_m^2} \right) (V_m - b) = RT $$

Where:

  • \( P \) = Pressure of gas
  • \( V_m \) = Molar volume of gas
  • \( T \) = Temperature
  • \( R \) = Universal gas constant
  • \( a \) = Correction for intermolecular forces
  • \( b \) = Correction for finite molecular volume

Effects of 'a' and 'b':

  • Larger \( a \) → Stronger intermolecular attraction (e.g., gases like CO₂, NH₃).
  • Larger \( b \) → Larger molecular size (e.g., gases like He, H₂).

Critical Temperature, Pressure & Volume

Definition: The critical temperature, pressure, and volume define the conditions at which a gas can no longer be liquefied by pressure alone.

Critical Constants:

  • Critical Temperature (\(T_c\)): The highest temperature at which a gas can be liquefied by applying pressure.
  • Critical Pressure (\(P_c\)): The minimum pressure required to liquefy a gas at its critical temperature.
  • Critical Volume (\(V_c\)): The volume occupied by one mole of a gas at its critical temperature and pressure.

Mathematical Expressions:

For van der Waals gas, critical constants are given by:

$$ T_c = \frac{8a}{27Rb} $$

$$ P_c = \frac{a}{27b^2} $$

$$ V_c = 3b $$

Applications:

  • Determines the ease of liquefaction of gases.
  • Used in designing refrigeration and liquefied gas storage systems.
  • Helps in understanding supercritical fluids used in industrial processes.

Liquid State & Its Properties

Definition: The liquid state is an intermediate phase between solid and gas, having a definite volume but no fixed shape.

General Characteristics:

  • Liquids have fluidity, allowing them to take the shape of the container.
  • They have definite volume but no fixed shape.
  • Show incompressibility compared to gases.
  • Exhibit surface tension due to intermolecular forces.
  • Display viscosity, which resists flow.

Important Properties of Liquids:

  • Surface Tension: The energy required to increase the surface area of a liquid. It decreases with increasing temperature.
  • Viscosity: Resistance to flow due to intermolecular attractions.
  • Vapor Pressure: The pressure exerted by vapor in equilibrium with its liquid phase.
  • Boiling Point: The temperature at which vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure.
  • Compressibility: Liquids are much less compressible than gases.

Applications:

  • Used in hydraulic systems.
  • Viscosity affects lubrication in machines.
  • Surface tension is important in biological systems (e.g., capillary action in plants).
