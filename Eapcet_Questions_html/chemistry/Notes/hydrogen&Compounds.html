Hydrogen and its Compounds

Position of Hydrogen in the Periodic Table

Hydrogen is the first element in the periodic table with atomic number 1 and electronic configuration 1s¹. Its position is unique because:

  • Resemblance to Group 1 (Alkali Metals): Forms H⁺ ion like alkali metals.
  • Resemblance to Group 17 (Halogens): Exists as H₂ molecule and forms H⁻ like halogens.
  • Unique Placement: Placed separately due to its dual behavior.

Methods of Preparation of Dihydrogen (H₂)

Dihydrogen (H₂) can be prepared using various methods, broadly classified as:

1. Laboratory Methods
  • Reaction of Dilute Acids with Metals:
    Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂ ↑
  • Reaction of Metals with Alkalis:
    Zn + 2NaOH → Na₂ZnO₂ + H₂ ↑
2. Industrial Methods
  • Electrolysis of Water:
    2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (using platinum electrodes)
  • Steam Reforming of Methane:
    CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂ (Nickel catalyst)
  • Lane's Process:
    Iron reacts with steam to produce H₂
    3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂ ↑
3. Biological Method

Some anaerobic bacteria produce H₂ by fermentation of organic matter.

Example: Glucose fermentation

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + 2H₂

Physical and Chemical Properties of Dihydrogen (H₂)

Dihydrogen (H₂) exhibits various physical and chemical properties, as detailed below:

1. Physical Properties
  • Color and Odor: H₂ is a colorless, odorless, and tasteless gas.
  • Density: It is the lightest known gas (0.0899 g/L at STP).
  • Solubility: Slightly soluble in water.
  • Boiling and Melting Points: Very low (Melting point: -259°C, Boiling point: -253°C).
  • Thermal and Electrical Conductivity: Poor conductor of heat and electricity.
  • Diffusion Rate: Highly diffusive due to its low molecular mass.
2. Chemical Properties

Dihydrogen is a highly reactive molecule and participates in various reactions:

(i) Combustion

H₂ burns in oxygen with a blue flame, producing water.

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O + Energy

(ii) Reaction with Halogens

Forms hydrogen halides:

H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl

H₂ + Br₂ → 2HBr

(iii) Reaction with Metals

Forms metal hydrides:

2Na + H₂ → 2NaH

Ca + H₂ → CaH₂

(iv) Reaction with Non-Metals

Forms compounds like ammonia and methane:

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

C + 2H₂ → CH₄

(v) Acts as a Reducing Agent

Reduces metal oxides to metals:

CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O

Fe₂O₃ + 3H₂ → 2Fe + 3H₂O

Uses of Dihydrogen (H₂)

Dihydrogen (H₂) is widely used in various industries and applications:

1. Industrial Uses
  • Ammonia Production: Used in the Haber process to produce ammonia (NH₃), a key ingredient in fertilizers.
  • Hydrogenation of Oils: Converts vegetable oils into solid fats (e.g., margarine).
  • Petroleum Industry: Used in hydrocracking and refining crude oil.
  • Metal Extraction: Acts as a reducing agent in the extraction of metals from their oxides.
  • Rocket Fuel: Used in liquid hydrogen-oxygen fuel for space exploration.
2. Energy Uses
  • Hydrogen Fuel Cells: Used in hydrogen-powered vehicles to produce clean energy.
  • Rocket Propulsion: Used as a fuel in space rockets.
3. Laboratory and Medical Uses
  • Reducing Agent: Used in laboratories for reduction reactions.
  • Medical Applications: Used in respiratory treatments and as an antioxidant.
4. Other Uses
  • Welding: Used in oxy-hydrogen torches for welding and cutting metals.
  • Balloon Filling: Used in weather balloons due to its low density.
  • Production of Heavy Water (D₂O): Used in nuclear reactors.
Different Forms of Hydrogen

Hydrogen exists in different forms based on its bonding, reactivity, and occurrence.

1. Atomic Hydrogen (H)

Atomic hydrogen consists of single hydrogen atoms. It is highly reactive and exists only under special conditions.

  • Produced by dissociation of molecular hydrogen at high temperatures.
  • Used in atomic hydrogen welding due to its high reactivity.
2. Molecular Hydrogen (H₂)

The most stable form of hydrogen, found in nature as a diatomic molecule (H₂).

  • Major component of the universe and used in industrial applications.
  • Exists as ortho-hydrogen and para-hydrogen based on spin states.
3. Nascent Hydrogen

Freshly liberated hydrogen atoms in a highly reactive state.

  • More reactive than molecular hydrogen.
  • Generated in situ during chemical reactions.
  • Used in reduction reactions (e.g., in metal extraction).
4. Adsorbed Hydrogen

Hydrogen that is adsorbed on the surface of metals like platinum and palladium.

  • Used in catalysis and hydrogenation reactions.
  • Known for its high storage capacity in metal hydrides.
5. Isotopic Forms of Hydrogen

Hydrogen exists in three isotopic forms:

  • Protium (¹H): The most common form, with one proton and no neutrons.
  • Deuterium (²H or D): Contains one proton and one neutron; used in nuclear reactors.
  • Tritium (³H or T): Contains one proton and two neutrons; radioactive and used in nuclear fusion.
Hydrides and Their Types

Hydrides are compounds of hydrogen with other elements. They are classified into different types based on their bonding and properties.

1. Ionic Hydrides (Saline Hydrides)

Formed when hydrogen combines with highly electropositive metals (alkali and alkaline earth metals).

  • Composed of H⁻ (hydride ion).
  • Examples: NaH (Sodium Hydride), CaH₂ (Calcium Hydride).
  • Highly reactive with water, liberating H₂ gas.
2. Covalent Hydrides (Molecular Hydrides)

Formed by non-metals and hydrogen through covalent bonding.

  • Exist as discrete molecules.
  • Examples: CH₄ (Methane), NH₃ (Ammonia), H₂O (Water).
  • Can be classified as electron-deficient (e.g., B₂H₆), electron-precise (e.g., CH₄), and electron-rich (e.g., NH₃).
3. Metallic Hydrides (Interstitial Hydrides)

Formed by transition metals where hydrogen atoms occupy interstitial spaces in the metal lattice.

  • Exhibit metallic properties (conductivity, luster).
  • Examples: TiH₂ (Titanium Hydride), PdH (Palladium Hydride).
  • Used in hydrogen storage applications.
4. Complex Hydrides

Contain metal cations bonded to hydride anions in a complex structure.

  • Highly reactive and used in hydrogen storage.
  • Examples: NaBH₄ (Sodium Borohydride), LiAlH₄ (Lithium Aluminium Hydride).
  • Used as reducing agents in organic synthesis.
Water (H₂O)

Water is a vital molecule essential for life, exhibiting unique physical and chemical properties.

1. Structure of Water

Water has a **bent (V-shaped)** structure due to **sp³ hybridization** of oxygen and **two lone pairs**.

  • Bond Angle: **104.5°**
  • Polar molecule due to dipole moment.
  • Forms strong **hydrogen bonds**, responsible for its unique properties.
2. Physical Properties of Water

Water exhibits unique physical properties due to hydrogen bonding.

  • **High Boiling & Melting Point**: Due to strong hydrogen bonding.
  • **Density Anomaly**: Water is denser at **4°C** than at 0°C.
  • **High Specific Heat Capacity**: Helps in temperature regulation.
  • **Universal Solvent**: Dissolves many ionic and polar substances.
  • **High Surface Tension**: Leads to capillary action.
3. Chemical Properties of Water

Water undergoes various chemical reactions:

  • Amphoteric Nature: Acts as both acid & base.
  • Hydrolysis: Reacts with salts like Na₂CO₃ → NaOH + CO₂ + H₂O.
  • Reaction with Metals: Forms metal hydroxides & hydrogen gas (e.g., Na + H₂O → NaOH + H₂).
  • Reaction with Non-Metals: Forms acids (e.g., Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HOCl).
  • Redox Reactions: Water gets reduced in electrolysis, forming H₂ & O₂.
Purification of Water & Types of Water

Water needs purification before consumption to remove impurities and make it safe for use. Water can be classified as soft and hard water.

1. Purification of Water

Purification of water involves removing **physical, chemical, and biological impurities** using various methods:

  • Filtration: Removal of suspended particles using sand filters or activated charcoal.
  • Boiling: Kills bacteria and microorganisms.
  • Chlorination: Addition of chlorine to disinfect water.
  • Ozonation: Use of ozone gas for sterilization.
  • Reverse Osmosis (RO): Removes dissolved salts and contaminants.
  • Ion Exchange: Used for softening hard water.
2. Soft Water vs Hard Water

Water is classified based on its ability to form lather with soap.

Soft Water

Soft water contains very low amounts of dissolved calcium and magnesium salts, allowing soap to form lather easily.

  • Examples: Rainwater, distilled water.
  • Does not cause scaling in boilers or pipes.
Hard Water

Hard water contains high levels of **Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺** ions, preventing soap from lathering properly.

  • Examples: Groundwater, well water.
  • Forms scum with soap and causes scaling in pipes.
Types of Hardness & Removal Methods

Hardness of water is caused by the presence of dissolved calcium and magnesium salts. It is classified into two types:

1. Types of Hardness

(a) Temporary Hardness: Caused by dissolved bicarbonates of calcium and magnesium (Ca(HCO₃)₂, Mg(HCO₃)₂). Can be removed by boiling.

(b) Permanent Hardness: Caused by dissolved chlorides and sulfates of calcium and magnesium (CaCl₂, MgSO₄). Cannot be removed by boiling.

2. Removal of Hardness

Different methods are used to remove **temporary and permanent hardness**:

Removal of Temporary Hardness

(a) Boiling: Converts bicarbonates into insoluble carbonates, which can be filtered out.

(b) Clark’s Method: Addition of slaked lime (Ca(OH)₂) precipitates calcium as calcium carbonate.

Ca(HCO₃)₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → 2CaCO₃ ↓ + 2H₂O
Removal of Permanent Hardness

(a) Washing Soda Method: Addition of sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃) forms insoluble carbonates.

CaCl₂ + Na₂CO₃ → CaCO₃ ↓ + 2NaCl

(b) Ion Exchange Method: Hard water is passed through ion-exchange resins, which replace Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions with Na⁺ ions.

(c) Calgon Method: Sodium hexametaphosphate (Na₆P₆O₁₈) binds to calcium and magnesium, preventing their precipitation.

(d) Synthetic Zeolite Process: Zeolites exchange Na⁺ for Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺, softening the water.

Hydrogen Peroxide (H₂O₂)

Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) is a pale blue liquid with strong oxidizing properties, used as a disinfectant, bleaching agent, and in chemical synthesis.

1. Methods of Preparation

(a) Laboratory Method: Hydrogen peroxide is prepared by reacting sodium peroxide (Na₂O₂) with dilute sulfuric acid.

Na₂O₂ + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + H₂O₂

(b) Industrial Method: Prepared by the auto-oxidation of 2-ethylanthraquinol.

C₂H₁₀O₄ + O₂ → C₂H₁₀O₆ (H₂O₂)

(c) Electrolysis of Sulfuric Acid: Electrolysis of 50% H₂SO₄ produces peroxodisulfuric acid, which decomposes to give hydrogen peroxide.

H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₈ → H₂O₂ + H₂SO₄
2. Physical Properties
  • Pale blue, syrupy liquid in pure form.
  • Soluble in water and forms a weakly acidic solution.
  • Has a boiling point of 150.2°C (decomposes before boiling).
  • Shows higher viscosity than water.
  • Decomposes easily into water and oxygen.
3. Strength of Hydrogen Peroxide

The strength of H₂O₂ is expressed in two ways:

  • Volume Strength: The volume of oxygen gas (O₂) obtained from 1 mL of H₂O₂ solution upon decomposition.
  • Percentage Strength: The mass of H₂O₂ in 100 mL of solution.

Formula to Convert Volume Strength to Percentage Strength:

Percentage Strength = (Volume Strength × 0.34)%

Common Strengths:

  • 10 Volume H₂O₂ → 3% solution (used as an antiseptic).
  • 20 Volume H₂O₂ → 6% solution (used for hair bleaching).
  • 30 Volume H₂O₂ → 9-10% solution (used in chemical industries).

Hydrogen Peroxide (H₂O₂)

Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) is a pale blue liquid with strong oxidizing properties, widely used in industrial, medical, and chemical applications.

1. Structure of Hydrogen Peroxide

H₂O₂ has a **non-planar** and **open book structure** due to repulsion between lone pairs of oxygen atoms.

  • Bond angle: **94.8° in gas phase**, **90.2° in solid phase**
  • Oxygen atoms are linked by a **single peroxide bond (-O-O-)**.
  • Exists in different conformations due to **free rotation around the O-O bond**.
2. Chemical Properties of H₂O₂

H₂O₂ acts as both **oxidizing and reducing agent** in acidic and basic mediums.

(a) Oxidizing Nature:

H₂O₂ oxidizes **Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺** in acidic solution.

2Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ + 2H⁺ → 2Fe³⁺ + 2H₂O

(b) Reducing Nature:

H₂O₂ reduces **KMnO₄ to Mn²⁺** in acidic medium.

5H₂O₂ + 2KMnO₄ + 3H₂SO₄ → 2MnSO₄ + K₂SO₄ + 8H₂O + 5O₂

(c) Decomposition: H₂O₂ decomposes easily into water and oxygen.

2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂
3. Storage of Hydrogen Peroxide
  • Stored in **dark, wax-coated, or plastic bottles** to prevent decomposition by light.
  • Stabilized by adding **small amounts of urea or phosphoric acid**.
  • Kept in **cool places away from heat and contaminants**.
4. Uses of Hydrogen Peroxide
  • Medical: Used as an **antiseptic and disinfectant**.
  • Bleaching Agent: Used for **paper, textiles, and hair bleaching**.
  • Rocket Fuel: Used in **propellants** due to oxygen release.
  • Pollution Control: Used for **wastewater treatment**.
  • Laboratory Reagent: Used in **chemical synthesis and oxidation reactions**.
Heavy Water (D₂O)

Heavy water (D₂O) is a form of water where **hydrogen atoms (¹H) are replaced with deuterium (²H or D)**, a heavier isotope of hydrogen.

1. Chemical Properties of Heavy Water

Heavy water shows similar chemical properties to normal water but reacts at a slower rate due to the heavier isotope.

(a) Electrolysis: D₂O undergoes electrolysis to form deuterium gas.

2D₂O → 2D₂ + O₂

(b) Reaction with Metal Carbides: Forms deuterated hydrocarbons.

CaC₂ + 2D₂O → C₂D₂ + Ca(OD)₂

(c) Reaction with Salts: Forms deuterated compounds.

NaCl + D₂O ⇌ NaOD + HCl

(d) Reaction with Oxides: Forms deuterated acids.

P₂O₅ + 3D₂O → 2D₃PO₄
2. Uses of Heavy Water
  • Moderator in Nuclear Reactors: Used to slow down neutrons in **nuclear power plants**.
  • Biological & Medical Research: Used as a **tracer in metabolic studies**.
  • Production of Deuterium: Source of deuterium gas (D₂) for industrial and research purposes.
  • Solvent in NMR Spectroscopy: Used in **Deuterium NMR studies**.
  • Study of Reaction Mechanisms: Used to understand **kinetics and reaction pathways**.
Hydrogen Economy

The **Hydrogen Economy** refers to the use of **hydrogen as a clean and efficient energy carrier** to replace fossil fuels, reducing carbon emissions and dependency on non-renewable sources.

1. Key Features of Hydrogen Economy
  • Hydrogen is used as a **fuel** for transportation, electricity generation, and industrial applications.
  • Hydrogen is produced through **electrolysis of water** or from **fossil fuels with carbon capture**.
  • Hydrogen fuel cells generate electricity **with water as the only byproduct**.
  • Encourages the transition to **renewable energy sources** like solar and wind.
2. Advantages of Hydrogen Economy
  • Eco-Friendly: Zero carbon emissions when used in **fuel cells**.
  • Energy Security: Reduces dependence on **fossil fuels**.
  • High Energy Density: Hydrogen has a **high energy-to-mass ratio**.
  • Renewable Production: Can be produced from **water and renewable energy sources**.
  • Versatile Applications: Used in **transport, industry, and power generation**.
3. Challenges of Hydrogen Economy
  • High Production Cost: Electrolysis and hydrogen extraction are expensive.
  • Storage and Transportation Issues: Hydrogen is **highly flammable** and difficult to store safely.
  • Infrastructure Development: Requires new **fuel stations and transportation networks**.
  • Energy Losses: Converting electricity to hydrogen and back **loses energy** in the process.
4. Applications of Hydrogen Economy
  • Fuel Cell Vehicles (FCVs): Cars, buses, and trucks powered by **hydrogen fuel cells**.
  • Electricity Generation: Hydrogen-based **power plants** reduce carbon footprint.
  • Industrial Use: Used in **steel manufacturing, refineries, and ammonia production**.
  • Space Exploration: NASA uses liquid hydrogen as **rocket fuel**.
