Polymers

Polymer

A polymer is a large molecule composed of repeating structural units called monomers. These monomers are bonded together through covalent bonds.

Example: Polyethylene (PE) is a polymer made from ethylene monomers.

Polymerization

Polymerization is the chemical process by which monomers are joined together to form a polymer.

Example: In the polymerization of ethylene (CH2==CH2), multiple ethylene molecules react under heat and pressure to form polyethylene:

Classification of Polymers Based on Source of Origin

Polymers are classified into three categories based on their source of origin:

Natural Polymers

These polymers are found in nature and are produced by living organisms.

Examples: Proteins, Cellulose, Starch, Natural Rubber

Semi-Synthetic Polymers

These polymers are chemically modified versions of natural polymers.

Examples: Cellulose Acetate (Rayon), Vulcanized Rubber

Synthetic Polymers

These polymers are man-made and synthesized in laboratories.

Examples: Synthetic Rubber, PVC, Teflon, Orlon, polyethene, etc

Classification of Polymers Based on Structure

Polymers can be classified based on their molecular structure into three types:

Linear Polymers

These polymers consist of long, straight-chain molecules that are closely packed.

Properties: High density, high tensile strength, high melting and boiling points.

Examples: Polyethylene (HDPE), Nylon, PVC, Glyptal

Branched Polymers

These polymers have a main chain with side branches, leading to less efficient packing.

Properties: Lower density than linear polymers, lower tensile strength, flexible.

Examples: Low-Density Polyethylene (LDPE), Starch, Glycogen

Cross-Linked Polymers

These polymers have covalent bonds linking different polymer chains, forming a rigid structure.

Properties: Hard, brittle, high thermal stability, insoluble in solvents.

Examples: Bakelite, Vulcanized Rubber, Melamine-Formaldehyde

Classification of Polymers Based on Mode of Polymerization

Polymers are classified based on their mode of polymerization into two types:

Addition Polymers

These polymers are formed by the repeated addition of monomers containing double or triple bonds.

Homopolymers

These are addition polymers formed from a single type of monomer.

Examples: Polyethylene, Polystyrene, Teflon, PVC

Copolymers

These are addition polymers formed from two or more different monomers.

Examples: Buna-S, Buna-N, ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)

Condensation Polymers

These polymers are formed by the repeated condensation reaction between monomers, eliminating small molecules like water or HCl.

Examples: Nylon-6,6, Terylene (PET), Bakelite, Glyptal

Classification of Polymers Based on Molecular Forces

Polymers can be classified based on the intermolecular forces acting between polymer chains into four types:

Elastomers

These polymers have weak intermolecular forces, allowing them to stretch and return to their original shape.

Examples: Natural Rubber, Buna-S, Buna-N, Neoprene

Fibers

These polymers have strong intermolecular forces like hydrogen bonding, making them rigid and tough.

Examples: Nylon-6,6, Terylene (PET), Orlon, Polyacrylonitrile(PAN)

Thermoplastics

These polymers have moderate intermolecular forces and soften on heating, allowing reshaping.

Examples: Polyethylene, Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC), Polystyrene

Thermosetting Polymers

These polymers undergo permanent hardening on heating due to strong cross-linking.

Examples: Bakelite, Melamine-Formaldehyde, Vulcanized Rubber

Molecular Mass of Polymers

The molecular mass of a polymer is not uniform and is expressed using different averages:

Number Average Molecular Mass (Mn)

It is given by the formula:

Mn = Î£ (Ni Mi) / Î£ Ni

Where:

  • Ni = Number of polymer molecules of molecular mass Mi

Mass Average Molecular Mass (Mw)

It is given by the formula:

Mw = Î£ (Ni Mi2) / Î£ (Ni Mi)

Where:

  • Ni = Number of polymer molecules of molecular mass Mi

Polydispersity Index (PDI)

It is the ratio of mass average molecular mass to number average molecular mass:

PDI = Mw / Mn

PDI = 1 for a perfectly uniform polymer (monodisperse). Higher values indicate greater molecular weight distribution.
