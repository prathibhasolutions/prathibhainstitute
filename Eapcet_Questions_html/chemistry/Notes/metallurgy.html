Metallurgy

Elements in Nature: Native State and Combined State
1. Native State

Definition: Elements found in their free, uncombined form in nature are said to occur in the native state. This occurs when the element is highly stable and does not react easily with air, water, or other compounds.

Examples:

  • Noble Metals: Gold (Au), Silver (Ag), Platinum (Pt) – Found as pure metals due to their low reactivity.
  • Non-Metals: Sulfur (S), Carbon (C) – Found as elemental deposits.
  • Noble Gases: Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar) – Found in the atmosphere in free form.

Conditions Favoring Native State:

  • Low chemical reactivity.
  • High stability in atmospheric conditions.
  • Weak tendency to form compounds.
2. Combined State

Definition: Most elements are highly reactive and exist in nature as compounds, known as the combined state.

Common Forms in Combined State:

  • Oxides: Found as metal oxides (e.g., Iron in Fe₂O₃ – Hematite, Aluminum in Al₂O₃ – Bauxite).
  • Sulfides: Found as metal sulfides (e.g., ZnS – Zinc blende, PbS – Galena).
  • Carbonates: Found as metal carbonates (e.g., CaCO₃ – Limestone, MgCO₃ – Magnesite).
  • Chlorides: Found as salts (e.g., NaCl – Common salt, KCl – Sylvite).
  • Silicates: Found in rocks and minerals (e.g., Feldspar, Mica).

Reasons for Occurrence in Combined State:

  • High reactivity with oxygen, sulfur, and halogens.
  • Formation of stable compounds.
  • Abundance in nature in mineral forms.
Minerals and Ores
1. Minerals

Definition: Minerals are naturally occurring inorganic substances that have a definite chemical composition and crystalline structure. They are formed through geological processes and are found in the Earth's crust.

Types of Minerals:

  • Metallic Minerals: Contain metals in their composition.
  • Non-Metallic Minerals: Do not contain metals and are used in industries like ceramics and glass.
2. Ores

Definition: Ores are naturally occurring mineral deposits from which metals or valuable substances can be extracted profitably.

Difference Between Minerals and Ores:

  • All ores are minerals, but not all minerals are ores.
  • Ores contain metals in an economically extractable form, while minerals may or may not be useful for extraction.

Flux and Its Types

Flux is a substance added during smelting to remove impurities from the ore. It reacts with the gangue (impurities) to form slag, which can be easily separated.

Types of Flux:
  • Acidic Flux: Used when the gangue is basic (e.g., lime, CaO). Example: Silica (SiO₂) removes basic impurities like FeO.
  • Basic Flux: Used when the gangue is acidic (e.g., silica, SiO₂). Example: imestone (CaCO₃) removes acidic impurities like SiO₂.
Slag

Slag is a byproduct formed when flux reacts with gangue during smelting. It is lighter than the molten metal and floats on top, allowing easy separation.

Example of Slag Formation:

In the extraction of iron from hematite (Fe₂O₃), limestone (CaCO₃) is used as a flux:

CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (Slag)

Gangue (Matrix)

Gangue (or Matrix) refers to unwanted earthy and siliceous impurities present in ores. These impurities must be removed before extraction.

Examples of Gangue and Their Ores:
  • Silica (SiO₂) in Hematite (Fe₂O₃)
  • Phosphates in Bauxite (Al₂O₃·xH₂O)
  • Sand and Clay in Copper Pyrites (CuFeS₂)
Metallurgy

Metallurgy is the branch of chemistry that deals with the extraction of metals from their ores and refining them for practical use.

Importance of Metallurgy:
  • Used for extraction and purification of metals.
  • Plays a key role in industrial applications.
  • Helps in obtaining metals in pure form for various uses.
Types of Metallurgical Processes

Different types of metallurgical processes are used depending on the nature of the ore and the metal to be extracted.

Major Types:
  • Pyrometallurgy: Extraction of metals using high temperatures (e.g., iron, copper).
  • Hydrometallurgy: Extraction using aqueous solutions (e.g., gold, silver).
  • Electrometallurgy: Extraction using electrolysis (e.g., aluminum, sodium).
Steps Involved in Metallurgy

The extraction of metals from ores involves multiple steps to obtain pure metals.

Main Steps in Metallurgy:
  1. Crushing and Grinding: Ores are crushed into fine powder.
  2. Concentration of Ore: Removal of gangue (impurities) by methods like gravity separation, froth flotation, or magnetic separation.
  3. Extraction of Metal: Using reduction methods like smelting, roasting, or leaching.
  4. Refining of Metal: Purification of extracted metal by methods like electrolysis or distillation.
Crushing of Ore

Crushing of ore is the first step in metallurgy where large chunks of ores are broken down into smaller pieces to facilitate further processing.

Methods:
  • Jaw Crusher: Used to crush large ore pieces into smaller fragments.
  • Gyratory Crusher: Used for heavy-duty crushing operations.
  • Ball Mill: Used to grind the crushed ore into fine powder.
Concentration of Ores

Concentration (or beneficiation) of ore is the process of removing gangue (impurities) from the ore before metal extraction.

Methods of Concentration:
1. Hydraulic Washing (Gravity Separation / Levigation)

This method is based on the difference in densities of the ore and the gangue. The heavier ore particles settle down while lighter impurities are washed away.

Example: Used for oxide ores like tin ore (SnO₂).
2. Froth Flotation Process

This process is used for sulphide ores. The ore is mixed with water, pine oil, and air is blown through the mixture, forming froth which carries ore particles while gangue remains in water.

Example: Used for ZnS (Zinc Blende), PbS (Galena), CuFeS₂ (Copper Pyrites).
3. Electromagnetic Separation

This method separates magnetic ores from non-magnetic gangue using an electromagnet.

Example: Fe₃O₄ (Magnetite), Fe₂O₃ (Hematite).
4. Electrostatic Separation

This method is based on the difference in electrical properties of the ore and gangue particles. It is used to separate conducting and non-conducting ores.

Example: Used for cassiterite (SnO₂) from silica impurities.
5. Leaching

This is a chemical method where ore is dissolved in a suitable solvent and then precipitated to obtain pure metal.

Examples:
  • Bauxite (Al₂O₃.2H₂O): Leached using NaOH (Bayer’s Process).
  • Silver (Ag) and Gold (Au): Leached using NaCN solution (Cyanide Process).
Extraction of Crude Metals from Concentrated Ore

After the ore is concentrated, the next step is the extraction of the crude metal. This is done using chemical or electrolytic processes.

Methods of Extraction:
1. Pyrometallurgy

Pyrometallurgy involves the use of high temperatures to extract metals from their ores. It includes several steps:

a. Roasting

Roasting is the process of heating concentrated ore in the presence of oxygen to convert sulfides into oxides and remove volatile impurities like sulfur, arsenic, and phosphorus.

Example:

2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂ (Zinc sulfide to Zinc oxide)

b. Calcination

Calcination is the process of heating the ore in the absence of oxygen to remove moisture and volatile impurities like CO₂.

Example:

CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (Limestone to quicklime)

c. Smelting

Smelting is the process of heating ore with reducing agents like carbon (coke) or CO to extract metal.

Example:

Fe₂O₃ + 3C → 2Fe + 3CO (Iron extraction in blast furnace)

2. Hydrometallurgy

Involves dissolving ores in suitable chemical solvents and then recovering metals from the solution.

Example:
  • Gold and silver extracted by cyanide process:

    • 4Au + 8CN⁻ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4[Au(CN)₂]⁻ + 4OH⁻

3. Electrolytic Reduction

Electrolytic reduction is used for highly reactive metals like Na, Al, and Mg, which cannot be reduced using chemical methods.

Example: Extraction of Aluminium

Hall-Héroult Process: Electrolytic reduction of alumina (Al₂O₃) in a molten cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) bath.

Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (At cathode)

Thermodynamic Principles in Extraction of Metals

The feasibility of extracting metals from their ores depends on Gibbs free energy (ΔG).

1. Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)

The spontaneity of metal extraction is determined by:

ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

  • If ΔG is negative: Reaction is spontaneous.
  • If ΔG is positive: Reaction is non-spontaneous, requires external energy.
  • Highly negative ΔG means better feasibility of metal extraction.
2. Role of Temperature in Metal Extraction

Since ΔG depends on temperature (T):

  • At higher temperatures, some reductions become feasible.
  • Metals like Al, Mg (highly negative ΔG) can reduce oxides of other metals.
Ellingham Diagram and Its Characteristics

The Ellingham diagram plots ΔG vs. Temperature for metal oxidation reactions.

1. General Features
  • Most metal oxidation lines have a positive slope.
  • A metal can reduce another metal oxide if its line lies below the other metal.
2. Important Observations
  • CO reduces FeO, CuO, etc., at high temperatures.
  • Al, Mg have highly negative ΔG and can reduce most metal oxides.
  • At higher temperatures, carbon becomes a better reducing agent.
3. Applications in Metallurgy
  • Helps determine the best reducing agent.
  • Predicts temperature conditions for metal extraction.
  • Explains why carbon cannot reduce Al₂O₃, MgO, etc.
Refining of Metals

Definition: Refining of metals is the process of removing impurities from crude metals obtained after extraction to get pure metal.

Physical Methods of Refining
Liquation

Process: The impure metal is heated on a sloping surface. The pure metal melts and flows away, leaving impurities behind.

Example: Tin (Sn) is purified using liquation.

Distillation

Process: Used for volatile metals. The impure metal is heated, and the vapors of the metal are collected and condensed.

Example: Zinc (Zn) and Mercury (Hg).

Electrolytic Refining

Process: The impure metal is the anode, and a thin strip of pure metal is the cathode. The metal gets deposited on the cathode.

Example: Copper (Cu) is purified using CuSO₄ and H₂SO₄ solution.

Chemical Methods of Refining
Zone Refining

Process: The impure metal rod is passed through a moving heater, melting and purifying the metal as impurities move to the end.

Example: Used for semiconductor materials like Silicon (Si) and Germanium (Ge).

Vapor Phase Refining

Process: The impure metal is first converted into a volatile compound and then decomposed to get pure metal.

  • Van Arkel Method: Titanium (Ti) reacts with Iodine to form TiI₄, which decomposes on a heated filament to give pure Ti.
  • Mond's Process: Nickel (Ni) reacts with CO to form Ni(CO)₄, which decomposes to yield pure Nickel.
Chromatographic Methods

Process: Based on adsorption principles, different components in a solution travel at different speeds over a stationary phase, separating impurities.

Example: Used for rare earth metals like Lanthanides.

Occurrence, Extraction, and Uses of Metals
1. Aluminium (Al)
Occurrence

Aluminium is the most abundant metal in Earth's crust, found in the form of bauxite (Al₂O₃·2H₂O) and cryolite (Na₃AlF₆).

Extraction

Method: Aluminium is extracted using the Bayer’s Process and Hall-Héroult Process.

Steps:

  • Bayer’s Process: Bauxite is purified to get alumina (Al₂O₃).
  • Hall-Héroult Process: Electrolytic reduction of Al₂O₃ in molten cryolite at 950°C gives pure Aluminium.
Uses
  • Used in aircraft and automobile industries due to its light weight.
  • Electrical wiring as it is a good conductor.
  • Used in packaging (foils, cans) and kitchen utensils.
2. Iron (Fe)
Occurrence

Found in haematite (Fe₂O₃), magnetite (Fe₃O₄), and siderite (FeCO₃).

Extraction

Method: Extracted by blast furnace reduction.

Steps:

  • Iron ore is mixed with coke and limestone and heated in a blast furnace.
  • Carbon monoxide reduces Fe₂O₃ to molten iron.
Uses
  • Used in construction (bridges, buildings, machinery).
  • Steel, an alloy of iron, is widely used in tools, vehicles, and appliances.
  • Cast iron is used in pipes and engine blocks.
3. Copper (Cu)
Occurrence

Found in chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂), malachite (Cu₂CO₃(OH)₂), and cuprite (Cu₂O).

Extraction

Method: Extracted by froth flotation followed by roasting and smelting.

Steps:

  • Copper ore is concentrated using froth flotation.
  • Roasting removes sulfur and converts CuFeS₂ to Cu₂O.
  • Reduction with carbon produces pure copper.
Uses
  • Used in electrical wiring due to excellent conductivity.
  • Used in plumbing, coins, and jewelry.
  • Brass (Cu-Zn alloy) and bronze (Cu-Sn alloy) are used in decorative items.
4. Zinc (Zn)
Occurrence

Found in zinc blende (ZnS), calamine (ZnCO₃), and smithsonite (ZnCO₃).

Extraction

Method: Zinc is extracted by roasting and reduction.

Steps:

  • ZnS is roasted in air to form ZnO.
  • ZnO is reduced using coke to obtain Zn metal.
Uses
  • Used in galvanization to prevent rusting of iron.
  • Used in making brass (Zn-Cu alloy).
  • Zinc oxide is used in paints and cosmetics.
