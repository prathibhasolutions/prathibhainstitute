Solid State

Solid State: Characteristics & Types

Definition: Solids are a state of matter characterized by structural rigidity and resistance to changes in shape and volume.

General Characteristics of Solids:

  • Have a definite shape and volume.
  • Intermolecular forces are strongest among the three states.
  • Solids are incompressible and have high density.
  • They can be crystalline (ordered structure) or amorphous (disordered structure).

Difference Between Crystalline & Amorphous Solids:

Property Crystalline Solids Amorphous Solids
Structure Regular, repeating arrangement of particles Irregular and disordered arrangement of particles
Melting Point Sharp, definite melting point No sharp melting point, softens over a range
Cleavage Property Breaks into clean surfaces Breaks into irregular shapes
Anisotropy Properties depend on direction Properties are the same in all directions (isotropic)
Examples Salt (NaCl), Quartz (SiO₂), Diamond Glass, Rubber, Plastic

Types of Crystalline Solids:

Type Forces Holding Particles Examples Properties
Ionic Solids Electrostatic forces NaCl, KCl, MgO Hard, high melting points, conduct electricity in molten state
Covalent Solids Covalent bonds Diamond, SiO₂ Very hard, high melting points, non-conductors
Molecular Solids Van der Waals forces I₂, CO₂, H₂O (Ice) Soft, low melting points, poor conductors
Metallic Solids Metallic bonds (electron sea model) Iron, Copper, Gold Malleable, ductile, good conductors

Applications:

  • Crystalline solids are used in electronics, jewelry, and optical devices.
  • Amorphous solids like glass are used in windows, screens, and optical fibers.
  • Metallic solids are essential in construction, machinery, and transportation.
Bragg’s Equation

Statement: Bragg’s Law explains the diffraction of X-rays by a crystal lattice, leading to constructive interference.

Mathematical Expression:

$$ n\lambda = 2d\sin\theta $$

Where:

  • \( n \) = Order of diffraction (integer: 1, 2, 3...)
  • \( \lambda \) = Wavelength of incident X-rays
  • \( d \) = Interplanar spacing between atomic layers
  • \( \theta \) = Angle of incidence (glancing angle)

Applications of Bragg’s Equation:

  • Determining crystal structures using X-ray diffraction.
  • Analyzing interatomic distances in solids.
  • Used in material science and nanotechnology research.
Crystal Lattice & Unit Cells

Definition: A crystal lattice is a three-dimensional arrangement of points representing atoms, ions, or molecules in a crystalline solid.

Unit Cell: The smallest repeating structure in a crystal lattice that retains the full symmetry of the solid.

Types of Unit Cells:

  • Simple (Primitive) Unit Cell: Lattice points only at corners.
  • Body-Centered Unit Cell: Lattice points at corners + one in the center.
  • Face-Centered Unit Cell: Lattice points at corners + one at each face center.
  • End-Centered Unit Cell: Lattice points at corners + one at the center of two opposite faces.
Number of Particles Per Unit Cell
Type of Unit Cell Contribution from Corner Atoms Contribution from Face-Centered Atoms Contribution from Body-Centered Atom Total Number of Particles
Simple Cubic (SC) 8 corners × 1/8 0 0 1
Body-Centered Cubic (BCC) 8 corners × 1/8 0 1 center × 1 2
Face-Centered Cubic (FCC) 8 corners × 1/8 6 faces × 1/2 0 4

Summary:

  • SC has atom per unit cell.
  • BCC has 2 atoms per unit cell.
  • FCC has 4 atoms per unit cell.

Coordination Number

Definition: The coordination number of an atom in a crystal is the number of nearest neighbor atoms surrounding it.

Crystal Type Unit Cell Type Coordination Number
Simple Cubic (SC) Primitive 6
Body-Centered Cubic (BCC) Body-Centered 8
Face-Centered Cubic (FCC) Face-Centered 12
Hexagonal Close Packing (HCP) Close-Packed 12

Observation: Higher coordination numbers result in better packing efficiency.

Close Packing in Crystals

1D Close Packing: Atoms are arranged in a single straight line, where each atom touches two neighbors.

Coordination Number: 2

2D Close Packing: Two possible arrangements:

  • Square Packing: Atoms arranged in straight rows & columns. (Coordination Number = 4)
  • Hexagonal Packing: Atoms arranged in a **honeycomb-like** pattern. (Coordination Number = 6)

3D Close Packing:

  • Simple Cubic Packing (SCP): Atoms stacked layer by layer (Coordination Number = 6, Packing Efficiency = 52.4%)
  • Body-Centered Cubic (BCC): Atoms arranged with one atom at center (Coordination Number = 8, Packing Efficiency = 68%)
  • Face-Centered Cubic (FCC) & Hexagonal Close Packing (HCP): Tightly packed arrangement (Coordination Number = 12, Packing Efficiency = 74%)

Packing Efficiency of Unit Cells

Definition: Packing efficiency is the percentage of total space occupied by particles in a unit cell.

1. Formula for Packing Efficiency

$$ \text{Packing Efficiency} = \left( \frac{\text{Volume of atoms in unit cell}}{\text{Total volume of unit cell}} \right) \times 100 $$

2. Packing Efficiency for Different Unit Cells
Crystal Structure Atoms per Unit Cell Relation (\( R \) and \( a \)) Packing Efficiency (%)
Simple Cubic (SC) 1 $$ a = 2R $$ 52.4%
Body-Centered Cubic (BCC) 2 $$ a = \frac{4R}{\sqrt{3}} $$ 68.0%
Face-Centered Cubic (FCC) 4 $$ a = \frac{4R}{\sqrt{2}} $$ 74.0%
Hexagonal Close Packing (HCP) 6 $$ a = 2R $$ 74.0%
3. Calculation Steps for FCC Packing Efficiency

Step 1: Volume of a single atom is:

$$ V_{\text{atom}} = \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3 $$

Step 2: Volume occupied by 4 atoms in FCC:

$$ V_{\text{occupied}} = 4 \times \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3 $$

Step 3: Volume of unit cell:

$$ V_{\text{cell}} = a^3 = \left( \frac{4R}{\sqrt{2}} \right)^3 $$

Step 4: Packing Efficiency:

$$ \text{Packing Efficiency} = \left( \frac{4 \times \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3}{\left( \frac{4R}{\sqrt{2}} \right)^3} \right) \times 100 $$

Final result:

$$ \boxed{74.0\%} $$

Density of a Unit Cell

Definition: The density (\( \rho \)) of a unit cell is the mass per unit volume of the cell.

Formula:

\[ \rho = \frac{Z \times M}{N_A \times a^3} \]

  • \( \rho \) = Density of unit cell (g/cm³)
  • \( Z \) = Number of atoms per unit cell
  • \( M \) = Molar mass of element (g/mol)
  • \( N_A \) = Avogadro’s number (\(6.022 \times 10^{23}\) atoms/mol)
  • \( a^3 \) = Volume of unit cell (cm³)

Example: For FCC structure:

\[ \rho = \frac{4 \times 63.5}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \times (3.61 \times 10^{-8})^3} \]

Observation: The higher the packing efficiency, the higher the density.

Imperfections (Defects) in Solids
1. Introduction

Definition: Imperfections or defects in solids refer to irregularities in the arrangement of constituent particles (atoms, ions, or molecules) within a crystalline solid.

Types of Defects:

  • Point Defects (Affect individual atoms or ions)
  • Line Defects (Affect rows of atoms, also called dislocations)
2. Types of Point Defects
Type Definition Example
Stoichiometric Defects Defects that do not disturb the stoichiometry of the solid. Schottky & Frenkel defects
Non-Stoichiometric Defects Defects that alter the ideal chemical formula of a compound. Metal excess & metal deficiency defects
Impurity Defects Defects caused by the presence of foreign atoms in the lattice. Doping in semiconductors
3. Stoichiometric Defects
Schottky Defect

Definition: Equal numbers of cations and anions are missing from their lattice sites.

Effect: Decreases the density of the crystal.

Examples: NaCl, KCl, CsCl

Frenkel Defect

Definition: A cation is displaced from its normal position to an interstitial site.

Effect: No change in density but increases electrical conductivity.

Examples: ZnS, AgCl

Comparison:

  • Schottky defects decrease density, while Frenkel defects do not.
  • Schottky defects occur in highly ionic compounds, while Frenkel defects are seen in low-coordination compounds.
4. Non-Stoichiometric Defects
Metal Excess Defect

Definition: Extra metal ions occupy interstitial positions.

Effect: Increases electrical conductivity and color.

Example: ZnO (yellow on heating)

Metal Deficiency Defect

Definition: A cation is missing, and another cation of a different oxidation state is present.

Effect: Creates holes in the structure.

Example: FeO

5. Impurity Defects

Definition: Introduction of foreign atoms in the lattice, commonly seen in semiconductors.

Example: Silicon doped with phosphorus (n-type semiconductor).

6. Line Defects

Definition: Also called dislocations, they occur due to misalignment of entire rows of atoms.

Effect: Responsible for mechanical properties like ductility and strength.

7. Consequences of Defects
  • Increase in electrical conductivity (due to free electrons in metal excess defects).
  • Change in density (Schottky defects lower density).
  • Introduction of color in crystals (ZnO turns yellow due to metal excess defects).
Electrical Properties of Solids
1. Classification Based on Electrical Conductivity
Type Conductivity (S m⁻¹) Examples
Conductors 10⁴ to 10⁷ Metals like Cu, Ag, Fe
Semiconductors 10⁻⁶ to 10⁴ Si, Ge
Insulators < 10⁻¹² Plastic, Wood, Rubber
2. Band Theory of Solids

Definition: Electrical conductivity depends on the availability of free electrons in the conduction band.

Explanation:

  • Conductors: Valence band and conduction band overlap.
  • Semiconductors: Small energy gap (~1 eV) between valence and conduction bands.
  • Insulators: Large energy gap (>3 eV), preventing electron movement.
3. Types of Semiconductors
Intrinsic Semiconductors

Pure semiconductors with electron-hole pairs generated thermally.

Example: Pure silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge).

Extrinsic Semiconductors

Impurities are added to enhance conductivity.

Types:

  • n-type: Extra electrons from donor atoms (P, As).
  • p-type: Holes due to acceptor atoms (B, Al).
