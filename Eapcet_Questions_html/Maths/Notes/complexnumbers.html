Complex Numbers

Complex Numbers

A complex number is a number of the form:

\( z = a + bi \)

where:

  • \( a \) is the real part of the complex number
  • \( b \) is the imaginary part
  • \( i \) (or \( j \) in electrical engineering) is the imaginary unit, where \( i^2 = -1 \)
Example:

Consider the complex number: \( z = 3 + 4i \)

  • Real part: \( 3 \)
  • Imaginary part: \( 4 \)

Purely Real and Purely Imaginary Numbers

Purely Real Numbers

A complex number is purely real if its imaginary part is zero.

Example: \( z = 5 + 0i = 5 \) (which is just a real number).

Purely Imaginary Numbers

A complex number is purely imaginary if its real part is zero.

Example: \( z = 0 + 7i = 7i \) (which is a purely imaginary number).

Iota (\( i \)) and Powers of \( i^n \)

Definition of Iota (\( i \))

The imaginary unit, denoted as \( i \), is defined as:

\( i = \sqrt{-1} \)

It follows that:

  • \( i^2 = -1 \)
  • \( i^3 = i^2 \cdot i = -i \)
  • \( i^4 = i^2 \cdot i^2 = 1 \)
General Formula for \( i^n \)

The powers of \( i \) repeat in a cycle of four:

\( i^n = i^{(n \mod 4)} \)

Cyclic Pattern:
  • \( i^1 = i \)
  • \( i^2 = -1 \)
  • \( i^3 = -i \)
  • \( i^4 = 1 \) (cycle repeats)

To compute \( i^n \), find \( n \mod 4 \) and use the above pattern.

Example Calculation:

Find \( i^{23} \):

\( 23 \mod 4 = 3 \), so \( i^{23} = i^3 = -i \).

Algebra of Complex Numbers

Operations on complex numbers involve addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Given two complex numbers:

\( z_1 = a + bi \) and \( z_2 = c + di \)

Addition

Sum of two complex numbers is given by:

\( (a + bi) + (c + di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i \)

Example:

\( (3 + 4i) + (2 + 5i) = (3+2) + (4+5)i = 5 + 9i \)

Subtraction

Difference of two complex numbers is given by:

\( (a + bi) - (c + di) = (a-c) + (b-d)i \)

Example:

\( (6 + 7i) - (2 + 3i) = (6-2) + (7-3)i = 4 + 4i \)

Multiplication

Product of two complex numbers is given by:

\( (a + bi) \times (c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i \)

Example:

\( (1 + 2i) \times (3 + 4i) = (1\times3 - 2\times4) + (1\times4 + 2\times3)i = (3 - 8) + (4 + 6)i = -5 + 10i \)

Division

Division of two complex numbers is given by:

\( \frac{a+bi}{c+di} = \frac{(a+bi)(c-di)}{c^2 + d^2} \)

Example:

\( \frac{(3 + 2i)}{(1 - i)} \)

Multiply numerator and denominator by conjugate \( (1 + i) \):

\( \frac{(3+2i)(1+i)}{(1-i)(1+i)} = \frac{(3 + 3i + 2i + 2i^2)}{(1^2 - i^2)} \)

\( = \frac{(3 + 3i + 2i - 2)}{(1+1)} = \frac{(1 + 5i)}{2} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{5}{2}i \)

Square Root of a Complex Number

The square root of a complex number \( z = a + bi \) is given by:

Formula:

\[ \sqrt{z} = \pm \left( \sqrt{\frac{r + a}{2}} + i \sqrt{\frac{r - a}{2}} \right) \]

where:

  • \( r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \) (Modulus of \( z \))
Example: Find \( \sqrt{3 + 4i} \)

Given \( z = 3 + 4i \), we first calculate:

  • \( r = |z| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \)
  • \( \sqrt{\frac{r + a}{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{5 + 3}{2}} = \sqrt{4} = 2 \)
  • \( \sqrt{\frac{r - a}{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{5 - 3}{2}} = \sqrt{1} = 1 \)

So, the square roots of \( 3 + 4i \) are:

\( \pm (2 + i) \)

Conjugate of Complex Numbers

Definition

The conjugate of a complex number \( z = a + bi \) is denoted as \( \overline{z} \) and is given by:

\( \overline{z} = a - bi \)

It is obtained by changing the sign of the imaginary part.

Example:

For \( z = 3 + 4i \), the conjugate is \( \overline{z} = 3 - 4i \).

Properties of Complex Conjugate
  • 1. Conjugate of a sum: \( \overline{(z_1 + z_2)} = \overline{z_1} + \overline{z_2} \)
  • 2. Conjugate of a product: \( \overline{(z_1 \times z_2)} = \overline{z_1} \times \overline{z_2} \)
  • 3. Conjugate of a difference: \( \overline{(z_1 - z_2)} = \overline{z_1} - \overline{z_2} \)
  • 4. Conjugate of a quotient: \( \overline{\left(\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right)} = \frac{\overline{z_1}}{\overline{z_2}} \)
  • 5. Modulus property: \( z \times \overline{z} = |z|^2 = a^2 + b^2 \)
  • 6. If \( z \) is purely real: \( \overline{z} = z \)
  • 7. If \( z \) is purely imaginary: \( \overline{z} = -z \)
Example:

For \( z_1 = 2 + 3i \) and \( z_2 = 1 - 4i \):

  • \( \overline{z_1} = 2 - 3i \), \( \overline{z_2} = 1 + 4i \)
  • \( \overline{(z_1 + z_2)} = \overline{(3 - i)} = 3 + i \)
  • \( \overline{(z_1 \times z_2)} = \overline{(14 - 5i)} = 14 + 5i \)

Modulus of a Complex Number

Definition

The modulus (or absolute value) of a complex number \( z = a + bi \) is denoted as \( |z| \) and is given by:

\( |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \)

It represents the distance of the complex number from the origin in the complex plane.

Example:

For \( z = 3 + 4i \), the modulus is:

\( |z| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \)

Properties of Modulus of Complex Numbers
  • 1. Modulus of a product: \( |z_1 \times z_2| = |z_1| \times |z_2| \)
  • 2. Modulus of a quotient: \( \left| \frac{z_1}{z_2} \right| = \frac{|z_1|}{|z_2|} \) (if \( z_2 \neq 0 \))
  • 3. Modulus of a conjugate: \( |\overline{z}| = |z| \)
  • 4. Triangle Inequality: \( |z_1 + z_2| \leq |z_1| + |z_2| \)
  • 5. Difference property: \( |z_1 - z_2| \geq ||z_1| - |z_2|| \)
  • 6. Modulus squared property: \( |z|^2 = z \times \overline{z} = a^2 + b^2 \)
  • 7. If \( z \) is purely real: \( |z| = |a| \) (since \( b = 0 \))
  • 8. If \( z \) is purely imaginary: \( |z| = |b| \) (since \( a = 0 \))
Example Calculations:

For \( z_1 = 3 + 4i \) and \( z_2 = 1 - 2i \):

  • \( |z_1| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5 \), \( |z_2| = \sqrt{1^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{1+4} = \sqrt{5} \)
  • \( |z_1 \times z_2| = |z_1| \times |z_2| = 5 \times \sqrt{5} \)
  • \( \left| \frac{z_1}{z_2} \right| = \frac{|z_1|}{|z_2|} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{5}} \)

Argand Plane and Argument of a Complex Number

Argand Plane

The Argand Plane is a way to represent complex numbers geometrically. Each complex number \( z = a + bi \) is plotted as a point \( (a, b) \) on a 2D coordinate system:

  • The x-axis represents the real part \( a \).
  • The y-axis represents the imaginary part \( b \).

For example, the complex number \( z = 3 + 4i \) is plotted at \( (3,4) \).

Argument of a Complex Number

The argument \( \theta \) of a complex number \( z = a + bi \) is the angle made by the line joining \( (a, b) \) to the origin with the positive real axis.

Formula:

\( \theta = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{b}{a} \right) \)

Cases Based on Quadrants:
  • Quadrant I: \( a > 0, b > 0 \Rightarrow \theta = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{b}{a} \right) \)
  • Quadrant II: \( a < 0, b > 0 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi - \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{b}{a} \right) \)
  • Quadrant III: \( a < 0, b < 0 \Rightarrow \theta = -\pi + \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{b}{a} \right) \)
  • Quadrant IV: \( a > 0, b < 0 \Rightarrow \theta = - \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{b}{a} \right) \)
Example:

For \( z = -1 + \sqrt{3}i \):

\( \theta = \pi + \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{\sqrt{3}}{-1} \right) = \pi - \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{2\pi}{3} \).

Properties of Argument of Complex Numbers
  • 1. Argument of a product: \( \arg(z_1 \times z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2) \)
  • 2. Argument of a quotient: \( \arg \left( \frac{z_1}{z_2} \right) = \arg(z_1) - \arg(z_2) \)
  • 3. Argument of a conjugate: \( \arg(\overline{z}) = -\arg(z) \)
  • 4. Argument of a negative real number: \( \arg(-z) = \arg(z) + \pi \)
  • 5. Argument of a purely imaginary number:
    - If \( z = bi \) ( \( b > 0 \) ), then \( \arg(z) = \frac{\pi}{2} \)
    - If \( z = bi \) ( \( b < 0 \) ), then \( \arg(z) = -\frac{\pi}{2} \)
  • 6. Argument lies within a principal range: \( -\pi < \arg(z) \leq \pi \)
Example Calculations:

For \( z_1 = 1 + i \) and \( z_2 = 1 - i \):

  • \( \arg(z_1) = \tan^{-1}(1) = \frac{\pi}{4} \)
  • \( \arg(z_2) = \tan^{-1}(-1) = -\frac{\pi}{4} \)
  • \( \arg(z_1 \times z_2) = \arg(z_1) + \arg(z_2) = 0 \)

Polar Form and Eulerian Form of a Complex Number

Polar Form of a Complex Number

A complex number \( z = a + bi \) can be represented in polar form using its modulus \( r \) and argument \( \theta \):

Formula:

\( z = r (\cos \theta + i \sin \theta) \)

where:

  • \( r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \) (Modulus of \( z \))
  • \( \theta = \arg(z) = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{b}{a} \right) \) (Argument of \( z \))
Example:

For \( z = 1 + \sqrt{3}i \):

  • \( r = \sqrt{1^2 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{4} = 2 \)
  • \( \theta = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{\sqrt{3}}{1} \right) = \frac{\pi}{3} \)

So, the polar form is:

\( z = 2 (\cos \frac{\pi}{3} + i \sin \frac{\pi}{3}) \)

Eulerian Form of a Complex Number

Using Euler’s formula:

\( e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i \sin \theta \)

The polar form can be rewritten as:

Formula:

\( z = r e^{i\theta} \)

Example:

For \( z = 1 + \sqrt{3}i \), we found that:

\( r = 2 \), \( \theta = \frac{\pi}{3} \)

So, the Eulerian form is:

\( z = 2 e^{i\pi/3} \)

De Moivre’s Theorem

De Moivre’s Theorem states that for any integer \( n \) and a complex number in polar form:

Formula:

\[ (r e^{i\theta})^n = r^n e^{i n \theta} \]

or in trigonometric form:

\[ (\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)^n = \cos (n\theta) + i \sin (n\theta) \]

Properties of De Moivre’s Theorem
  • Helps in computing powers and roots of complex numbers.
  • For negative exponents: \[ (\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)^{-n} = \cos (-n\theta) + i \sin (-n\theta) \]
  • For fractional exponents (nth roots): \[ z^{1/n} = r^{1/n} e^{i (\theta + 2k\pi)/n}, \quad k = 0, 1, ..., n-1 \]
  • Multiplication of Complex Numbers (using cis notation): \[ \operatorname{cis}(\theta_1) \times \operatorname{cis}(\theta_2) = \operatorname{cis}(\theta_1 + \theta_2) \]
  • Division of Complex Numbers: \[ \frac{\operatorname{cis}(\theta_1)}{\operatorname{cis}(\theta_2)} = \operatorname{cis}(\theta_1 - \theta_2) \]
Example: Compute \( (1 + i)^5 \) Using De Moivre’s Theorem

Step 1: Convert \( 1 + i \) to polar form:

  • \( r = |z| = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2} \)
  • \( \theta = \tan^{-1} (1/1) = \frac{\pi}{4} \)

Step 2: Apply De Moivre’s Theorem:

\[ (1 + i)^5 = (\sqrt{2})^5 e^{i (5\pi/4)} \]

Step 3: Convert back to rectangular form:

\[ = 4 (\cos (5\pi/4) + i \sin (5\pi/4)) \]

\[ = 4 \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} - i\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \]

\[ = -2\sqrt{2} - 2\sqrt{2} i \]

So, \( (1 + i)^5 = -2\sqrt{2} - 2\sqrt{2} i \).

Cube Roots of Unity

The cube roots of unity are the three solutions of the equation:

Equation:

\[ x^3 = 1 \]

These roots are given by:

\[ 1, \quad \omega = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{3}i}{2}, \quad \omega^2 = \frac{-1 - \sqrt{3}i}{2} \]

Properties of Cube Roots of Unity
  • \( 1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0 \)
  • \( \omega^3 = 1 \) and \( \omega^4 = \omega \), \( \omega^5 = \omega^2 \), etc.
  • \( \frac{1}{\omega} = \omega^2 \) and \( \frac{1}{\omega^2} = \omega \)
  • \( \omega^2 = \bar{\omega} \) (complex conjugate of \( \omega \))
  • \( \omega^n \) repeats every three steps, following the cycle: \[ 1, \omega, \omega^2, 1, \omega, \omega^2, \dots \]
Example: Compute \( (1 + \omega)^3 \)

Step 1: Expand using binomial theorem:

\[ (1 + \omega)^3 = 1^3 + 3(1^2 \omega) + 3(1 \omega^2) + \omega^3 \]

Step 2: Use properties \( \omega^3 = 1 \) and \( 1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0 \):

\[ = 1 + 3\omega + 3\omega^2 + 1 \]

\[ = 2 + 3(-1) = -1 \]

So, \( (1 + \omega)^3 = -1 \).
