Differentiation Formulas

Formula
\( \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = n x^{n-1} \), where \( n \) is any real number
\( \frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[a^x] = a^x \ln(a) \), where \( a \) is a constant
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\ln(x)] = \frac{1}{x} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\log_a(x)] = \frac{1}{x \ln(a)} \), where \( a \) is a constant
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x)] = \cos(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\cos(x)] = -\sin(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\tan(x)] = \sec^2(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\sec(x)] = \sec(x) \tan(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\csc(x)] = -\csc(x) \cot(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\cot(x)] = -\csc^2(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\sin^{-1}(x)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\cos^{-1}(x)] = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\tan^{-1}(x)] = \frac{1}{1+x^2} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\sinh(x)] = \cosh(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\cosh(x)] = \sinh(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\tanh(x)] = \text{sech}^2(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{sech}(x)] = -\text{sech}(x) \tanh(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{csch}(x)] = -\text{csch}(x) \cot(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{coth}(x)] = -\text{csch}^2(x) \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{sinh}^{-1}(x)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x^2}} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{cosh}^{-1}(x)] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2-1}} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{tanh}^{-1}(x)] = \frac{1}{1-x^2} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{sech}^{-1}(x)] = -\frac{1}{x \sqrt{1 - x^2}} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{csch}^{-1}(x)] = -\frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{1 + x^2}} \)
\( \frac{d}{dx}[\text{coth}^{-1}(x)] = \frac{1}{1 - x^2} \)

Fundamental Rules for Derivatives

Product Rule

The derivative of a product of two functions is given by:

\( \frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \cdot g(x)] = f'(x) \cdot g(x) + f(x) \cdot g'(x) \)

Example: Let \( f(x) = x^2 \) and \( g(x) = \sin(x) \). Then:

\( \frac{d}{dx}[x^2 \cdot \sin(x)] = 2x \cdot \sin(x) + x^2 \cdot \cos(x) \)

Quotient Rule

The derivative of a quotient of two functions is given by:

\( \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{f'(x) \cdot g(x) - f(x) \cdot g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \)

Example: Let \( f(x) = x^2 \) and \( g(x) = \cos(x) \). Then:

\( \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{x^2}{\cos(x)}\right] = \frac{2x \cdot \cos(x) + x^2 \cdot \sin(x)}{\cos^2(x)} \)

Sum/Difference Rule

The derivative of a sum or difference of two functions is the sum or difference of their derivatives:

\( \frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \pm g(x)] = f'(x) \pm g'(x) \)

Example: For \( f(x) = x^2 \) and \( g(x) = \cos(x) \), the derivative is:

\( \frac{d}{dx}[x^2 \pm \cos(x)] = 2x \pm (-\sin(x)) = 2x \mp \sin(x) \)

Chain Rule

The derivative of a composition of functions is the derivative of the outer function times the derivative of the inner function:

\( \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \)

Example: Let \( f(x) = \sin(x^2) \). Then:

\( \frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x^2)] = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x \)

Implicit Differentiation

For equations where \( y \) is defined implicitly as a function of \( x \), differentiate both sides with respect to \( x \), treating \( y \) as a function of \( x \), and use the chain rule where necessary:

Example: For \( x^2 + y^2 = 1 \), differentiate implicitly:

\( 2x + 2y \frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \)

Solving for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \), we get:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y} \)

Parametric Differentiation

If \( x \) and \( y \) are both functions of \( t \), then the derivative \( \frac{dy}{dx} \) is given by:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} \)

Example: For \( x(t) = t^2 \) and \( y(t) = t^3 \), the derivative is:

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3t^2}{2t} = \frac{3}{2}t \)

Logarithmic Differentiation

For differentiating functions of the form \( y = f(x)^{g(x)} \), logarithmic differentiation is used:

Take the natural logarithm of both sides:

\( \ln(y) = g(x) \ln(f(x)) \)

Now differentiate both sides with respect to \( x \):

\( \frac{1}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx}[g(x) \ln(f(x))] \)

Then solve for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \).

Example: Let \( y = x^x \), then:

\( \ln(y) = x \ln(x) \)

Differentiate both sides:

\( \frac{1}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \ln(x) + 1 \)

\( \frac{dy}{dx} = x^x (\ln(x) + 1) \)
