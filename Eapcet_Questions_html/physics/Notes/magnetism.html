Magnetism and Matter

Magnetism and Matter

Magnetism is a fundamental force associated with moving charges. It governs the behavior of magnets and magnetic materials.

Natural and Artificial Magnets

  • Natural Magnets: Occur in nature, such as magnetite (lodestone), and possess permanent magnetism.
  • Artificial Magnets: Manufactured magnets, including bar magnets, horseshoe magnets, and electromagnets.

Properties of Magnets

  • Every magnet has two poles: **North** and **South**.
  • Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.
  • Magnetic force acts at a distance.
  • A freely suspended magnet aligns with Earth's magnetic field.
  • Magnetism is strongest at the poles.

Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines represent the direction and strength of the magnetic field around a magnet.

  • They emerge from the **North pole** and enter the **South pole** externally.
  • They never intersect.
  • Density of lines indicates field strength.

Magnetic Dipole

A **magnetic dipole** consists of two opposite poles (N and S) separated by a distance.

Pole Strength

Pole strength refers to the ability of a pole to exert a magnetic force.

Magnetic Dipole Moment

The **magnetic dipole moment (M)** is given by:

\( M = m \times 2l \)

Where:

  • \( m \) = Pole strength
  • \( 2l \) = Distance between poles

Special Cases for Pole Strength & Magnetic Dipole Moment

✅ Case 1: Bar Magnet Cut into Two Equal Halves Transversely

When a bar magnet is cut perpendicular to its length:

  • The **pole strength remains the same**.
  • The **magnetic length becomes half**.
  • The new magnetic moment:
    \( M' = \frac{M}{2} \)
✅ Case 2: Bar Magnet Cut into Two Equal Halves Longitudinally

When a bar magnet is cut parallel to its length:

  • The **pole strength is halved**.
  • The **magnetic length remains the same**.
  • The new magnetic moment:
    \( M' = \frac{M}{2} \)
✅ Case 3: Magnet Placed in a Uniform Magnetic Field

The torque (\( \tau \)) acting on a magnetic dipole in a uniform magnetic field is given by:

\( \tau = MB \sin \theta \)

Where:

  • \( M \) = Magnetic dipole moment
  • \( B \) = Magnetic field strength
  • \( \theta \) = Angle between \( M \) and \( B \)

Magnetic Field Due to a Magnetic Dipole on Axial Line

For a magnetic dipole at a distance \( r \) on its axial line, the magnetic field is given by:

\( B_{\text{axial}} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{2M}{r^3} \)

Where:

  • \( \mu_0 \) = Permeability of free space
  • \( M \) = Magnetic dipole moment
  • \( r \) = Distance from the dipole

Magnetic Field Due to a Magnetic Dipole on Equatorial Line

For a magnetic dipole at a distance \( r \) on its equatorial line, the magnetic field is given by:

\( B_{\text{equatorial}} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{M}{r^3} \)

Where:

  • \( \mu_0 \) = Permeability of free space
  • \( M \) = Magnetic dipole moment
  • \( r \) = Distance from the dipole

Torque Acting on a Magnetic Dipole

When a magnetic dipole is placed in a uniform magnetic field, it experiences a torque given by:

\( \tau = MB \sin \theta \)

Where:

  • \( M \) = Magnetic dipole moment
  • \( B \) = Magnetic field strength
  • \( \theta \) = Angle between \( M \) and \( B \)

Potential Energy of a Magnetic Dipole in Uniform Magnetic Field

The potential energy of a magnetic dipole in a uniform magnetic field is given by:

\( U = -MB \cos \theta \)

Where:

  • \( M \) = Magnetic dipole moment
  • \( B \) = Magnetic field strength
  • \( \theta \) = Angle between \( M \) and \( B \)

Coulomb’s Law

Statement: The force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

\( F = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \cdot \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \)

Where:

  • \( q_1, q_2 \) = Magnitudes of the two charges
  • \( r \) = Distance between the charges
  • \( \epsilon_0 \) = Permittivity of free space (\( 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \) C²/N·m²)

Magnetic Moment of a Current-Carrying Loop

The magnetic moment of a current-carrying loop is given by:

\( M = I A \)

Where:

  • \( I \) = Current flowing through the loop
  • \( A \) = Area enclosed by the loop

It acts perpendicular to the plane of the loop, following the right-hand rule.

Magnetic Moment of an Atom

In an atom, electrons revolve around the nucleus, creating a magnetic moment.

The total magnetic moment of an atom is given by:

\( M = \sqrt{l(l+1)} \cdot \mu_B \)

Where:

  • \( l \) = Orbital quantum number
  • \( \mu_B \) = Bohr magneton (\( 9.27 \times 10^{-24} \) A·m²)

Magnetic Dipole Moment of a Revolving Electron

An electron moving in a circular orbit behaves as a tiny current loop with a magnetic dipole moment given by:

\( M = \frac{e v r}{2} \)

Using Bohr’s model, it can also be expressed as:

\( M = \frac{n e h}{4 \pi m} \)

Where:

  • \( e \) = Charge of an electron (\( 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \) C)
  • \( v \) = Electron’s velocity
  • \( r \) = Radius of the orbit
  • \( n \) = Principal quantum number
  • \( h \) = Planck’s constant (\( 6.626 \times 10^{-34} \) Js)
  • \( m \) = Mass of an electron (\( 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \) kg)

Oscillations of a Freely Suspended Magnet

When a freely suspended bar magnet is displaced from its equilibrium position, it oscillates about the vertical axis due to the torque exerted by Earth’s magnetic field.

The time period of oscillation is given by:

\( T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{I}{MB}} \)

Where:

  • \( I \) = Moment of inertia of the magnet
  • \( M \) = Magnetic dipole moment
  • \( B \) = Horizontal component of Earth's magnetic field

Gauss’s Law in Magnetism

Gauss’s law states that the total magnetic flux through a closed surface is always zero.

\( \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0 \)

Since magnetic monopoles do not exist, the net number of magnetic field lines entering a closed surface is equal to those leaving it.

Earth’s Magnetism

Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet with a magnetic field generated by currents in its molten outer core.

✅ Key Features:
  • The Earth’s magnetic field is tilted relative to its rotational axis.
  • Magnetic poles are not the same as geographic poles.
  • Intensity of the magnetic field varies with location.

Magnetic & Geographic Meridians

Geographic Meridian: A vertical plane passing through the geographic north and south poles.

Magnetic Meridian: A vertical plane passing through the magnetic north and south poles.

The angle between these two meridians is known as the magnetic declination.

Elements of Earth’s Magnetism

✅ 1) Magnetic Declination

The angle between the geographic north and the magnetic north.

\( D = \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{B_y}{B_x}\right) \)

✅ 2) Magnetic Inclination (Magnetic Dip)

The angle that Earth's magnetic field makes with the horizontal plane at a given location.

\( \tan I = \frac{B_V}{B_H} \)

✅ 3) Horizontal & Vertical Components of Earth’s Magnetic Field

Earth’s magnetic field (\( B \)) can be divided into:

  • Horizontal Component: \( B_H = B \cos I \)
  • Vertical Component: \( B_V = B \sin I \)

Neutral Points

Neutral points are locations where the magnetic field due to an external magnet cancels out the Earth’s magnetic field, resulting in zero net magnetic field.

✅ Key Points:
  • Found when a bar magnet is placed in a uniform magnetic field.
  • At these points, the magnetic field of the magnet and Earth’s field are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.
  • Commonly observed when the magnet is placed perpendicular to the horizontal component of Earth's magnetic field.

Magnetic Permeability

Magnetic permeability (\( \mu \)) is a measure of how easily a material can support the formation of a magnetic field within itself.

\( \mu = \frac{B}{H} \)

Where:

  • \( B \) = Magnetic field inside the material
  • \( H \) = Magnetizing field

✔ **High permeability** means the material allows magnetic field lines to pass through easily (e.g., iron).

✔ **Low permeability** means the material resists the magnetic field (e.g., vacuum, air).

Magnetic Force

The force experienced by a moving charge in a magnetic field is called the **magnetic force**.

\( F = q (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}) \)

Where:

  • \( q \) = Charge of the particle
  • \( v \) = Velocity of the particle
  • \( B \) = Magnetic field
  • \( \theta \) = Angle between \( v \) and \( B \)

✔ The force is maximum when \( \theta = 90^\circ \) (perpendicular motion) and zero when \( \theta = 0^\circ \) or \( 180^\circ \) (parallel motion).

Intensity of Magnetisation

Intensity of magnetisation (\( I \)) represents the magnetic moment per unit volume of a material.

\( I = \frac{M}{V} \)

Where:

  • \( M \) = Magnetic moment
  • \( V \) = Volume of the material

✔ **Stronger magnetisation** leads to a higher intensity of magnetisation.

Magnetic Susceptibility

Magnetic susceptibility (\( \chi \)) is a measure of how much a material gets magnetised when placed in an external magnetic field.

\( \chi = \frac{I}{H} \)

Where:

  • \( I \) = Intensity of magnetisation
  • \( H \) = Magnetising field
✅ Types of Materials:
  • Diamagnetic materials: \( \chi \) is small and negative (e.g., copper, gold).
  • Paramagnetic materials: \( \chi \) is small and positive (e.g., aluminum, platinum).
  • Ferromagnetic materials: \( \chi \) is large and positive (e.g., iron, nickel).

Classification of Magnetic Materials

✅ Types of Magnetic Materials:
  • Diamagnetic Substances: Weakly repelled by a magnetic field, small and negative susceptibility (\( \chi < 0 \)).
    Examples: Copper, Gold, Bismuth.
  • Paramagnetic Substances: Weakly attracted by a magnetic field, small and positive susceptibility (\( \chi > 0 \)).
    Examples: Aluminum, Platinum, Oxygen.
  • Ferromagnetic Substances: Strongly attracted to a magnetic field, large and positive susceptibility.
    Examples: Iron, Nickel, Cobalt.

Curie's Law in Magnetism

Curie’s Law: The magnetisation of a paramagnetic material is directly proportional to the applied magnetic field and inversely proportional to the absolute temperature.

\( M = \frac{C \cdot B}{T} \)

Where:

  • \( M \) = Magnetisation
  • \( C \) = Curie constant
  • \( B \) = Magnetic field
  • \( T \) = Absolute temperature (in Kelvin)

✔ As temperature increases, magnetisation decreases.

Hysteresis

Hysteresis: The phenomenon in which a material retains some magnetism even after the external magnetic field is removed.

✅ Key Points:
  • When a magnetic field is applied to a ferromagnetic material, it becomes magnetised.
  • When the external field is removed, the material retains some magnetism (residual magnetism).
  • The area of the hysteresis loop represents energy loss due to magnetic domain realignment.

Retentivity (Residual Magnetism)

Retentivity: The ability of a material to retain magnetism even after the external magnetic field is removed.

✅ Applications:
  • Used in permanent magnets.
  • Essential for data storage devices like hard disks.

Coercivity

Coercivity: The amount of reverse magnetic field required to completely demagnetise a material.

✅ Key Points:
  • Materials with high coercivity retain their magnetism for a long time (used in permanent magnets).
  • Materials with low coercivity lose their magnetism easily (used in temporary magnets like electromagnets).
