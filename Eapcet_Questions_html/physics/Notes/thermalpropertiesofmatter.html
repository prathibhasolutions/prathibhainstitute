Thermal Properties of Matter

Heat: Definition, Units, and Formula
Definition of Heat

Heat is a form of energy that is transferred between systems or objects with different temperatures. It flows from a higher temperature body to a lower temperature body.

Units of Heat
  • SI Unit: Joule (J)
  • Other Units:
    • Calorie (cal) → \( 1 \text{ cal} = 4.186 \text{ J} \)
    • Kilocalorie (kcal) → \( 1 \text{ kcal} = 1000 \text{ cal} \)
Temperature
Definition of Temperature

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. It determines the direction of heat flow between two objects.

Measurement of Temperature
  • Temperature is measured using thermometers.
  • Common scales used for temperature measurement:
    • Celsius (°C)
    • Fahrenheit (°F)
    • Kelvin (K)
    • Rankine (°R)
General Temperature Conversion Formula

The relationship between different temperature scales can be expressed using a single formula:

\[ \frac{C - 0}{100} = \frac{F - 32}{180} = \frac{K - 273.15}{100} \]

Where:

  • \( C \) = Temperature in Celsius
  • \( F \) = Temperature in Fahrenheit
  • \( K \) = Temperature in Kelvin
Thermometric Properties
Thermometric Properties and Formulas
Definition of Thermometric Property

A thermometric property is any physical property of a substance that varies consistently and measurably with temperature, allowing it to be used for temperature measurement.

Pressure of a Gas at Constant Volume

The pressure of a fixed mass of gas at constant volume varies linearly with temperature.

\[ P = P_0 \left( 1 + \alpha t \right) \]

  • \( P \) = Pressure at temperature \( t \)
  • \( P_0 \) = Pressure at \( 0^\circ C \)
  • \( \alpha \) = Coefficient of pressure variation
  • \( t \) = Temperature in \( ^\circ C \)
Electric Resistance of Metals

The resistance of a metal increases with temperature and follows the equation:

\[ R = R_0 \left( 1 + \alpha t \right) \]

  • \( R \) = Resistance at temperature \( t \)
  • \( R_0 \) = Resistance at \( 0^\circ C \)
  • \( \alpha \) = Temperature coefficient of resistance
  • \( t \) = Temperature in \( ^\circ C \)
Thermal Expansion of Solids
Definition

When a solid is heated, its dimensions increase due to the increase in the kinetic energy of its molecules. This phenomenon is known as thermal expansion.

Linear Expansion

Change in length:

\[ \Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T \]

Where:

  • \(\alpha\) = Coefficient of linear expansion
  • \(L_0\) = Initial length
  • \(\Delta L\) = Change in length
  • \(\Delta T\) = Change in temperature
Areal Expansion

Change in area:

\[ \Delta A = \beta A_0 \Delta T \]

Where:

  • \(\beta = 2\alpha\) = Coefficient of areal expansion
  • \(A_0\) = Initial area
  • \(\Delta A\) = Change in area
  • \(\Delta T\) = Change in temperature
Cubical Expansion

Change in volume:

\[ \Delta V = \gamma V_0 \Delta T \]

Where:

  • \(\gamma = 3\alpha\) = Coefficient of cubical expansion
  • \(V_0\) = Initial volume
  • \(\Delta V\) = Change in volume
  • \(\Delta T\) = Change in temperature
Ratio of Expansion Coefficients

\[ \alpha : \beta : \gamma = 1 : 2 : 3 \]

\[ \beta = 2\alpha, \quad \gamma = 3\alpha \]

Specific Heat and Its Types
Definition

The amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of a substance by 1°C (or 1 K) is called specific heat capacity.

Specific Heat at Constant Volume (\(C_v\))

Heat required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of gas at constant volume:

\[ C_v = \left( \frac{dQ}{dT} \right)_V \]

Where:

  • \(C_v\) = Specific heat at constant volume
  • \(dQ\) = Small amount of heat added
  • \(dT\) = Small change in temperature
Specific Heat at Constant Pressure (\(C_p\))

Heat required to raise the temperature of a unit mass of gas at constant pressure:

\[ C_p = \left( \frac{dQ}{dT} \right)_P \]

Where:

  • \(C_p\) = Specific heat at constant pressure
  • \(dQ\) = Small amount of heat added
  • \(dT\) = Small change in temperature
Ratio of Specific Heats

\[ \gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v} \]

Where:

  • \(\gamma\) = Adiabatic index
  • \(C_p\) = Specific heat at constant pressure
  • \(C_v\) = Specific heat at constant volume
Relation Between Specific Heats

\[ C_p - C_v = R \]

Where:

  • \(C_p\) = Specific heat at constant pressure
  • \(C_v\) = Specific heat at constant volume
  • \(R\) = Universal gas constant
Amount of Energy Required

\[ Q = mc\Delta T \]

Where:

  • \(Q\) = Heat energy
  • \(m\) = Mass of the substance
  • \(c\) = Specific heat capacity
  • \(\Delta T\) = Change in temperature
Adiabatic Index (\(\gamma\)) for Different Gases
Type of Gas \(\gamma = C_p/C_v\)
Monoatomic Gas (e.g. He, Ne, Ar) \(\frac{5}{3} \approx 1.67\)
Diatomic Gas (e.g. N₂, O₂) \(\frac{7}{5} \approx 1.4\)
Polyatomic Gas (e.g. CO₂, CH₄) \(\approx 1.33\)
Heat Capacity
Definition

The total amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a given body by 1°C (or 1 K) is called Heat Capacity (\(C\)).

Formula

\[ C = \frac{dQ}{dT} \]

Where:

  • \(C\) = Heat Capacity
  • \(dQ\) = Small amount of heat added
  • \(dT\) = Small change in temperature
Latent Heat
Definition

Latent heat is the amount of heat energy required to change the state of a substance without changing its temperature.

Formula

\[ Q = mL \]

Where:

  • \(Q\) = Heat energy
  • \(m\) = Mass of the substance
  • \(L\) = Latent heat of transformation
Latent Heat of Fusion (\(L_f\))

Heat required to convert 1 kg of a solid into liquid at constant temperature.

Latent Heat of Vaporization (\(L_v\))

Heat required to convert 1 kg of a liquid into gas at constant temperature.

Calorimetry
Principle of Calorimetry

According to the principle of calorimetry, when two bodies at different temperatures are brought into thermal contact, the heat lost by the hotter body is equal to the heat gained by the colder body, assuming no heat loss to the surroundings.

Heat lost = Heat gained

Formulae

\[ m_1 c_1 (T_1 - T_f) = m_2 c_2 (T_f - T_2) \]

Where:

  • \(m_1, m_2\) = Mass of substances
  • \(c_1, c_2\) = Specific heat capacities
  • \(T_1, T_2\) = Initial temperatures
  • \(T_f\) = Final equilibrium temperature
Special Cases
Condition Final Temperature (\(T_f\)) Formula
Same Material (\(c_1 = c_2 = c\)) \[ T_f = \frac{m_1 T_1 + m_2 T_2}{m_1 + m_2} \]
Same Mass (\(m_1 = m_2 = m\)) \[ T_f = \frac{c_1 T_1 + c_2 T_2}{c_1 + c_2} \]
Same Mass & Material (\(m_1 = m_2 = m, c_1 = c_2 = c\)) \[ T_f = \frac{T_1 + T_2}{2} \]
Types of Heat Transmission
Conduction

The process by which heat is transferred through a solid without any movement of the particles from one place to another.

Convection

The process of heat transfer in fluids (liquids and gases) due to the actual movement of particles from one region to another.

Radiation

The transfer of heat in the form of electromagnetic waves without the need for a medium.

Conduction of Heat in a Slab
Heat Conduction Formula

\[ Q = \frac{kA(T_1 - T_2)}{d} \]

Where:

  • \( Q \) = Heat transferred per second
  • \( k \) = Thermal conductivity
  • \( A \) = Cross-sectional area
  • \( T_1, T_2 \) = Temperatures of the two sides
  • \( d \) = Thickness of the slab
Slabs in Series

Equivalent thermal resistance:

\[ \frac{1}{K_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{d_1}{k_1} + \frac{d_2}{k_2} + \frac{d_3}{k_3} + \dots \]

Slabs in Parallel

Equivalent thermal conductivity:

\[ K_{\text{eq}} = \frac{k_1 A_1 + k_2 A_2 + k_3 A_3 + \dots}{A_{\text{total}}} \]

Conduction of Heat in a Slab
Heat Conduction Formula

\[ Q = \frac{kA(T_1 - T_2)}{d} \]

Where:

  • \( Q \) = Heat transferred per second
  • \( k \) = Thermal conductivity
  • \( A \) = Cross-sectional area
  • \( T_1, T_2 \) = Temperatures of the two sides
  • \( d \) = Thickness of the slab
Thermal Resistance

Thermal resistance is the opposition to heat flow and is given by:

\[ R = \frac{d}{kA} \]

Where:

  • \( R \) = Thermal resistance
  • \( d \) = Thickness of the slab
  • \( k \) = Thermal conductivity
  • \( A \) = Cross-sectional area
Slabs in Series

Equivalent thermal resistance:

\[ R_{\text{eq}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots \]

\[ R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{d_1}{k_1 A} + \frac{d_2}{k_2 A} + \frac{d_3}{k_3 A} + \dots \]

Slabs in Parallel

Equivalent thermal resistance:

\[ \frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \dots \]

\[ \frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{k_1 A_1}{d_1} + \frac{k_2 A_2}{d_2} + \frac{k_3 A_3}{d_3} + \dots \]

Reflectance, Absorptance, and Transmittance
Reflectance (\( R \))

Fraction of incident radiation that is reflected by a surface.

\[ R = \frac{I_r}{I_i} \]

Where:

  • \( I_r \) = Reflected intensity
  • \( I_i \) = Incident intensity
Absorptance (\( A \))

Fraction of incident radiation that is absorbed by a surface.

\[ A = \frac{I_a}{I_i} \]

Where:

  • \( I_a \) = Absorbed intensity
  • \( I_i \) = Incident intensity
Transmittance (\( T \))

Fraction of incident radiation that passes through a surface.

\[ T = \frac{I_t}{I_i} \]

Where:

  • \( I_t \) = Transmitted intensity
  • \( I_i \) = Incident intensity
Energy Conservation Relation

Since all incident energy is either reflected, absorbed, or transmitted:

\[ R + A + T = 1 \]

Heat Radiation Laws & Concepts
Emissive Power (\( E \))

The total thermal radiation emitted per unit area per unit time by a surface.

\[ E = \sigma T^4 \]

Where:

  • \( \sigma \) = Stefan-Boltzmann constant
  • \( T \) = Absolute temperature (Kelvin)
Emissivity (\( e \))

The ratio of the radiation emitted by a surface to the radiation emitted by a black body at the same temperature.

\[ e = \frac{E}{E_b} \]

Where:

  • \( E \) = Emissive power of the surface
  • \( E_b \) = Emissive power of a black body
Perfectly Black Body

A body that absorbs all incident radiation and emits maximum possible radiation at a given temperature.

For a black body: \( e = 1 \).

Stefan’s Law

The total radiant energy emitted by a black body is proportional to the fourth power of its absolute temperature.

\[ E_b = \sigma T^4 \]

Where: \( \sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} Wm^{-2}K^{-4} \)

Newton’s Law of Cooling

The rate of cooling of a body is proportional to the temperature difference between the body and its surroundings.

\[ \frac{dT}{dt} = -k (T - T_s) \]

Where:

  • \( k \) = Cooling constant
  • \( T \) = Body temperature
  • \( T_s \) = Surrounding temperature
Wien’s Displacement Law

The wavelength at which the maximum emission of radiation occurs is inversely proportional to the absolute temperature.

\[ \lambda_{max} T = b \]

Where: \( b = 2.898 \times 10^{-3} mK \)
