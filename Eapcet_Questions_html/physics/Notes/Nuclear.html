Nuclear Physics - The Nucleus and Related Terms

The **nucleus** is the small, dense, positively charged center of an atom, consisting of **protons and neutrons** (nucleons). It is responsible for the mass and stability of an atom.

1️⃣ Atomic Number (Z)

The **atomic number (Z)** represents the number of **protons** in the nucleus of an atom. It uniquely identifies an element.

\( Z = \text{Number of Protons} \)

✅ Example: Hydrogen (\( Z = 1 \)), Carbon (\( Z = 6 \)).

2️⃣ Mass Number (A)

The **mass number (A)** is the total number of **protons and neutrons** in the nucleus.

\( A = Z + N \)

Where:

  • \( Z \) = Number of **protons**
  • \( N \) = Number of **neutrons**

✅ Example: Oxygen-16 (\( Z = 8, N = 8, A = 16 \)).

3️⃣ Nuclear Size

The **radius of a nucleus** depends on the mass number and is given by:

\( R = R_0 A^{1/3} \)

Where:

  • \( R_0 \) = **1.2 fm** (Fermi meters, \( 1 \text{ fm} = 10^{-15} \text{ m} \))
  • \( A \) = Mass number

✅ Larger nuclei have **greater radii**.

4️⃣ Nuclear Density

The **density of a nucleus** is nearly constant for all elements and is given by:

\( \rho = \frac{\text{Mass of nucleus}}{\text{Volume of nucleus}} \)

Using \( R = R_0 A^{1/3} \), the formula simplifies to:

\( \rho = \frac{3 m_N}{4\pi R_0^3} \approx 2.3 \times 10^{17} \text{ kg/m}^3 \)

✅ **Nuclear density is extremely high**, about \( 10^{14} \) times the density of solid matter.

5️⃣ Atomic Mass Unit (AMU)

The **atomic mass unit (AMU)** is a standard unit for measuring atomic and nuclear masses:

\( 1 \text{ amu} = \frac{1}{12} \text{ mass of a carbon-12 atom} \)

In kg:

\( 1 \text{ amu} = 1.6605 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg} \)

✅ Example: Mass of a proton = **1.007 amu**, mass of a neutron = **1.008 amu**.

The **nucleus** exhibits different properties based on its composition, energy, and forces. Here, we discuss **nuclear classification and energy-related concepts.**

1️⃣ Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the **same element** with the **same atomic number (Z) but different mass numbers (A)** due to different numbers of neutrons.

\( A_1 \neq A_2, \quad Z_1 = Z_2 \)

✅ Example: Hydrogen isotopes: **Protium** (\( ^1H \)), **Deuterium** (\( ^2H \)), **Tritium** (\( ^3H \)).

2️⃣ Isobars

Isobars are atoms of **different elements** with the **same mass number (A) but different atomic numbers (Z).**

\( A_1 = A_2, \quad Z_1 \neq Z_2 \)

✅ Example: **Carbon-14** (\( ^{14}C \)) and **Nitrogen-14** (\( ^{14}N \)).

3️⃣ Isotones

Isotones are nuclei that have the **same number of neutrons (N) but different atomic numbers (Z) and mass numbers (A).**

\( N_1 = N_2, \quad Z_1 \neq Z_2 \)

✅ Example: **Carbon-14** (\( ^{14}C \)) and **Oxygen-16** (\( ^{16}O \)) both have **8 neutrons**.

4️⃣ Isomers

Isomers are **nuclei with the same atomic number (Z) and mass number (A) but different energy states.**

✅ Example: **Technetium-99m** and **Technetium-99** (metastable state \( m \) means higher energy).

5️⃣ Nuclear Force

The **nuclear force** is the strong force that **binds protons and neutrons** together in the nucleus. It has the following properties:

  • **Attractive** at short distances.
  • **Repulsive** at extremely short ranges (< 0.5 fm) to prevent collapse.
  • Independent of charge (acts equally on neutrons and protons).
6️⃣ Mass Defect

The actual mass of a nucleus is **less than the sum of its individual protons and neutrons.** This difference is called **mass defect**:

\( \Delta m = Z m_p + N m_n - M \)

✅ This **lost mass** is converted into **binding energy** via **Einstein’s equation**.

7️⃣ Nuclear Binding Energy

The **energy required to break a nucleus into its individual nucleons** is called **binding energy (BE):**

\( E_B = (\Delta m) c^2 \)

✅ More binding energy means **greater nuclear stability.**

8️⃣ Packing Fraction

The **packing fraction** is a measure of how tightly nucleons are packed in a nucleus:

\( PF = \frac{\text{Mass defect per nucleon}}{\text{Mass number} (A)} \)

✅ It helps compare nuclear stability among elements.

9️⃣ Binding Energy Curve

The **binding energy per nucleon** graph shows **nuclear stability trends:**

  • **Iron-56** has the **highest binding energy**, making it the most stable element.
  • **Light nuclei (e.g., H, He) fuse to form heavier stable nuclei** (**Fusion**).
  • **Heavy nuclei (e.g., Uranium) split to form more stable nuclei** (**Fission**).

✅ This curve explains why **fusion and fission release energy.**

Nuclear Reactions & Energy

Nuclear reactions involve changes in the **nucleus of an atom**, often releasing or absorbing large amounts of energy. This energy is used in both **power generation and weapons technology.**

1️⃣ Nuclear Reaction

A nuclear reaction occurs when a **nucleus interacts** with another nucleus, particle, or radiation, leading to the transformation of one element into another.

✅ Example: **Alpha decay of Uranium-238:**

\( ^{238}_{92}U \rightarrow ^{234}_{90}Th + ^4_2He \)

2️⃣ Q-Value of Nuclear Reaction

The **Q-value** represents the net energy released or absorbed in a nuclear reaction:

\( Q = (m_{\text{initial}} - m_{\text{final}}) c^2 \)

✅ If \( Q > 0 \) → **Exothermic reaction (energy released)**.

✅ If \( Q < 0 \) → **Endothermic reaction (energy absorbed)**.

3️⃣ Nuclear Energy

**Nuclear energy** is the energy stored in an atom's nucleus, released through **fission or fusion.**

✅ **E=mc²** equation explains mass-energy conversion.

4️⃣ Nuclear Fission

In **fission**, a heavy nucleus splits into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy.

\( ^{235}_{92}U + ^1_0n \rightarrow ^{141}_{56}Ba + ^{92}_{36}Kr + 3 ^1_0n + \text{Energy} \)

✅ Used in **nuclear reactors and bombs**.

5️⃣ Nuclear Chain Reaction

A **self-sustaining reaction** where neutrons released in fission trigger more fission events.

✅ Controlled in **nuclear reactors**, uncontrolled in **nuclear bombs**.

6️⃣ Nuclear Reactor

A **nuclear reactor** controls fission reactions to generate electricity.

✅ Components of a Nuclear Reactor:

🔹 Fuel

Fissionable material like **Uranium-235** or **Plutonium-239**.

🔹 Moderator

Slows down neutrons to sustain the reaction. **Example:** Graphite, Heavy Water.

🔹 Coolant

Removes excess heat. **Example:** Water, Liquid Sodium.

🔹 Control Rods

Absorb excess neutrons to control the reaction. **Example:** Cadmium, Boron.

7️⃣ Nuclear Fusion

**Fusion** occurs when two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous energy.

\( ^2_1H + ^3_1H \rightarrow ^4_2He + ^1_0n + \text{Energy} \)

✅ **Powers the Sun and hydrogen bombs.**

✅ Requires extremely **high temperature (~10⁷ K).**

Radioactivity & Radiation Types

Definition: Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of particles or electromagnetic waves from an unstable atomic nucleus.

✅ Types of Radiations:
  • Alpha (𝛼) Radiation:
    • Consists of 2 protons and 2 neutrons (Helium nucleus).
    • High ionizing power, but low penetration (stopped by a sheet of paper).
    • Example: \( _{92}^{238}U \rightarrow _{90}^{234}Th + _{2}^{4}\alpha \)
  • Beta (𝛽) Radiation:
    • Emission of electrons (β⁻) or positrons (β⁺).
    • Moderate penetration power, can pass through paper but stopped by metal sheets.
    • Example: \( _{6}^{14}C \rightarrow _{7}^{14}N + e^- \)
  • Gamma (𝛾) Radiation:
    • High-energy electromagnetic waves (no mass, no charge).
    • High penetration power (requires lead or concrete to stop).
    • Example: \( _{60}^{27}Co \rightarrow _{60}^{28}Ni + \gamma \)

📌 1. Statement of Radioactive Decay Law

The rate of decay of a radioactive substance is directly proportional to the number of undecayed nuclei present at that instant.

\( \frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N \)

  • N: Number of undecayed nuclei at time t.
  • λ: Decay constant (probability of decay per unit time).
  • \( \frac{dN}{dt} \): Rate of decay of nuclei.

🔹 The negative sign indicates that the number of nuclei decreases over time.

📌 2. Derivation of the Decay Law

Rearranging the equation:

\( \frac{dN}{N} = -\lambda dt \)

Integrating both sides:

\( \ln N = -\lambda t + C \)

Taking exponent on both sides:

\( N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \)

✅ The number of undecayed nuclei follows an **exponential decay** pattern.

📌 3. Activity of a Radioactive Sample

The activity (A) is the rate of decay of nuclei:

\( A = -\frac{dN}{dt} = \lambda N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \)

✅ The activity also follows **exponential decay**.

📌 4. Half-Life \(T_{1/2}\)

The half-life is the time required for half of a radioactive sample to decay:

\( T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda} \)

🔹 The half-life is independent of the initial quantity.

📌 5. Mean Life (Average Life) \(\tau\)

The mean life is the average time a nucleus remains undecayed:

\( \tau = \frac{1}{\lambda} \)

✅ Mean life is always **greater than half-life**.

📌 6. Applications of Radioactive Decay Law

  • ✅ Carbon Dating: Used to determine the age of fossils by measuring Carbon-14 decay.
  • ✅ Medical Applications: Radioisotopes like Technetium-99 are used in medical imaging.
  • ✅ Nuclear Power: Uranium and Plutonium undergo decay in nuclear reactors to produce energy.
  • ✅ Radiotherapy: Gamma radiation is used in cancer treatment.
