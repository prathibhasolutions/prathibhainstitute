Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the branch of physics that deals with the study of heat, energy, and their transformations into different forms of work.

Key Concepts
  • System: The part of the universe under study.
  • Surroundings: Everything outside the system.
  • Boundary: The surface separating the system from surroundings.
  • Energy Transfer: Occurs through heat and work interactions.
Thermodynamic System & Its Types

Thermodynamic System: A thermodynamic system is a specific quantity of matter or a region in space chosen for analysis. It is separated from the surroundings by a boundary.

Open System

Both mass and energy can be exchanged with the surroundings.

Example: Boiling water in an open pot.

Closed System

Only energy can be exchanged, but not mass.

Example: A sealed container with hot coffee.

Isolated System

Neither mass nor energy is exchanged with surroundings.

Example: A thermos flask with hot water.

Thermodynamic Variables & Their Types

Thermodynamic Variables: These are the physical quantities used to describe the state of a thermodynamic system.

Extensive Variables

Depend on the size or amount of substance in the system.

Examples:

  • Mass (\(m\))
  • Volume (\(V\))
  • Internal Energy (\(U\))
  • Entropy (\(S\))
Intensive Variables

Do not depend on the size or amount of substance.

Examples:

  • Pressure (\(P\))
  • Temperature (\(T\))
  • Density (\(\rho\))
  • Specific Heat (\(c\))
Thermal Equilibrium & Adiabatic Wall
Thermal Equilibrium

A system is in thermal equilibrium when there is no net flow of heat between its parts or with the surroundings, meaning all parts of the system have the same temperature.

Key Concept: If two bodies are in thermal equilibrium with a third body, they are also in thermal equilibrium with each other (Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics).

Adiabatic Wall

An adiabatic wall is a type of boundary that does not allow heat to pass through it.

Properties:

  • No heat transfer occurs across an adiabatic wall.
  • It ensures that the system remains thermally isolated.
  • Commonly used in thermodynamic systems to study energy changes without heat exchange.
Work Done in Thermodynamics
Definition

In thermodynamics, work is the energy transferred by a system when it undergoes a change in volume against an external pressure.

Basic Formula

\[ dW = P \, dV \]

Where:

  • \(dW\) = Infinitesimal work done
  • \(P\) = External pressure
  • \(dV\) = Change in volume
Sign Convention
  • Positive Work (\( W > 0 \)): When the system expands (\( dV > 0 \)), work is done by the system.
  • Negative Work (\( W < 0 \)): When the system contracts (\( dV < 0 \)), work is done on the system.
Internal Energy
Definition

Internal energy (U) is the total energy possessed by a system due to the kinetic and potential energies of its molecules. It is a state function and depends only on the state of the system.

Key Points
  • It is an extensive property, meaning it depends on the mass of the system.
  • It increases when heat is added and decreases when work is done by the system.
  • For an isolated system, internal energy remains constant.
Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics
Definition

The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics states that if two systems (A and B) are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system (C), then they are also in thermal equilibrium with each other.

Key Concept

This law establishes the concept of temperature as a fundamental and measurable property of matter.

First Law of Thermodynamics
Definition

The First Law of Thermodynamics states that the total energy of an isolated system remains constant. It is a statement of the conservation of energy, which means that energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

Mathematical Form

\[ \Delta U = Q - W \]

Where:

  • \(\Delta U\) = Change in internal energy
  • \(Q\) = Heat supplied to the system
  • \(W\) = Work done by the system
Sign Convention
  • \(Q\) is positive if heat is added to the system.
  • \(Q\) is negative if heat is lost from the system.
  • \(W\) is positive if work is done by the system.
  • \(W\) is negative if work is done on the system.
Thermodynamic Processes
Quasi-Static Process

A process that occurs infinitely slowly so that the system remains in equilibrium at all times.

Isothermal Process (\(T = \text{constant}\))

In an isothermal process, the temperature remains constant, meaning \(\Delta U = 0\).

Equations:

  • Work Done: \( W = nRT \ln \frac{V_f}{V_i} \)
  • Heat Added: \( Q = W \)
  • Internal Energy Change: \( \Delta U = 0 \)
Adiabatic Process (\(Q = 0\))

In an adiabatic process, no heat is exchanged with the surroundings.

Equations:

  • Work Done: \( W = \frac{nR(T_i - T_f)}{\gamma - 1} \)
  • Internal Energy Change: \( \Delta U = -W \)

Condition: \( PV^\gamma = \text{constant} \)

Isobaric Process (\(P = \text{constant}\))

In an isobaric process, pressure remains constant.

Equations:

  • Work Done: \( W = P(V_f - V_i) \)
  • Heat Added: \( Q = n C_p \Delta T \)
  • Internal Energy Change: \( \Delta U = n C_v \Delta T \)
Isochoric Process (\(V = \text{constant}\))

In an isochoric process, volume remains constant, meaning \( W = 0 \).

Equations:

  • Heat Added: \( Q = n C_v \Delta T \)
  • Internal Energy Change: \( \Delta U = Q \)
Cyclic Process

In a cyclic process, the system returns to its initial state after undergoing a series of thermodynamic changes.

Key Equation:

  • Net Work Done: \( W = Q_{\text{in}} - Q_{\text{out}} \)
  • Change in Internal Energy: \( \Delta U = 0 \)

Graphical Representation:

The work done in a cyclic process is equal to the area enclosed by the curve on a PV diagram.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that the entropy of an isolated system always increases over time and that energy transfers have a direction.

Kelvin-Planck Statement

"It is impossible to construct a device that operates in a cyclic process and converts all heat energy into work without any loss."

Key Implication: No heat engine can be 100% efficient.

Clausius Statement

"It is impossible for a system to transfer heat from a colder body to a hotter body without the input of external work."

Key Implication: Heat cannot flow spontaneously from a colder body to a hotter body.

Reversible and Irreversible Processes
Reversible Process

A thermodynamic process that can be reversed by an infinitesimal change in conditions without any loss of energy is called a Reversible Process.

Characteristics:

  • Occurs infinitely slowly.
  • No loss of heat due to friction or dissipation.
  • System remains in equilibrium at all times.
Irreversible Process

A process that cannot be reversed exactly to restore the system and surroundings to their initial states is called an Irreversible Process.

Characteristics:

  • Occurs rapidly and spontaneously.
  • Involves energy dissipation (e.g., friction, heat loss).
  • System passes through non-equilibrium states.
Entropy and Change in Entropy
Entropy (\(S\))

Entropy is a thermodynamic property that measures the degree of disorder or randomness in a system.

Key Points:

  • Denoted by \(S\).
  • Greater disorder â†’ Higher entropy.
  • Entropy of the universe always increases (Second Law of Thermodynamics).
Change in Entropy (\(\Delta S\))

The change in entropy for a reversible process is given by:

\[ \Delta S = \int \frac{dQ_{rev}}{T} \]

For a finite change:

\[ \Delta S = \frac{Q_{rev}}{T} \]

Where:

  • \(\Delta S\) = Change in entropy
  • \(Q_{rev}\) = Heat absorbed or released in a reversible process
  • \(T\) = Absolute temperature (in Kelvin)
Heat Engine & Its Formulae
What is a Heat Engine?

A heat engine is a device that converts heat energy into mechanical work by undergoing a cyclic process.

Working:

  • Absorbs heat from a hot reservoir (\(Q_1\))
  • Converts part of heat into work (\(W\))
  • Rejects remaining heat to a cold reservoir (\(Q_2\))
  • Operates in a cyclic manner
Heat Engine Cycle

\[ W = Q_1 - Q_2 \]

Where:

  • \(W\) = Work output
  • \(Q_1\) = Heat absorbed from the hot source
  • \(Q_2\) = Heat rejected to the cold sink
Efficiency of a Heat Engine

Efficiency (\(\eta\)) is the ratio of work done to heat absorbed:

\[ \eta = \frac{W}{Q_1} = \frac{Q_1 - Q_2}{Q_1} \]

In terms of temperature (for a Carnot engine):

\[ \eta = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1} \]

Where:

  • \(T_1\) = Temperature of the hot reservoir
  • \(T_2\) = Temperature of the cold reservoir
Carnot Cycle

The Carnot Cycle is an ideal reversible cycle consisting of two isothermal and two adiabatic processes.

Processes Involved:

  • Isothermal Expansion (\(T_1\))
  • Adiabatic Expansion
  • Isothermal Compression (\(T_2\))
  • Adiabatic Compression

Efficiency of Carnot Engine:

\[ \eta = 1 - \frac{T_2}{T_1} \]

Where:

  • \(T_1\) = Temperature of hot reservoir
  • \(T_2\) = Temperature of cold reservoir
Refrigerator

A refrigerator is a device that transfers heat from a lower temperature region (cold reservoir) to a higher temperature region (hot reservoir).

Coefficient of Performance (COP) of a Refrigerator:

\[ \text{COP}_R = \frac{Q_2}{W} = \frac{T_2}{T_1 - T_2} \]

Where:

  • \(Q_2\) = Heat extracted from the cold region
  • \(W\) = Work input
  • \(T_1\) = Hot reservoir temperature
  • \(T_2\) = Cold reservoir temperature
Heat Pump

A heat pump is a device that transfers heat from a colder area to a hotter area, primarily used for heating purposes.

Coefficient of Performance (COP) of a Heat Pump:

\[ \text{COP}_H = \frac{Q_1}{W} = \frac{T_1}{T_1 - T_2} \]

Where:

  • \(Q_1\) = Heat delivered to the hot region
  • \(W\) = Work input
  • \(T_1\) = Hot reservoir temperature
  • \(T_2\) = Cold reservoir temperature

Relation between COP of Refrigerator & Heat Pump:

\[ \text{COP}_H = \text{COP}_R + 1 \]
