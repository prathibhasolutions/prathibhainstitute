Kinetic Theory of Gases

The Kinetic Theory of Gases explains the behavior of gases by considering their molecular composition and motion.

It provides a microscopic understanding of macroscopic properties like pressure, temperature, and volume.

Assumptions of Kinetic Theory
  • A gas consists of a large number of identical molecules in continuous random motion.
  • The size of gas molecules is negligible compared to the distance between them.
  • Molecules undergo perfectly elastic collisions with each other and the container walls.
  • There are no intermolecular forces except during collisions.
  • The average kinetic energy of molecules is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.
  • The time spent in collisions is negligible compared to the time spent in free motion.
  • The pressure exerted by the gas is due to the collisions of molecules with the container walls.
  • The motion of gas molecules obeys Newton’s laws of motion.
  • The distribution of molecular speeds follows the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.
  • All molecules do not have the same speed; instead, their speeds vary over a range.
Gas Laws

Gas laws describe the relationships between pressure, volume, temperature, and the number of gas molecules.

Boyle’s Law

Statement: At constant temperature, the pressure of a gas is inversely proportional to its volume.

\[ P \propto \frac{1}{V} \quad \text{or} \quad P_1V_1 = P_2V_2 \]

Charles’ Law

Statement: At constant pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

\[ V \propto T \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2} \]

Gay-Lussac’s Law

Statement: At constant volume, the pressure of a gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature.

\[ P \propto T \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{P_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2}{T_2} \]

Avogadro’s Law

Statement: At constant temperature and pressure, the volume of a gas is directly proportional to the number of molecules.

\[ V \propto n \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{V_1}{n_1} = \frac{V_2}{n_2} \]

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures

Statement: The total pressure exerted by a mixture of non-reacting gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of individual gases.

\[ P_{\text{total}} = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + \dots \]

Ideal Gas Equation

Equation: The relationship between pressure, volume, temperature, and the number of moles of an ideal gas is given by:

\[ PV = nRT \]

Where:

  • \( P \) = Pressure
  • \( V \) = Volume
  • \( n \) = Number of moles
  • \( R \) = Universal gas constant
  • \( T \) = Temperature

Values of \( R \):

  • \( R = 8.314 \, J \cdot mol^{-1} \cdot K^{-1} \)
  • \( R = 0.0821 \, L \cdot atm \cdot mol^{-1} \cdot K^{-1} \)
  • \( R = 1.987 \, cal \cdot mol^{-1} \cdot K^{-1} \)
Real Gases and Van der Waals Equation

Real Gases: Deviate from ideal behavior due to intermolecular forces and finite molecular volume. Deviations are significant at high pressures and low temperatures.

Van der Waals Equation:

\[ \left( P + \frac{a}{V^2} \right) (V - b) = RT \]

Where:

  • \( a \) = Attraction between molecules
  • \( b \) = Volume occupied by gas molecules
  • \( P \), \( V \), \( T \), and \( R \) are standard gas variables
Pressure of a Gas & Kinetic Energy of Gas Molecules
Pressure of a Gas

Equation for Pressure:

\[ P = \frac{1}{3} \frac{mN}{V} \bar{v^2} \]

Where:

  • \( P \) = Pressure
  • \( m \) = Mass of one molecule
  • \( N \) = Number of molecules
  • \( V \) = Volume of gas
  • \( \bar{v^2} \) = Mean square velocity of molecules
Kinetic Energy of a Gas Molecule

Expression for Kinetic Energy:

\[ E_k = \frac{3}{2} k_B T \]

Where:

  • \( E_k \) = Average kinetic energy of a gas molecule
  • \( k_B \) = Boltzmann constant \( (1.38 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{J/K}) \)
  • \( T \) = Temperature (in Kelvin)
Pressure in Terms of Density

Equation:

\[ P = \frac{1}{3} \rho \bar{v^2} \]

Where:

  • \( \rho \) = Density of the gas
  • \( \bar{v^2} \) = Mean square velocity of molecules
Relation Between Kinetic Energy & Pressure

Equation:

\[ P V = \frac{2}{3} N E_k \]

Where:

  • \( P \) = Pressure
  • \( V \) = Volume
  • \( N \) = Number of molecules
  • \( E_k \) = Average kinetic energy
Speed of Gas Molecules
Root Mean Square (RMS) Speed

Equation:

\[ v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}} \]

Where:

  • \( v_{\text{rms}} \) = Root mean square speed
  • \( R \) = Universal gas constant
  • \( T \) = Temperature (in Kelvin)
  • \( M \) = Molar mass of the gas
Average Speed

Equation:

\[ v_{\text{avg}} = \sqrt{\frac{8RT}{\pi M}} \]

Where:

  • \( v_{\text{avg}} \) = Average speed of molecules
  • \( R \) = Universal gas constant
  • \( T \) = Temperature (in Kelvin)
  • \( M \) = Molar mass of the gas
Most Probable Speed

Equation:

\[ v_{\text{mp}} = \sqrt{\frac{2RT}{M}} \]

Where:

  • \( v_{\text{mp}} \) = Most probable speed
  • \( R \) = Universal gas constant
  • \( T \) = Temperature (in Kelvin)
  • \( M \) = Molar mass of the gas
Comparison of Different Speeds

Ratio of Speeds:

\[ v_{\text{mp}} : v_{\text{avg}} : v_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{2} : \sqrt{\frac{8}{\pi}} : \sqrt{3} \approx 1 : 1.128 : 1.225 \]

Degrees of Freedom

The degrees of freedom of a gas molecule refer to the independent ways in which it can store energy through motion.

Type of Molecule Translational Rotational Vibrational Total Degrees of Freedom
Monoatomic 3 0 0 3
Diatomic (Rigid) 3 2 0 5
Diatomic (Non-Rigid) 3 2 1 6
Polyatomic (Non-Linear) 3 3 Vibrational Modes \(3N\)
Specific Heat and Degree of Freedom

\[ C_v = \frac{f}{2} R, \quad C_p = C_v + R \]

\[ \gamma = \frac{C_p}{C_v} = 1+\frac{2}{f} \]

Mean Free Path

The mean free path (\(\lambda\)) is the average distance a gas molecule travels before colliding with another molecule.

Formula for Mean Free Path

\[ \lambda = \frac{k_B T}{\sqrt{2} \pi d^2 P} \]

Where:

  • \(\lambda\) = Mean free path
  • \(k_B\) = Boltzmann constant
  • \(T\) = Absolute temperature (K)
  • \(d\) = Diameter of the gas molecule
  • \(P\) = Pressure of the gas
Dependence of Mean Free Path
  • \(\lambda\) is inversely proportional to pressure (\(P\)).
  • \(\lambda\) increases with temperature (\(T\)).
  • \(\lambda\) is inversely proportional to molecular diameter (\(d\)).
Critical Temperature, Pressure, and Volume

The critical point of a gas is defined by the critical temperature, pressure, and volume, beyond which the gas cannot be liquefied by pressure alone.

Critical Temperature (\(T_c\))

The highest temperature at which a gas can be liquefied by applying pressure.

\[ T_c = \frac{8a}{27Rb} \]

Critical Pressure (\(P_c\))

The minimum pressure required to liquefy a gas at its critical temperature.

\[ P_c = \frac{a}{27b^2} \]

Critical Volume (\(V_c\))

The volume occupied by one mole of gas at the critical temperature and pressure.

\[ V_c = 3b \]

Critical Constants Relation

\[ P_c V_c = \frac{R T_c}{8} \]

All critical constants are related through the Van der Waals equation.
