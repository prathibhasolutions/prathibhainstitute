Mechanical Properties of Solids

Deforming Force

Definition: A force that, when applied to a body, changes its shape or size is called a Deforming Force. If the body regains its original shape after removing the force, it is said to be elastic.

Elasticity

Definition: Elasticity is the property of a material by virtue of which it regains its original shape and size after the removal of a deforming force.

Example: Rubber, steel, and copper exhibit elasticity.

Elastic Limit

Definition: The maximum stress a material can withstand without undergoing permanent deformation is called the Elastic Limit. Beyond this limit, the body will not return to its original shape.

Perfectly Elastic Bodies

Definition: A body that completely regains its original shape and size after the removal of a deforming force is called a Perfectly Elastic Body.

Example: Quartz and Phosphor Bronze are considered nearly perfectly elastic materials.

Plastic Bodies

Definition: A body that does not regain its original shape and size after the removal of a deforming force is called a Plastic Body.

Example: Clay and lead are examples of plastic materials.

Stress

Definition: Stress is the internal restoring force per unit area that develops within a material when it is subjected to an external force.

General Formula:

\[ \text{Stress} = \frac{\text{Force (F)}}{\text{Area (A)}} \]

SI Unit: Pascal (Pa) or \( N/m^2 \)

Types of Stress:

  • Tensile Stress: Occurs when a body is stretched, increasing its length.
  • Compressive Stress: Occurs when a body is compressed, decreasing its length.
  • Shear (Tangential) Stress: Occurs when a body is subjected to parallel forces in opposite directions, causing angular deformation.
  • Volumetric Stress: Occurs when a uniform force acts on all sides of a body, causing a change in volume without a change in shape.

Formulas:

\[ \text{Tensile/Compressive Stress} = \frac{F}{A} \]

\[ \text{Shear Stress} = \frac{F}{A} \]

\[ \text{Volumetric Stress} = \frac{\Delta P}{\text{Original Volume (V)}} \]

Where:

  • \( F \) = Applied Force
  • \( A \) = Cross-sectional Area
  • \( \Delta P \) = Change in Pressure
  • \( V \) = Initial Volume of the body

Strain

Definition: Strain is the measure of deformation in a material due to applied stress. It is a dimensionless quantity, representing the ratio of change in dimension to the original dimension.

General Formula:

\[ \text{Strain} = \frac{\text{Change in Dimension}}{\text{Original Dimension}} \]

Types of Strain:

  • Longitudinal (Tensile/Compressive) Strain: Change in length due to applied tensile or compressive stress.
  • Shear Strain: Angular deformation caused by shear stress.
  • Volumetric Strain: Change in volume due to applied volumetric stress.

Formulas:

\[ \text{Longitudinal Strain} = \frac{\Delta L}{L} \]

\[ \text{Shear Strain} = \tan\theta \]

\[ \text{Volumetric Strain} = \frac{\Delta V}{V} \]

Where:

  • \( \Delta L \) = Change in length
  • \( L \) = Original length
  • \( \theta \) = Shear angle
  • \( \Delta V \) = Change in volume
  • \( V \) = Original volume

Hooke’s Law & Elastic Moduli

Hooke’s Law: Hooke’s Law states that within the elastic limit of a material, the stress applied is directly proportional to the strain produced.

Mathematical Form:

\[ \text{Stress} \propto \text{Strain} \]

\[ \text{Stress} = E \times \text{Strain} \]

Where:

  • \( E \) = Elastic Modulus (Modulus of Elasticity)

Elastic Moduli: The Elastic Modulus is the ratio of stress to strain and determines the stiffness of a material.

Young’s Modulus (Y)

Definition: It measures the elasticity of a material in tension or compression.

\[ Y = \frac{\text{Longitudinal Stress}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} \]

\[ Y = \frac{F L}{A \Delta L} \]

Shear Modulus (G)

Definition: It measures the elasticity of a material under shear stress.

\[ G = \frac{\text{Shear Stress}}{\text{Shear Strain}} \]

\[ G = \frac{F}{A \tan\theta} \]

Bulk Modulus (B)

Definition: It measures the elasticity of a material under uniform pressure.

\[ B = \frac{\text{Volumetric Stress}}{\text{Volumetric Strain}} \]

\[ B = \frac{\Delta P}{\frac{\Delta V}{V}} \]

Poisson’s Ratio (σ)

Definition: Poisson’s Ratio is the ratio of lateral strain to longitudinal strain.

\[ \sigma = \frac{\text{Lateral Strain}}{\text{Longitudinal Strain}} \]

Range:

\[ -1 \leq \sigma \leq 0.5 \]

For most materials, Poisson’s Ratio lies between **0.2 to 0.4**.

Compressibility (β)

Definition: Compressibility is the reciprocal of Bulk Modulus and measures how easily a material can be compressed.

\[ K = \frac{1}{B} \]

SI Unit: \( Pa^{-1} \) or \( m^2/N \)

Relations Between Elastic Moduli

Elastic moduli are interrelated through Poisson’s Ratio (σ). The key relations are:

Relation Between Y, K, and σ

\[ Y = 3K(1 - 2\sigma) \]

Where:

  • \( Y \) = Young’s Modulus
  • \( K \) = Bulk Modulus
  • \( \sigma \) = Poisson’s Ratio
Relation Between Y, G, and σ

\[ Y = 2G(1 + \sigma) \]

Where:

  • \( Y \) = Young’s Modulus
  • \( G \) = Shear Modulus
  • \( \sigma \) = Poisson’s Ratio
Relation Between K, G, and Y

\[ \frac{1}{K} = \frac{9}{Y} + \frac{3}{G} \]

Where:

  • \( K \) = Bulk Modulus
  • \( G \) = Shear Modulus
  • \( Y \) = Young’s Modulus
Relation Between K, G, and σ

\[ K = \frac{2G(1 + \sigma)}{3(1 - 2\sigma)} \]

Where:

  • \( K \) = Bulk Modulus
  • \( G \) = Shear Modulus
  • \( \sigma \) = Poisson’s Ratio

Elastic Potential Energy in a Stretched Wire

When a wire is stretched within its elastic limit, work is done in stretching it, which is stored as Elastic Potential Energy.

Elastic Potential Energy Formula

\[ U = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Stress} \times \text{Strain} \times \text{Volume} \]

or

\[ U = \frac{1}{2} Y \left( \frac{\Delta L}{L} \right)^2 \times AL \]

Where:

  • \( U \) = Elastic Potential Energy
  • \( Y \) = Young’s Modulus
  • \( \Delta L \) = Extension in the wire
  • \( L \) = Original length of the wire
  • \( A \) = Cross-sectional area
Elastic Energy Density

\[ u = \frac{U}{\text{Volume}} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Stress} \times \text{Strain} \]

Where:

  • \( u \) = Energy Density (Energy per unit volume)
  • \( U \) = Elastic Potential Energy
  • \( \text{Stress} = \frac{F}{A} \)
  • \( \text{Strain} = \frac{\Delta L}{L} \)

Elastic Potential Energy in a Stretched Spring

When a spring is stretched or compressed, work is done against the restoring force. This work is stored as elastic potential energy in the spring.

Elastic Potential Energy Formula

\[ U = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 \]

Where:

  • \( U \) = Elastic Potential Energy
  • \( k \) = Spring Constant (Force per unit extension)
  • \( x \) = Displacement from equilibrium position
Hooke’s Law

\[ F = - k x \]

Where:

  • \( F \) = Restoring Force
  • \( k \) = Spring Constant
  • \( x \) = Displacement

Note: The negative sign indicates that the force acts in the opposite direction to displacement.

Energy in Series & Parallel Springs

For two springs in series:

\[ \frac{1}{k_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{k_1} + \frac{1}{k_2} \]

For two springs in parallel:

\[ k_{\text{eq}} = k_1 + k_2 \]

Where:

  • \( k_{\text{eq}} \) = Equivalent Spring Constant
  • \( k_1, k_2 \) = Individual Spring Constants
Work Done in Stretching a Spring

\[ W = \int_0^x F dx = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 \]

Where:

  • \( W \) = Work Done
  • \( k \) = Spring Constant
  • \( x \) = Displacement

Note: The work done in stretching or compressing a spring is equal to the elastic potential energy stored in it.

Thermal Stress

Thermal stress is generated when a material undergoes a temperature change, causing it to expand or contract. If the material is restrained, this results in stress.

Thermal Stress Formula

\[ \sigma = Y \times \alpha \times \Delta T \]

Where:

  • \( \sigma \) = Thermal Stress
  • \( Y \) = Young's Modulus
  • \( \alpha \) = Coefficient of Linear Expansion
  • \( \Delta T \) = Change in Temperature
Thermal Strain Formula

\[ \varepsilon = \alpha \times \Delta T \]

Where:

  • \( \varepsilon \) = Thermal Strain
  • \( \alpha \) = Coefficient of Linear Expansion
  • \( \Delta T \) = Change in Temperature
