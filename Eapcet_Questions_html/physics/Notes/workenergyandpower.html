Work, Energy and Power

Work and Its Formulae

Definition: Work is said to be done when a force is applied on an object and the object gets displaced in the direction of the applied force.

General Formula

\( W = F d \cos\theta \)

Where:

  • W = Work done
  • F = Force applied
  • d = Displacement
  • θ = Angle between force and displacement
When Work is Positive or Negative
  • Positive Work: When \( \theta < 90^\circ \), \( W > 0 \) (Force and displacement in the same direction).
  • Negative Work: When \( \theta > 90^\circ \), \( W < 0 \) (Force opposes displacement).
  • Zero Work: When \( \theta = 90^\circ \), \( W = 0 \) (Force is perpendicular to displacement).
Work Done by a Spring

\( W = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 \)

Where:

  • k = Spring constant
  • x = Displacement from equilibrium

Work Done When Displacement is from \( x_1 \) to \( x_2 \):

\( W = \frac{1}{2} k (x_2^2 - x_1^2) \)

Work Done by Gravity

\( W = mgh \)

If an object moves downward, work done by gravity is positive. If it moves upward, work done by gravity is negative.

Work Done by a Couple (Torque)

\( W = \tau \theta \)

Where \( \tau \) is the torque and \( \theta \) is the angular displacement.

Energy and Its Types

Definition: Energy is the capacity to do work.

Mechanical Energy

Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic energy and potential energy of a system.

\( E = K.E + P.E \)

Kinetic Energy (KE)

\( K.E = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 = \frac{p^2}{2m} \)

Where:

  • m = Mass of the object
  • v = Velocity of the object
  • p = Momentum (\( p = mv \))
Potential Energy (PE)

Potential energy is the energy stored in an object due to its position or configuration.

\( F(x) = -\frac{dU}{dx} \)

Where:

  • F(x) = Force acting at position \( x \)
  • U = Potential Energy function
Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)

\( P.E = mgh \)

Where:

  • m = Mass
  • g = Acceleration due to gravity
  • h = Height above a reference level
Elastic Potential Energy

\( P.E = \frac{1}{2} k x^2 \)

Where:

  • k = Spring constant
  • x = Deformation (compression/stretching)
Electric Potential Energy

\( U = \frac{k q_1 q_2}{r} \)

Where:

  • q₁, q₂ = Charges
  • r = Distance between charges
  • k = Coulomb’s constant
Equilibrium and Its Types

Definition: A body is said to be in equilibrium if the net force and net torque acting on it are zero.

\( \sum F = 0, \quad \sum \tau = 0 \)

Stable Equilibrium

A body is in **stable equilibrium** if, when displaced slightly, it returns to its original position.

\( U \) is minimum, \( \frac{d^2U}{dx^2} > 0 \)

Example: A ball at the bottom of a bowl.

Unstable Equilibrium

A body is in **unstable equilibrium** if, when displaced slightly, it moves further away from its original position.

\( U \) is maximum, \( \frac{d^2U}{dx^2} < 0 \)

Example: A ball balanced on top of a hill.

Neutral Equilibrium

A body is in **neutral equilibrium** if, when displaced slightly, it remains in the new position without returning or moving further away.

\( U \) is constant, \( \frac{d^2U}{dx^2} = 0 \)

Example: A ball on a flat surface.

Work-Energy Theorem & Other Forms of Energy

Work-Energy Theorem: The net work done on a body is equal to the change in its kinetic energy.

\( W = \Delta K.E = K.E_f - K.E_i \)

Other Forms of Energy
  • Mechanical Energy: Sum of kinetic and potential energy.
  • Chemical Energy: Stored in chemical bonds (e.g., fuel, food).
  • Thermal Energy: Energy due to the motion of particles (heat energy).
  • Electrical Energy: Energy due to movement of charges.
  • Radiant Energy: Energy in the form of electromagnetic waves (light, X-rays).
  • Nuclear Energy: Energy released in nuclear reactions.
Principle of Conservation of Energy

Statement: Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; it can only be converted from one form to another, but the total energy of an isolated system remains constant.

\( K.E + P.E = \text{Constant} \)

Example:

  • In a freely falling body, potential energy decreases while kinetic energy increases, but total mechanical energy remains constant.
  • In a pendulum, energy continuously transforms between kinetic and potential energy.
Power

Definition: Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.

\( P = \frac{W}{t} \) (SI Unit: Watt \( W \))

Different Forms of Power:

  • Instantaneous Power: \( P = F \cdot v \) (Force × Velocity)
  • Average Power: \( P_{\text{avg}} = \frac{W_{\text{total}}}{t} \)
  • Electrical Power: \( P = VI = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R} \)

Units:

  • SI Unit: **Watt (W) = Joule/second**
  • Other units: Horsepower (1 HP = 746 W), Kilowatt (1 kW = 1000 W)
Collisions and Their Types

Definition: A collision is an event where two or more bodies interact for a short duration and exchange energy and momentum.

Types of Collisions:

Elastic Collision

Conditions:

  • Total kinetic energy is conserved.
  • Total momentum is conserved.

\( m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 \)

\( \frac{1}{2} m_1 u_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 u_2^2 = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_2^2 \)

Example: Collision between gas molecules.

Inelastic Collision

Conditions:

  • Only total momentum is conserved.
  • Kinetic energy is not conserved (some energy is lost as heat, sound, or deformation).

Example: A car crash, where cars stick together.

Perfectly Inelastic Collision

Condition: The colliding bodies stick together after the collision.

\( m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v \)

Example: A bullet embedding into a block of wood.

Coefficient of Restitution

Definition: The coefficient of restitution (\( e \)) measures how much kinetic energy remains after a collision.

\( e = \frac{\text{Relative velocity after collision}}{\text{Relative velocity before collision}} \)

\( e = \frac{v_2 - v_1}{u_1 - u_2} \)

Values of \( e \):

  • \( e = 1 \) → Perfectly elastic collision (KE conserved).
  • \( 0 < e < 1 \) → Inelastic collision (KE not conserved).
  • \( e = 0 \) → Perfectly inelastic collision (bodies stick together).

Example: A rubber ball bouncing on the floor has \( e \approx 0.8 \).

One-Dimensional Perfectly Elastic Collision

Definition: A perfectly elastic collision is one in which both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.

Final Velocities:

\( v_1 = \frac{(m_1 - m_2) u_1 + 2 m_2 u_2}{m_1 + m_2} \)

\( v_2 = \frac{(m_2 - m_1) u_2 + 2 m_1 u_1}{m_1 + m_2} \)

Kinetic Energy Loss:

\( \Delta KE = 0 \) (Since kinetic energy is conserved)

Important Cases:

  • If \( m_1 = m_2 \), the velocities are exchanged: \( v_1 = u_2 \), \( v_2 = u_1 \).
  • If \( m_1 >> m_2 \) (heavy object collides with a small one), \( v_1 \approx u_1 \), \( v_2 \approx 2u_1 - u_2 \).
  • If \( m_1 << m_2 \) (small object collides with a heavy one), \( v_1 \approx 2u_2 - u_1 \), \( v_2 \approx u_2 \).
  • If the second body is initially at rest (\( u_2 = 0 \)), then:
    • \( v_1 = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)}{m_1 + m_2} u_1 \)
    • \( v_2 = \frac{2m_1}{m_1 + m_2} u_1 \)
One-Dimensional Perfectly Inelastic Collision

Definition: In a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects stick together after collision, and kinetic energy is not conserved.

Final Velocity:

\( v = \frac{m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2}{m_1 + m_2} \)

Kinetic Energy Loss:

\( \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2} \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} (u_1 - u_2)^2 \)

Important Points:

  • Momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not.
  • Maximum kinetic energy is lost in this type of collision.
  • After the collision, both bodies move together with a common velocity.
Coefficient of Restitution Formula

Definition: The coefficient of restitution \( e \) measures how much energy is retained after a collision or rebound. It is given by:

\[ e = \sqrt{\frac{h_2}{h_1}} \]

Where:

  • \( e \) = Coefficient of restitution
  • \( h_1 \) = Initial height from which the body is dropped
  • \( h_2 \) = Height to which the body rebounds

Important Cases:

  • If \( e = 1 \), the body rebounds to the same height (perfectly elastic).
  • If \( e = 0 \), the body does not rebound at all (perfectly inelastic).
  • For real cases, \( 0 < e < 1 \), meaning the body loses some energy on impact.
Body Dropped from Height \( h \)

Given: A body is dropped from height \( h \) and rebounds with a coefficient of restitution \( e \).

Height After Each Rebound:

\( h_n = e^n h \) (Height after \( n \)th rebound)

Total Distance Traveled Before Coming to Rest:

\( S_{\text{total}} = h + 2h e + 2h e^2 + 2h e^3 + \dots \)

Since this forms an infinite geometric series:

\( S_{\text{total}} = \frac{h (1 + e)}{1 - e} \), for \( 0 < e < 1 \)

Important Cases:

  • If \( e = 1 \), the body rebounds to the same height forever (perfectly elastic).
  • If \( e = 0 \), the body does not rebound (perfectly inelastic).
  • For real-life cases, \( 0 < e < 1 \), so the body eventually comes to rest.
Two-Dimensional (Oblique) Collision

Definition: When two bodies collide at an angle, the collision is two-dimensional. The velocity components must be resolved along two perpendicular directions: parallel and perpendicular to the line of impact.

Velocity Components After Collision:

Along the Line of Impact (x-axis):

\( v_{1x} = \frac{(m_1 - e m_2) u_{1x} + (1+e) m_2 u_{2x}}{m_1 + m_2} \)

\( v_{2x} = \frac{(m2 - e m_1) u_{2x} + (1+e) m_1 u_{1x}}{m_1 + m_2} \)

Perpendicular to the Line of Impact (y-axis):

Since no force acts in this direction:

\( v_{1y} = u_{1y}, \quad v_{2y} = u_{2y} \)

Special Case - When One Body is at Rest Before Collision:

  • If \( u_{2x} = 0 \), then:

    • \( v_{1x} = \frac{(m_1 - e m_2) u_{1x}}{m_1 + m_2} \)

    \( v_{2x} = \frac{(1+e) m_1 u_{1x}}{m_1 + m_2} \)

Important Notes:

  • Momentum is conserved in both x and y directions.
  • Kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions.
  • Coefficient of restitution applies only along the line of impact.
