Waves and Sound

Introduction to Waves

Definition: A wave is a disturbance that propagates energy through a medium or space without the transport of matter.

Types of Waves

Waves can be classified based on the nature of their propagation.

✅ 1. Mechanical Waves:
  • Require a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to propagate.
  • Example: Sound waves, water waves, seismic waves.
✅ 2. Electromagnetic Waves:
  • Do not require a medium; can propagate in a vacuum.
  • Example: Light, X-rays, radio waves, microwaves.
✅ 3. Matter Waves (De Broglie Waves):
  • Associated with moving particles (Quantum Mechanics).
  • Example: Electrons, protons, and other subatomic particles.

Nature of Waves

✅ 1. Transverse Waves:
  • Particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.
  • Example: Light waves, water waves, electromagnetic waves.
✅ 2. Longitudinal Waves:
  • Particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave propagation.
  • Example: Sound waves, seismic P-waves.

Important Terms Related to Wave Motion

✅ 1. Amplitude (\(A\)):

The maximum displacement of a particle from its mean position.

Formula: \( y = A \sin(\omega t + \phi) \)

✅ 2. Wavelength (\(\lambda\)):

The distance between two consecutive crests or troughs in a wave.

Unit: Meter (m)

✅ 3. Time Period (\(T\)):

The time taken to complete one full wave cycle.

Formula: \( T = \frac{1}{f} \)

✅ 4. Frequency (\(f\)):

The number of wave cycles passing a point per second.

Formula: \( f = \frac{1}{T} \)

✅ 5. Wave Velocity (\(v\)):

The speed at which a wave propagates in a medium.

Formula: \( v = f \lambda \)

Sound Waves and Their Types

Sound waves are longitudinal mechanical waves that travel through a medium (solid, liquid, or gas).

✅ 1. Infrasonic Waves:
  • Frequency less than 20 Hz.
  • Not audible to humans.
  • Example: Earthquakes, animal communication (elephants, whales).
✅ 2. Audible Waves:
  • Frequency range 20 Hz to 20 kHz.
  • Can be heard by humans.
  • Example: Normal human speech, musical instruments.
✅ 3. Ultrasonic Waves:
  • Frequency greater than 20 kHz.
  • Used in medical imaging, sonar, and cleaning.
  • Example: Bats, dolphins, ultrasound machines.

Velocity of Longitudinal Sound Waves

Longitudinal sound waves travel through a medium in the form of compressions and rarefactions.

✅ General Formula:

\( v = \sqrt{\frac{E}{\rho}} \)

where,

  • \( v \) = Velocity of sound wave
  • \( E \) = Elasticity of the medium
  • \( \rho \) = Density of the medium

Newton's Formula

Newton assumed that sound waves propagate isothermally (constant temperature).

For gases, the bulk modulus is \( B = P \), so the velocity is:

\( v = \sqrt{\frac{P}{\rho}} \)

where, \( P \) = Pressure of the gas, \( \rho \) = Density of gas.

❌ Issue: This formula gave incorrect results.

Laplace's Correction

Laplace corrected Newton’s formula by considering the propagation as an adiabatic process.

The correct formula is:

\( v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}} \)

where,

  • \( \gamma \) = Adiabatic index (\( \frac{C_p}{C_v} \))
  • \( P \) = Pressure of the gas
  • \( \rho \) = Density of the gas

✅ This correction matched experimental results.

Factors Affecting Velocity of Sound

✅ 1. Effect of Pressure:

For gases, since \( v = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}} \), at constant temperature, \( P/\rho \) is constant, so velocity is independent of pressure.

✅ 2. Effect of Temperature:

Velocity increases with temperature.

\( v_T = v_0 \sqrt{\frac{T}{T_0}} \)

where \( v_0 \) is velocity at temperature \( T_0 \).

✅ 3. Effect of Density:

Velocity is inversely proportional to the square root of density.

\( v \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\rho}} \)

✅ 4. Effect of Humidity:

Velocity increases with humidity because water vapor is less dense than air.

\( v_{\text{humid}} > v_{\text{dry}} \)

Speed of Transverse Motion

For a stretched string, the speed of a transverse wave is given by:

\( v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} \)

where:

  • \( T \) = Tension in the string
  • \( \mu \) = Mass per unit length of the string (\( \mu = \frac{m}{L} \))

✅ The speed increases with higher tension and decreases with higher mass per unit length.

Plane Progressive Simple Harmonic Wave

A progressive wave traveling in the positive x-direction can be represented as:

\( y(x,t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t + \phi) \)

where:

  • \( A \) = Amplitude of wave
  • \( k \) = Angular wave number (\( k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \))
  • \( \omega \) = Angular frequency (\( \omega = 2\pi f \))
  • \( \lambda \) = Wavelength
  • \( f \) = Frequency
  • \( \phi \) = Initial phase

Important Relations in Progressive Wave

✅ 1. Wave Velocity:

\( v = f\lambda \)

Velocity depends on wavelength and frequency.

✅ 2. Wavelength & Angular Wave Number:

\( k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \)

✅ 3. Time Period & Frequency:

\( f = \frac{1}{T} \) , \( \omega = 2\pi f \)

✅ 4. Particle Velocity:

\( v_p = \frac{\partial y}{\partial t} = A\omega \cos(kx - \omega t + \phi) \)

✅ 5. Phase of Vibration:

The phase of a vibrating particle in a wave is:

\( \theta = kx - \omega t + \phi \)

Energy in a Wave

In wave motion, energy is continuously transferred through the medium without the actual transport of matter.

Energy Density

The energy stored per unit volume of the medium is called energy density:

\( u = \frac{1}{2} \rho \omega^2 A^2 \)

where:

  • \( \rho \) = Density of the medium
  • \( \omega \) = Angular frequency
  • \( A \) = Amplitude of the wave

Power of a Wave

The rate at which energy is transmitted by a wave is called its power:

\( P = \frac{1}{2} \rho v \omega^2 A^2 \)

where:

  • \( v \) = Velocity of the wave

Intensity of a Wave

Intensity is defined as power per unit area:

\( I = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{1}{2} \rho v \omega^2 A^2 \)

✅ Intensity is directly proportional to **square of amplitude**.

Superposition of Waves

When two or more waves travel simultaneously through a medium, their displacements add algebraically. This is known as the **principle of superposition**:

\( y_{\text{resultant}} = y_1 + y_2 \)

✅ Conditions:
  • If the waves are in phase, they interfere constructively (amplitudes add).
  • If the waves are out of phase, they interfere destructively (amplitudes subtract).

Interference of Waves

When two or more waves of the same frequency and phase (or constant phase difference) overlap, the resultant displacement is given by the **principle of superposition**. This phenomenon is called **interference**.

Constructive Interference

Occurs when two waves meet in phase, resulting in an increase in amplitude.

✅ Conditions:
  • Path difference: \( \Delta x = n\lambda \) (where \( n = 0, 1, 2, \dots \))
  • Phase difference: \( \Delta \phi = 2n\pi \)

Resultant Amplitude:

\( A_{\text{res}} = A_1 + A_2 \)

Resultant Intensity:

\( I_{\text{res}} = I_1 + I_2 + 2\sqrt{I_1 I_2} \)

Destructive Interference

Occurs when two waves meet out of phase, resulting in a decrease in amplitude.

✅ Conditions:
  • Path difference: \( \Delta x = (2n+1) \frac{\lambda}{2} \)
  • Phase difference: \( \Delta \phi = (2n+1) \pi \)

Resultant Amplitude:

\( A_{\text{res}} = |A_1 - A_2| \)

Resultant Intensity:

\( I_{\text{res}} = I_1 + I_2 - 2\sqrt{I_1 I_2} \)

Beats

Beats occur when two sound waves of slightly different frequencies interfere, producing alternating loud and soft sounds.

✅ Beat Frequency Formula:

\( f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2| \)

Reflection of Waves

When a wave hits a surface and bounces back, it undergoes reflection.

✅ Laws of Reflection:
  • The angle of incidence = The angle of reflection.
  • The incident wave, reflected wave, and normal lie in the same plane.

Echo

An echo is the reflection of sound that is heard after a delay. It occurs when the reflected sound reaches the listener at least **0.1 seconds** after the original sound.

✅ Formula for Echo:

\( d = \frac{v \times t}{2} \)

where:

  • \( d \) = Minimum distance for echo (17.2 m in air)
  • \( v \) = Speed of sound
  • \( t \) = Time interval

Stationary (Standing) Waves

Stationary waves are formed when two identical waves traveling in opposite directions interfere.

✅ Equation of a Stationary Wave:

\( y = 2A \sin(kx) \cos(\omega t) \)

Nodes & Antinodes

In stationary waves:

  • Nodes (N): Points where displacement is always zero.
  • Antinodes (A): Points where displacement is maximum.
✅ Positions of Nodes:

\( x = n\frac{\lambda}{2} \), where \( n = 0, 1, 2, ... \)

✅ Positions of Antinodes:

\( x = (2n+1) \frac{\lambda}{4} \), where \( n = 0, 1, 2, ... \)

Important Points on Stationary Waves

  • Formed due to superposition of two waves of equal frequency and amplitude moving in opposite directions.
  • Energy is not transmitted, unlike progressive waves.
  • Distance between two consecutive nodes (or antinodes) is \( \lambda/2 \).

Vibrations in a Stretched String

When a string is plucked or struck, it vibrates and produces sound waves. The wave motion in a stretched string follows the wave equation:

\( v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} \)

  • \( v \) = Velocity of the wave
  • \( T \) = Tension in the string
  • \( \mu \) = Linear mass density of the string (\( \mu = \frac{m}{L} \))

Vibrations of a String Fixed at One End

When a string is fixed at one end and free at the other, it produces standing waves with a **node at the fixed end** and an **antinode at the free end**.

Standing Waves in a String Fixed at Both Ends

A string fixed at both ends produces stationary waves with nodes at both ends and antinodes in between.

✅ Fundamental Frequency:

\( f_1 = \frac{1}{2L} \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} \)

✅ Overtones:

The higher frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.

Organ Pipes

Organ pipes are tubes in which sound is produced by vibrating air columns. They are of two types:

Open Organ Pipe

An organ pipe open at both ends forms a standing wave with antinodes at both ends.

✅ Fundamental Frequency:

\( f_1 = \frac{v}{2L} \)

Closed Organ Pipe

An organ pipe closed at one end has a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end.

✅ Fundamental Frequency:

\( f_1 = \frac{v}{4L} \)

Terms Related to Vibrating Air Columns & Strings

  • Fundamental Note: The lowest frequency of vibration.
  • Overtones: Higher frequencies above the fundamental frequency.
  • Harmonics: Frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.
  • First Harmonic: The same as the fundamental frequency.

Vibrations in Open Organ Pipe

An open organ pipe has both ends open, allowing the air column to vibrate with antinodes at both ends and nodes in between.

✅ Fundamental Frequency:

\( f_1 = \frac{v}{2L} \)

✅ Overtones:
  • 2nd Harmonic (First Overtone): \( f_2 = \frac{2v}{2L} \)
  • 3rd Harmonic (Second Overtone): \( f_3 = \frac{3v}{2L} \)

Vibrations in Closed Organ Pipe

A closed organ pipe has one end closed and the other open, creating a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end.

✅ Fundamental Frequency:

\( f_1 = \frac{v}{4L} \)

✅ Overtones:
  • 3rd Harmonic (First Overtone): \( f_2 = \frac{3v}{4L} \)
  • 5th Harmonic (Second Overtone): \( f_3 = \frac{5v}{4L} \)

🔹 Only odd harmonics are present in a closed organ pipe.

End Correction

Due to the spreading of sound waves at the open end of a pipe, the effective length of the pipe increases slightly. This additional length is called end correction.

✅ Formula:

\( e = 0.6d \)

  • \( e \) = End correction
  • \( d \) = Diameter of the pipe

🔹 The effective length of the pipe is given by:

\( L_{\text{eff}} = L + e \) (for one open end)

\( L_{\text{eff}} = L + 2e \) (for both open ends)

Factors Affecting Frequency of a Wave in a Pipe

  • Length of the Pipe: Frequency is inversely proportional to the length of the pipe.

    • \( f \propto \frac{1}{L} \)

  • Velocity of Sound in Air: Frequency increases with the velocity of sound.

    • \( f \propto v \)

  • Temperature: Higher temperature increases the speed of sound, increasing the frequency.
  • Humidity: More humidity increases the speed of sound, thus increasing the frequency.
  • Nature of Gas in Pipe: Sound travels faster in lighter gases, affecting the frequency.

Doppler's Effect

The **Doppler Effect** is the change in frequency (or pitch) of a wave due to the relative motion between the source and the observer.

✅ Formula for Apparent Frequency:

\( f' = f \times \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s} \)

  • \( f' \) = Apparent frequency
  • \( f \) = Actual frequency of source
  • \( v \) = Speed of sound in medium
  • \( v_o \) = Speed of observer
  • \( v_s \) = Speed of source
  • Use + sign when moving towards and - sign when moving away.

Case 1: Source Moving, Observer at Rest

When the source moves with velocity \( v_s \) and the observer is stationary:

✅ Frequency when source moves towards observer:

\( f' = f \times \frac{v}{v - v_s} \)

✅ Frequency when source moves away from observer:

\( f' = f \times \frac{v}{v + v_s} \)

Case 2: Source at Rest, Observer Moving

When the observer moves with velocity \( v_o \) and the source is stationary:

✅ Frequency when observer moves towards source:

\( f' = f \times \frac{v + v_o}{v} \)

✅ Frequency when observer moves away from source:

\( f' = f \times \frac{v - v_o}{v} \)

Case 3: Both Source and Observer Moving

When both the source and the observer are moving:

✅ General Formula:

\( f' = f \times \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s} \)

  • Use + sign in \( v_o \) when the observer moves towards the source.
  • Use - sign in \( v_o \) when the observer moves away from the source.
  • Use - sign in \( v_s \) when the source moves towards the observer.
  • Use + sign in \( v_s \) when the source moves away from the observer.
