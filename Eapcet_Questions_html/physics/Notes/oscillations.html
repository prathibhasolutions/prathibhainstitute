Oscillations

Periodic Motion and Oscillatory Motion

Periodic and oscillatory motions are fundamental types of motion observed in physics.

Periodic Motion

A motion that repeats itself at regular intervals of time is called Periodic Motion.

Examples: Motion of planets around the sun, motion of a pendulum.

Oscillatory Motion

An object is said to be in Oscillatory Motion if it moves back and forth about a fixed point in a definite time interval.

Examples: Motion of a simple pendulum, vibrating strings of a guitar.

Difference Between Periodic and Oscillatory Motion
Periodic Motion Oscillatory Motion
Motion repeats at regular intervals. Motion moves back and forth around a mean position.
All oscillatory motions are periodic. Not all periodic motions are oscillatory.
Example: Earth’s revolution around the Sun. Example: Pendulum swinging.
Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a type of periodic motion in which an object oscillates about a mean position under a restoring force proportional to its displacement.

Definition

A motion is said to be Simple Harmonic if the restoring force acting on the object is directly proportional to its displacement from the mean position and is always directed toward the mean position.

\[ F = - k x \]

Where:

  • \( F \) = Restoring force
  • \( k \) = Force constant
  • \( x \) = Displacement from the mean position
Displacement, Amplitude, and Phase
  • Displacement (\( x \)): The distance of the particle from the mean position at any instant.
  • Amplitude (\( A \)): The maximum displacement of the particle from the mean position.
  • Phase (\( \phi \)): The quantity that determines the initial position of oscillation.

\[ x = A \cos(\omega t + \phi) \]

Velocity in SHM

The velocity of a particle in SHM is given by:

\[ v = \omega \sqrt{A^2 - x^2} \]

Velocity is maximum at the mean position (\( v_{max} = \omega A \)) and zero at extreme positions.

Acceleration in SHM

The acceleration of a particle in SHM is:

\[ a = -\omega^2 x \]

Acceleration is maximum at the extreme positions and zero at the mean position.

Time Period (\( T \))

\[ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi \sqrt{ \frac{m}{k}} \]

Time taken to complete one full oscillation.

Frequency (\( f \))

\[ f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} \]

Number of oscillations per second.

Terms Related to SHM
  • Mean Position: The equilibrium position where the net force is zero.
  • Restoring Force: The force that brings the system back to equilibrium.
Important Values in Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

In SHM, velocity and acceleration vary periodically. The maximum and minimum values occur at specific positions in the motion.

Maximum Velocity (\( v_{max} \))

\[ v_{max} = \omega A \]

Occurs at: Mean position (\( x = 0 \))

Minimum Velocity (\( v_{min} \))

\[ v_{min} = 0 \]

Occurs at: Extreme positions (\( x = \pm A \))

Maximum Acceleration (\( a_{max} \))

\[ a_{max} = \omega^2 A \]

Occurs at: Extreme positions (\( x = \pm A \))

Minimum Acceleration (\( a_{min} \))

\[ a_{min} = 0 \]

Occurs at: Mean position (\( x = 0 \))

Force and Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)
Force in SHM

Expression for Force:

\[ F = -m \omega^2 x \]

Where:

  • \( F \) = Restoring force
  • \( m \) = Mass of the particle
  • \( \omega \) = Angular frequency
  • \( x \) = Displacement from mean position

Key Point: The negative sign indicates that the force is always directed towards the mean position.

Energy in SHM
Kinetic Energy (\( KE \))

\[ KE = \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 (A^2 - x^2) \]

Key Points:

  • Maximum at the mean position (\( x = 0 \)).
  • Minimum (zero) at the extreme positions (\( x = \pm A \)).
Potential Energy (\( PE \))

\[ PE = \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 \]

Key Points:

  • Maximum at the extreme positions (\( x = \pm A \)).
  • Minimum (zero) at the mean position (\( x = 0 \)).
Total Energy (\( TE \))

\[ TE = \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 A^2 \]

Key Points:

  • Remains constant throughout the motion.
  • Sum of kinetic and potential energy at any point.
Simple Pendulum
Definition

A simple pendulum consists of a small mass (bob) suspended from a fixed point by a light, inextensible string of length \( L \), which oscillates back and forth under the influence of gravity.

Time Period of a Simple Pendulum

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \]

Where:

  • \( T \) = Time period of oscillation
  • \( L \) = Length of the pendulum
  • \( g \) = Acceleration due to gravity

Key Point: The time period is independent of the mass of the bob.

Frequency

\[ f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}} \]

Maximum Velocity

\[ v_{\max} = \omega A = \sqrt{\frac{2gA}{L}} \]

Where:

  • \( v_{\max} \) = Maximum velocity
  • \( A \) = Amplitude of oscillation
  • \( \omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}} \) = Angular frequency
Small Angle Approximation

For small angles (\( \theta \approx \sin\theta \)), the motion of a simple pendulum is approximately simple harmonic.

Special Cases of Simple Pendulum
1. Length of Pendulum Equal to Radius of Earth (\( L = R \))

Time period is given by:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{R}{2g}} \]

2. Infinite Length (\( L \to \infty \))

For an infinitely long pendulum, the time period becomes very large:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{R}{g}} \]

3. Effect of Temperature on Time Period

If temperature increases, the length increases as:

\[ L' = L(1 + \alpha \Delta T) \]

New time period:

\[ T' = T \left(1 + \frac{\alpha \Delta T}{2} \right) \]

Where: \( \alpha \) = Coefficient of linear expansion.

4. Simple Pendulum in a Fluid of Density \( \rho \)

Effective acceleration due to gravity:

\[ g' = g \left(1 - \frac{\rho}{\sigma} \right) \]

Modified time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g'}} \]

Where: \( \sigma \) = Density of the pendulum bob.

5. Simple Pendulum in a Horizontally Accelerating Vehicle

For a vehicle accelerating with acceleration \( a \):

Effective gravity:

\[ g' = \sqrt{g^2 + a^2} \]

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g'}} \]

6. Simple Pendulum in a Vehicle Sliding Down an Inclined Plane

For an incline with angle \( \theta \):

Effective gravity:

\[ g' = g \cos\theta \]

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g\cos\theta}} \]

7. Simple Pendulum in a Uniform Electric Field (Upward)

For a charged bob of charge \( q \) in an electric field \( E \) acting upwards:

Effective gravity:

\[ g' = g - \frac{qE}{m} \]

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g'}} \]

Types of Pendulums
1. Second's Pendulum

A pendulum that takes 1 second to move from one extreme to another (total time period = 2 seconds).

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \]

For a second’s pendulum:

\[ T = 2 \text{ s}, \quad L = 0.994 \text{ m} \quad (\approx 1 \text{ m}) \]

2. Conical Pendulum

A pendulum moving in a horizontal circular path with a constant angular velocity, making an angle \( \theta \) with the vertical.

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L \cos \theta}{g}} \]

Centripetal force:

\[ T \sin \theta = m \frac{v^2}{r} \]

Height of conical motion:

\[ h = L \cos \theta \]

3. Spring Pendulum

When a mass \( m \) is attached to a spring with force constant \( k \), it oscillates vertically.

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} \]

4. Spring Pendulum (Springs in Series)

For two springs in series with force constants \( k_1 \) and \( k_2 \), the effective spring constant is:

\[ \frac{1}{k_{\text{eff}}} = \frac{1}{k_1} + \frac{1}{k_2} \]

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k_{\text{eff}}}} \]

5. Spring Pendulum (Springs in Parallel)

For two springs in parallel with force constants \( k_1 \) and \( k_2 \), the effective spring constant is:

\[ k_{\text{eff}} = k_1 + k_2 \]

Time period:

\[ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k_{\text{eff}}}} \]
