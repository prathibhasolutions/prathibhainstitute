Magnetic Effect of Current

Oersted's Experiment: In 1820, Hans Christian Oersted discovered that a current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it.

✅ Key Observations:
  • A deflection in the magnetic needle was observed when current was passed through the wire.
  • Reversing the current direction reversed the deflection.
  • Stronger current produced a stronger deflection.

Magnetic Field

The region around a current-carrying conductor where its influence is experienced is called the magnetic field.

Unit: Tesla (T) or Gauss (G)

Rules to Find the Direction of Magnetic Field

✅ Important Rules:
  • Maxwell's Corkscrew Rule: If a right-handed corkscrew is rotated in the direction of current, its tip moves in the direction of the magnetic field.
  • Ampere's Swimming Rule: If a person swims in the direction of current with their face towards the conductor, the magnetic field acts in the left-to-right direction.
  • Right-Hand Thumb Rule: If the thumb of the right hand points in the direction of current, the curled fingers show the direction of the magnetic field.

Biot-Savart's Law

Statement: The magnetic field \( dB \) at a point due to a small element of current-carrying conductor is given by:

\( dB = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{I \, dl \, \sin \theta}{r^2} \)

Where:

  • \( dB \) = Magnetic field at a point
  • \( \mu_0 \) = Permeability of free space (\(4\pi \times 10^{-7} \) Tm/A)
  • \( I \) = Current in the conductor
  • \( dl \) = Small length element
  • \( \theta \) = Angle between \( dl \) and line joining the element to the point
  • \( r \) = Distance from the element to the point

Magnetic Field Due to a Straight Current-Carrying Conductor

The magnetic field at a distance \( r \) from a long straight conductor carrying current \( I \) is given by:

\( B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r} \)

Magnetic Field at the Center of a Circular Current-Carrying Coil

The magnetic field at the center of a circular loop of radius \( R \) carrying current \( I \) is:

\( B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R} \)

Magnetic Field on the Axis of a Current-Carrying Circular Coil

The magnetic field at a point on the axis of a circular loop of radius \( R \), carrying current \( I \), at a distance \( x \) from the center is:

\( B = \frac{\mu_0 I R^2}{2 (R^2 + x^2)^{3/2}} \)

Ampere's Circuital Law

Statement: The line integral of the magnetic field around a closed path is equal to the product of the permeability of free space and the total current enclosed by the path.

∮ B ⋅ dl = μ₀ I

Magnetic Field Due to a Current-Carrying Long Circular Cylindrical Wire

The magnetic field inside and outside a long current-carrying cylindrical wire depends on the distance from the center:

Inside the wire (r ≤ R):

B = (μ₀ I r) / (2π R²)

Outside the wire (r ≥ R):

B = (μ₀ I) / (2π r)

Magnetic Field Due to a Solenoid

A solenoid is a long coil of wire wound in a helical shape. The magnetic field inside a long solenoid is given by:

B = μ₀ n I

where n is the number of turns per unit length.

Magnetic Field Due to a Toroid

A toroid is a solenoid bent into a circular shape. The magnetic field inside a toroid is:

B = (μ₀ N I) / (2π r)

where N is the total number of turns and r is the radius of the toroid.

Force on a Charged Particle in a Uniform Magnetic Field

The force on a charged particle moving in a magnetic field is given by:

F = q (v × B)

where q is the charge, v is the velocity, and B is the magnetic field.

Fleming's Left Hand Rule

Fleming's Left Hand Rule states that if the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the left hand are held perpendicular to each other:

  • Thumb represents the force (motion) on the conductor.
  • Forefinger represents the direction of the magnetic field.
  • Middle finger represents the direction of current.

Motion of a Charged Particle in a Uniform Magnetic Field

A charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field follows a circular path with radius:

r = (m v) / (q B)

The time period of motion is:

T = (2π m) / (q B)

Lorentz Force

The total force experienced by a charged particle moving in combined electric and magnetic fields is given by:

F = q (E + v × B)

Cyclotron and Principle of Cyclotron

A cyclotron is a device used to accelerate charged particles using a perpendicular magnetic field and an alternating electric field.

Principle:

Charged particles are accelerated in a circular path due to the magnetic force, and their velocity increases as they spiral outward.

The maximum kinetic energy gained by a particle is given by:

K = (q² B² R²) / (2m)

Limitations of Cyclotron

  • It cannot accelerate neutral or very heavy particles.
  • Relativistic effects cause deviations at very high speeds.
  • It does not work for electrons due to their small mass.

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force given by:

F = BIL sinθ

where B is the magnetic field, I is the current, L is the length of the conductor, and θ is the angle between the conductor and the field.

Force Between Two Infinitely Long Parallel Current-Carrying Conductors

The force per unit length between two parallel conductors carrying currents I₁ and I₂ separated by distance d is:

F/L = (μ₀ I₁ I₂) / (2π d)

If the currents are in the same direction, the conductors attract; if in opposite directions, they repel.

Definition of Ampere

Ampere is defined based on the force between two parallel current-carrying conductors:

1 Ampere is the current which, when flowing through two parallel conductors placed 1 meter apart in vacuum, produces a force of 2 × 10⁻⁷ N per meter of length.

Magnetic Force Between Two Moving Charges

Two moving charges exert a magnetic force on each other given by:

F = (μ₀ q₁ q₂ v₁ v₂ sinθ) / (4π r²)

where q₁ and q₂ are the charges, v₁ and v₂ are their velocities, r is the separation, and θ is the angle between their velocity vectors.

Magnetic Dipole

A magnetic dipole consists of two equal and opposite magnetic poles separated by a distance. The dipole moment is:

M = m × d

where m is the pole strength and d is the separation between poles.

Torque on a Current-Carrying Coil in a Uniform Magnetic Field

A rectangular current-carrying coil placed in a uniform magnetic field experiences a torque given by:

τ = N B I A sinθ

where N is the number of turns, B is the magnetic field, I is the current, A is the area of the coil, and θ is the angle between the coil and the field.

Moving Coil Galvanometer

A moving coil galvanometer is a device used to detect small electric currents.

Working Principle: It works on the principle that a current-carrying coil placed in a magnetic field experiences a torque.

τ = N B I A sinθ

where N = number of turns, B = magnetic field, I = current, A = area of the coil, and θ = angle between coil and field.

Current Sensitivity

Current sensitivity of a galvanometer is the deflection per unit current.

Sc = (N B A) / k

where k is the torsional constant of the suspension wire.

Voltage Sensitivity

Voltage sensitivity of a galvanometer is the deflection per unit voltage.

Sv = Sc / R

where R is the resistance of the galvanometer.

Ammeter

An ammeter is a device used to measure electric current in a circuit. It is always connected in series.

Key Properties:

  • It has a low resistance.
  • It does not alter the current significantly.

Conversion of Galvanometer into an Ammeter

A galvanometer is converted into an ammeter by connecting a low resistance called a shunt in parallel with it.

S = (G Ig) / (I - Ig)

where S = shunt resistance, G = galvanometer resistance, I = total current, and Ig = current through the galvanometer.

Voltmeter

A voltmeter is a device used to measure voltage. It is always connected in parallel across a component.

Key Properties:

  • It has a high resistance.
  • It does not draw significant current.

Conversion of Galvanometer into a Voltmeter

A galvanometer is converted into a voltmeter by connecting a high resistance in series with it.

R = (V / Ig) - G

where R = series resistance, V = voltage to be measured, Ig = maximum current in the galvanometer, and G = galvanometer resistance.
