System of Particles and Rotational Motion

Center of Mass & System of Particles

Definition: The center of mass (COM) is the point where the entire mass of a system is assumed to be concentrated for motion analysis.

Coordinates of Center of Mass:

\[ x_{cm} = \frac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i}, \quad y_{cm} = \frac{\sum m_i y_i}{\sum m_i}, \quad z_{cm} = \frac{\sum m_i z_i}{\sum m_i} \]

Velocity of Center of Mass:

\[ V_{cm} = \frac{\sum m_i V_i}{\sum m_i} \]

Acceleration of Center of Mass:

\[ a_{cm} = \frac{\sum m_i a_i}{\sum m_i} \]

Linear Momentum of System of Particles

Total Linear Momentum:

\[ P = M V_{cm} \]

Newton’s Second Law for System:

\[ F_{ext} = M a_{cm} \]

Where \( F_{ext} \) is the external force acting on the system.

Important Points:

  • The COM moves as if all mass were concentrated at a single point.
  • If no external force acts, the velocity of the COM remains constant.
  • COM of a rigid body depends only on its shape and mass distribution.
  • For a symmetric body with uniform density, the COM is at its geometric center.
Rigid Body, Translational & Rotational Motion

Rigid Body: A rigid body is an object in which the relative positions of all particles remain constant under any force.

Translational Motion

In translational motion, every point in the body moves parallel to others. It follows Newton’s laws.

Equations of Motion (SUVAT):

\[ v = u + at, \quad s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2, \quad v^2 = u^2 + 2as \]

Momentum: \( P = mv \)

Kinetic Energy: \( K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \)

Rotational Motion

In rotational motion, different points move in circular paths around a fixed axis.

Angular Kinematics Equations:

\[ \omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t, \quad \theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^2, \quad \omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha \theta \]

Moment of Inertia: \( I = \sum m_i r_i^2 \)

Torque: \( \tau = I \alpha \)

Angular Momentum: \( L = I \omega \)

Kinetic Energy of Rotation: \( K_{rot} = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2 \)

Comparison of Translational & Rotational Motion
Translational Motion Rotational Motion
Displacement \( x \) Angular Displacement \( \theta \)
Velocity \( v \) Angular Velocity \( \omega \)
Acceleration \( a \) Angular Acceleration \( \alpha \)
Force \( F = ma \) Torque \( \tau = I \alpha \)
Momentum \( P = mv \) Angular Momentum \( L = I \omega \)
Kinetic Energy \( K = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 \) Rotational Kinetic Energy \( K_{rot} = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2 \)
Moment of Inertia & Related Theorems

Moment of Inertia (\( I \)): It is the rotational analog of mass in linear motion, representing a body's resistance to rotational motion.

\[ I = \sum m_i r_i^2 \]

Depends on:

  • Mass of the body
  • Distribution of mass about the axis
  • Position of the axis of rotation
Radius of Gyration

Definition: The distance from the axis of rotation at which the entire mass of the body can be assumed to be concentrated to have the same moment of inertia.

\[ I = M k^2 \]

Where:

  • \( I \) = Moment of Inertia
  • \( M \) = Mass of the body
  • \( k \) = Radius of Gyration
Parallel Axes Theorem

Statement: The moment of inertia about any axis parallel to the center of mass axis is given by:

\[ I = I_{cm} + Md^2 \]

Where:

  • \( I \) = Moment of inertia about any parallel axis
  • \( I_{cm} \) = Moment of inertia about the center of mass
  • \( M \) = Mass of the body
  • \( d \) = Perpendicular distance between axes
Perpendicular Axes Theorem

Statement: The moment of inertia about an axis perpendicular to a plane is the sum of the moments of inertia about two mutually perpendicular axes in the same plane.

\[ I_z = I_x + I_y \]

Where:

  • \( I_z \) = Moment of inertia about the perpendicular axis
  • \( I_x, I_y \) = Moments of inertia about the two perpendicular axes in the plane

Torque (Moment of Force)

Definition: Torque is the rotational equivalent of force, which causes an object to rotate about an axis.

\[ \tau = r \times F = rF \sin\theta \]

Where:

  • \( \tau \) = Torque
  • \( r \) = Perpendicular distance from axis of rotation
  • \( F \) = Applied force
  • \( \theta \) = Angle between \( r \) and \( F \)

Units: Newton-meter (N·m)

Angular Momentum

Definition: Angular momentum is the rotational equivalent of linear momentum.

\[ L = r \times p = r m v \sin\theta \]

For a rotating body:

\[ L = I \omega \]

Where:

  • \( L \) = Angular momentum
  • \( r \) = Radius vector
  • \( p \) = Linear momentum
  • \( I \) = Moment of Inertia
  • \( \omega \) = Angular velocity

Units: kg·m²/s

Principle of Moments

Statement: For an object in rotational equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments equals the sum of counterclockwise moments.

\[ \sum \tau_{clockwise} = \sum \tau_{counterclockwise} \]

Application:

  • Used in balancing beams, levers, and seesaws.
  • Essential in engineering and mechanical design.
Conservation of Angular Momentum

Statement: If no external torque acts on a system, its total angular momentum remains constant.

\[ I_1 \omega_1 = I_2 \omega_2 \]

Where:

  • \( I_1, I_2 \) = Moments of inertia before and after
  • \( \omega_1, \omega_2 \) = Angular velocities before and after

Example: A spinning ice skater pulls in their arms to rotate faster.

Torque & Angular Momentum of a System of Particles

For a system of particles:

\[ \tau_{ext} = \frac{dL}{dt} \]

Where:

  • \( \tau_{ext} \) = Net external torque on the system
  • \( L \) = Total angular momentum
  • \( \frac{dL}{dt} \) = Rate of change of angular momentum

Implication: If \( \tau_{ext} = 0 \), then \( L \) is conserved.

Equilibrium

Definition: A body is said to be in equilibrium if the net force and net torque acting on it are zero.

Types of Equilibrium:

  • Static Equilibrium: Object is at rest.
  • Dynamic Equilibrium: Object moves with constant velocity.

Conditions for Equilibrium:

\[ \sum F = 0, \quad \sum \tau = 0 \]

Couple

Definition: A couple is a pair of equal and opposite forces acting at different points, producing rotation without translation.

Torque Due to a Couple:

\[ \tau = F \times d \]

Where:

  • \( F \) = Magnitude of force
  • \( d \) = Perpendicular distance between forces

Examples: Steering wheel, opening a bottle cap.

Centre of Gravity

Definition: The centre of gravity of a body is the point where the entire weight of the body acts.

Formula:

\[ X_{cg} = \frac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i}, \quad Y_{cg} = \frac{\sum m_i y_i}{\sum m_i} \]

Examples: Centre of gravity of a uniform rod is at its midpoint.

Angular Impulse

Definition: Angular impulse is the product of torque and the time duration for which it acts.

\[ J = \int \tau dt \]

Impulse-Angular Momentum Theorem:

\[ J = \Delta L \]

Where:

  • \( J \) = Angular impulse
  • \( \tau \) = Torque
  • \( L \) = Angular momentum
Rotational Kinetic Energy

Definition: The kinetic energy possessed by a rotating object due to its angular motion.

\[ KE_{rot} = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2 \]

Where:

  • \( I \) = Moment of inertia
  • \( \omega \) = Angular velocity

Example: A spinning wheel has rotational kinetic energy.

Rolling Motion on an Inclined Plane

Definition: When a body rolls down an inclined plane without slipping, it has both translational and rotational motion.

Acceleration of Rolling Body:

\[ a = \frac{g \sin\theta}{1 + \frac{K^2}{R^2}} \]

Velocity at the Bottom:

\[ v = \sqrt{\frac{2 g h}{1 + \frac{K^2}{R^2}}} \]

Time Taken to Reach the Bottom:

\[ t = \sqrt{\frac{2 h (1 + \frac{K^2}{R^2})}{g \sin\theta}} \]

Where:

  • \( g \) = Acceleration due to gravity
  • \( \theta \) = Angle of inclination
  • \( K \) = Radius of gyration
  • \( R \) = Radius of the rolling body
  • \( h \) = Height from which the body starts rolling

Power Delivered by Torque:

\[ P = \tau \omega \]

Work Done by Torque:

\[ W = \tau \theta \]

Condition for Rolling Without Slipping:

\[ v = R \omega \]

Energy Conservation:

\[ mgh = \frac{1}{2} M v^2 + \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2 \]

Comparison of Angular & Linear Speeds:

  • In **pure translational motion**, all points have the **same linear speed** and **zero angular speed**.
  • In **pure rotational motion**, all points have **different linear speeds**, but the **same angular speed**.
  • In **rolling motion**, all points have the **same angular speed** but **different linear speeds**, with the velocity of the point in contact with the surface being zero.
