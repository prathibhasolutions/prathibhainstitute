What is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest unit of an element that retains its chemical properties. It consists of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons.

Characteristics of Atoms
  • Atoms are the **basic building blocks** of matter.
  • They are **extremely small**, with a typical size of **0.1 nm**.
  • Consist of **subatomic particles**: **protons (+), electrons (-), and neutrons (0).**

Dalton's Atomic Theory

John Dalton proposed the first scientific theory of the atom in 1803. His postulates were:

  • All matter is composed of **indivisible and indestructible atoms**.
  • Atoms of the same element are **identical** in mass and properties.
  • Atoms of different elements have **different properties and masses**.
  • Compounds are formed by the **chemical combination of atoms** in fixed ratios.
  • Chemical reactions involve the **rearrangement of atoms** but do not create or destroy them.

Thomson's Atomic Model

J.J. Thomson proposed the **"Plum Pudding Model"** in 1897 after discovering the electron.

Key Feature Explanation
Atom is a Sphere The atom is a **positively charged sphere**.
Electrons are Embedded Electrons are randomly spread throughout the atom, like **plums in a pudding**.
Neutral Charge Positive charge balances negative electrons, making the atom **neutral**.
Limitations of Thomson's Model
  • Did not explain **atomic nucleus** and **proton discovery**.
  • Could not justify the **stability of electrons** inside the atom.
  • Later disproved by **Rutherford‚Äôs Gold Foil Experiment**.

Rutherford's Atomic Model

Ernest Rutherford proposed a nuclear model of the atom in 1911 based on his **Gold Foil Experiment**.

Observation Conclusion
Most alpha particles passed through Atom is mostly **empty space**.
Few alpha particles deflected There is a **dense, positively charged nucleus**.
Very few particles bounced back Nucleus is **tiny and massive**.

Distance of Closest Approach (ùëü‚ÇÄ)

It is the minimum distance at which an alpha particle reaches the nucleus before being repelled.

Correct Formula:

\( r_0 = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{2Ze^2}{K} \)

Where:
  • \( r_0 \) = Distance of closest approach
  • \( Z \) = Atomic number of the nucleus
  • \( e \) = Charge of an electron
  • \( K \) = Initial kinetic energy of the alpha particle
  • \( \varepsilon_0 \) = Permittivity of free space

Impact Parameter (b)

The **impact parameter** is the perpendicular distance between the initial velocity vector of an alpha particle and the center of the nucleus.

Formula:

\( b = \frac{Z e^2}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 K} \cot \frac{\theta}{2} \)

Where:
  • \( b \) = Impact Parameter
  • \( Z \) = Atomic number of nucleus
  • \( e \) = Charge of an electron
  • \( K \) = Initial kinetic energy of the alpha particle
  • \( \theta \) = Scattering angle
  • \( \varepsilon_0 \) = Permittivity of free space
Significance of Impact Parameter
  • If **b is small**, the alpha particle comes **closer to the nucleus** and experiences a **stronger repulsive force**, leading to **greater deflection**.
  • If **b is large**, the alpha particle remains **far from the nucleus** and experiences **less deflection**.
  • Impact parameter helps determine the **scattering angle** of alpha particles in nuclear experiments.

Rutherford's Scattering Formula

The **Rutherford Scattering Formula** describes the angular distribution of alpha particles scattered by a thin metal foil due to the electrostatic force from the atomic nucleus.

Formula:

\( N(\theta) = \left( \frac{Z e^2}{16 \pi \varepsilon_0 K} \right)^2 \frac{N_0 t n}{\sin^4(\theta/2)} \)

Where:
  • \( N(\theta) \) = Number of alpha particles scattered at angle \( \theta \)
  • \( Z \) = Atomic number of the target nucleus
  • \( e \) = Charge of an electron (\( 1.602 \times 10^{-19} C \))
  • \( K \) = Initial kinetic energy of the alpha particle
  • \( \theta \) = Scattering angle
  • \( \varepsilon_0 \) = Permittivity of free space (\( 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, C^2/N \cdot m^2 \))
  • \( N_0 \) = Total number of incident alpha particles
  • \( t \) = Thickness of the target foil
  • \( n \) = Number of atoms per unit volume in the target
Key Predictions of Rutherford's Formula
  • Scattering **decreases sharply** with increasing angle \( \theta \).
  • Heavier nuclei scatter **more strongly** due to larger \( Z \).
  • The number of particles scattered at a large angle is **very small**.

Limitations of Rutherford's Atomic Model

Although Rutherford‚Äôs model was a significant breakthrough, it had several limitations:

1. Could Not Explain Electron Stability

According to classical electrodynamics, an electron moving in a circular orbit should **continuously emit radiation** and spiral into the nucleus, but this does not happen.

2. Did Not Account for Atomic Spectra

The model could not explain **discrete spectral lines** in the hydrogen spectrum, which were later explained by Bohr‚Äôs theory.

3. Failed to Incorporate Quantum Mechanics

Rutherford‚Äôs model was entirely **classical**, ignoring quantum mechanical principles, which later became essential for understanding atomic structure.

4. Could Not Explain Chemical Properties

Rutherford‚Äôs model did not explain why **different elements have different chemical properties**, which later required quantum mechanical descriptions.

Bohr‚Äôs Atomic Model

Bohr‚Äôs Model was proposed in 1913 to explain the structure of atoms and spectral lines of hydrogen. It is based on the idea that electrons move in fixed circular orbits around the nucleus without radiating energy, and they can only occupy certain allowed energy levels.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Radius of Electron Orbit

The radius of an electron orbit in a hydrogen-like atom is given by:

\( r_n = \frac{4\pi \varepsilon_0 n^2 h^2}{m e^2 Z} \)

For **hydrogen** (\( Z=1 \)): \( r_n = 0.529 \times n^2 \) √Ö (Bohr radius for \( n=1 \)).

‚úÖ Larger orbits have **higher energy**.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Velocity of Electron

The velocity of an electron in a given orbit is:

\( v_n = \frac{e^2 Z}{2 \varepsilon_0 h n} \)

For **hydrogen**: \( v_n = \frac{2.18 \times 10^6 Z}{n} \) m/s.

‚úÖ Electrons in **lower orbits move faster**.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Frequency of Electron

The frequency of electron motion around the nucleus is:

\( f_n = \frac{Z^2 m e^4}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0^2 h^3 n^3} \)

‚úÖ It determines the time period of electron revolution.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Kinetic Energy (KE)

The kinetic energy of an electron in an orbit is:

\( KE = \frac{1}{2} m v_n^2 = \frac{e^4 m Z^2}{8 \varepsilon_0^2 h^2 n^2} \)

‚úÖ Electrons in **lower orbits have more kinetic energy**.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Potential Energy (PE)

The potential energy of the electron due to the nuclear attraction is:

\( PE = - \frac{e^4 m Z^2}{4 \varepsilon_0^2 h^2 n^2} \)

‚úÖ Negative sign shows that the electron is **bound to the nucleus**.

6Ô∏è‚É£ Total Energy (TE)

The total energy of the electron in orbit is:

\( TE = KE + PE = - \frac{e^4 m Z^2}{8 \varepsilon_0^2 h^2 n^2} \)

For **hydrogen** (\( Z=1 \)): \( TE = -13.6 \times \frac{Z^2}{n^2} \) eV.

‚úÖ The **energy is always negative**, meaning the electron is **bound to the nucleus**.

7Ô∏è‚É£ Excitation Energy

Excitation energy is the energy required to move an electron from a **lower orbit to a higher orbit**:

\( E_{excitation} = E_n - E_{ground} \)

‚úÖ Excitation leads to **spectral line emission** when electrons return to lower energy states.

8Ô∏è‚É£ Ionization Energy

Ionization energy is the energy required to completely remove an electron from the atom:

\( E_{ionization} = 13.6 Z^2 \) eV

‚úÖ For **hydrogen** (\( Z=1 \)), \( E_{ionization} = 13.6 \) eV.

9Ô∏è‚É£ Ionization Potential

Ionization potential is the ionization energy **per unit charge**:

\( V_{ionization} = \frac{E_{ionization}}{e} \)

‚úÖ It is measured in **volts (V)**.

De Broglie's Explanation of Bohr‚Äôs Second Postulate

Bohr‚Äôs second postulate states that electrons revolve in certain **allowed orbits** without radiating energy. However, Bohr did not explain why these specific orbits exist.

De Broglie (1924) proposed that **electrons exhibit wave nature**, and only those orbits where an integer number of electron wavelengths fit are allowed.

üîπ De Broglie Wavelength

According to De Broglie, the wavelength of a moving electron is given by:

\( \lambda = \frac{h}{mv} \)

where \( h \) is Planck‚Äôs constant, \( m \) is mass, and \( v \) is velocity of the electron.

üîπ Condition for Stable Orbits

For an electron to have a stable orbit, its circumference must be an integer multiple of its wavelength:

\( 2\pi r_n = n\lambda \)

Substituting \( \lambda = h/mv \), we get:

\( mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \)

‚úÖ This is exactly Bohr‚Äôs second postulate!

Hydrogen Spectrum & Spectral Series

When an electron in a hydrogen atom transitions from a **higher energy level** (\( n_2 \)) to a **lower energy level** (\( n_1 \)), it emits a photon of wavelength \( \lambda \), given by:

\( \frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H \left( \frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2} \right) \)

where \( R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7 \) m\(^{-1}\) is the Rydberg constant.

Wave Model of Electrons

Electrons do not move in fixed circular orbits but exhibit **wave behavior**. This explains why only certain energy levels exist.

üîπ Electron as a Standing Wave

When an electron moves around the nucleus, its wave must form a **standing wave**, meaning:

\( 2\pi r = n\lambda \)

‚úÖ This explains **why only discrete orbits exist**.
