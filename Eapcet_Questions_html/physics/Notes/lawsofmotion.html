Laws of Motion

Inertia and Its Types

Definition: Inertia is the tendency of a body to resist any change in its state of motion or rest unless acted upon by an external force.

1. Inertia of Rest

A body at rest tends to remain at rest unless acted upon by an external force.

Example: When a stationary bus starts suddenly, passengers tend to fall backward.

2. Inertia of Motion

A moving body continues in its state of motion unless acted upon by an external force.

Example: When a moving bus stops suddenly, passengers tend to fall forward.

3. Inertia of Direction

A body continues to move in the same direction unless acted upon by an external force.

Example: When a car turns sharply, passengers experience a sideways push.

Force and Its Types

Definition: Force is a push or pull that changes the state of motion of a body or its shape.

1. Contact Forces

Forces that act when two bodies are in physical contact.

Examples:

  • Frictional Force
  • Normal Reaction
  • Tension in a String
  • Spring Force
2. Non-Contact (Distant) Forces

Forces that act without physical contact between two bodies.

Examples:

  • Gravitational Force
  • Electrostatic Force
  • Magnetic Force
Impulsive Force

Definition: An impulsive force is a large force acting on a body for a very short duration, producing a sudden change in momentum.

Examples:

  • Hitting a ball with a bat
  • A hammer striking a nail
  • Collision between two vehicles
Linear Momentum and Conservation Law

Definition: The linear momentum of a body is the product of its mass and velocity.

\[ p = mv \]

Where:

  • \( p \) = Linear momentum
  • \( m \) = Mass of the body
  • \( v \) = Velocity of the body
Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum

Statement: The total linear momentum of an isolated system remains constant if no external force acts on it.

\[ m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 \]

Example: Recoil of a gun â€“ when a bullet is fired, the gun moves backward to conserve momentum.

Impulse

Definition: Impulse is the change in momentum of a body due to an applied force over a short time.

\[ J = F \cdot \Delta t = \Delta p \]

Where:

  • \( J \) = Impulse
  • \( F \) = Force applied
  • \( \Delta t \) = Time duration of force applied
  • \( \Delta p \) = Change in momentum

Example: A cricket player lowers their hands while catching a ball to reduce impact force by increasing time.

Newton's Laws of Motion
First Law (Law of Inertia)

Statement: A body remains at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force.

Example: A passenger jerks forward when a moving bus stops suddenly.

Second Law (Law of Acceleration)

Statement: The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of force.

\[ F = ma \]

Example: A greater force is needed to accelerate a heavier object than a lighter one.

Third Law (Action-Reaction)

Statement: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Example: A rocket moves upward as it expels gases downward.

Thrust on a Rocket

Formula:

\[ T = \frac{d(mv)}{dt} = v_e \frac{dm}{dt} \]

Where:

  • \( T \) = Thrust force
  • \( v_e \) = Exhaust velocity of gases
  • \( \frac{dm}{dt} \) = Rate of change of mass (mass of gases expelled per second)

Example: A spacecraft propels forward by ejecting fuel gases backward.

Equilibrium of a Particle & Lami's Theorem

Equilibrium Condition: A particle is in equilibrium if the net force acting on it is zero.

\[ \sum \vec{F} = 0 \]

Example: A book resting on a table is in equilibrium as the normal force balances gravity.

Lami's Theorem

Statement: If a particle is in equilibrium under three concurrent forces, then:

\[ \frac{A}{\sin \alpha} = \frac{B}{\sin \beta} = \frac{C}{\sin \gamma} \]

Where:

  • \( A, B, C \) = Magnitudes of three forces
  • \( \alpha, \beta, \gamma \) = Angles opposite to respective forces

Example: Forces acting on a suspended lamp connected by two strings satisfy Lami's theorem.

Common Forces in Mechanics
Weight

Definition: The gravitational force acting on a body due to Earth's gravity.

\[ W = mg \]

Where:

  • \( W \) = Weight
  • \( m \) = Mass of the body
  • \( g \) = Acceleration due to gravity
Normal Reaction

Definition: The force exerted by a surface perpendicular to the contact surface.

For a body on a horizontal surface:

\[ N = mg \]

For an inclined plane:

\[ N = mg \cos \theta \]

Tension

Definition: The force exerted by a string or rope when pulled.

For a hanging mass:

\[ T = mg \]

Apparent Weight in a Lift

Definition: The normal reaction experienced by a person in an accelerating or decelerating lift.

Lift Moving Upward with Acceleration (\( a \))

\[ N = m(g + a) \]

Lift Moving Downward with Acceleration (\( a \))

\[ N = m(g - a) \]

Lift in Free Fall (\( a = g \))

Condition: When the lift falls freely under gravity (\( a = g \)), apparent weight becomes zero.

\[ N = 0 \]

Example: Astronauts in space experience weightlessness due to free fall conditions.

Friction

Definition: The resistive force acting between two surfaces in contact.

Types of Friction
  • Static Friction (\( f_s \)): The friction force before motion starts.
  • Kinetic Friction (\( f_k \)): The friction force when the object is sliding.
  • Rolling Friction (\( f_r \)): The friction force when an object rolls over a surface.
Important Formulae

Static Friction:

\[ f_s \leq \mu_s N \]

Kinetic Friction:

\[ f_k = \mu_k N \]

Rolling Friction:

\[ f_r = \mu_r N \]

Where:

  • \( \mu_s \), \( \mu_k \), \( \mu_r \) = Coefficients of static, kinetic, and rolling friction
  • \( N \) = Normal reaction force
Motion on a Rough Inclined Plane

Definition: Motion of a body on an inclined plane where friction is present.

Forces Acting on a Body
  • Weight: \( mg \) (Acts downward)
  • Normal Reaction: \( N = mg \cos\theta \)
  • Component of Weight Along the Plane: \( mg \sin\theta \)
  • Friction Force: \( f = \mu N = \mu mg \cos\theta \)
Case 1: Object Sliding Down the Plane

Equation of Motion:

\[ a = g (\sin\theta - \mu \cos\theta) \]

Velocity After Distance \( s \):

\[ v^2 = u^2 + 2 a s \]

Time to Reach Bottom:

\[ t = \sqrt{\frac{2s}{g (\sin\theta - \mu \cos\theta)}} \]

Case 2: Object Moving Up the Plane

Equation of Motion:

\[ a = g (\sin\theta + \mu \cos\theta) \]

Maximum Height Reached:

\[ s = \frac{u^2}{2 g (\sin\theta + \mu \cos\theta)} \]

Time to Reach Maximum Height:

\[ t = \frac{u}{g (\sin\theta + \mu \cos\theta)} \]

Condition for No Motion (Limiting Friction)

The object will not slide if:

\[ \tan\theta \leq \mu \]
